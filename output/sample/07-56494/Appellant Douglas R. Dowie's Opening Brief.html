*1 I.
Statement of Jurisdiction
The district court had jurisdiction based upon diversity of citizenship of the parties. Plaintiff is a resident of the State of California. Defendant Fleishman- Hillard is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in St. Louis, Missouri. Omnicom Group is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of. business in New York, New York. 28 U.S.C. §1332.
The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit has jurisdiction because this is an appeal from a judgment entered upon an order granting defendants' Motions to Dismiss pursuant to Rule 12(b)(6) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. [Excerpt of Record (hereafter “E.R.”) 4, 7.] The judgment disposed of all claims of all parties. 28 U.S.C. §1291.
The judgment appealed from was entered on August 28, 2007. [E.R. 4.] The Notice of Appeal was filed on September 18, 2007. [E.R. 1.]
II.
Statement of Issues and Standard of Review
Plaintiffs Complaint pleaded six causes of action arising under California law, all of which were dismissed by the district court upon defendants' Motions to Dismiss pursuant to Rule 12(b)(6) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
*2 The issue in this case is whether plaintiffs Complaint alleged sufficient facts which, assuming them to be true, state causes of action upon which relief may be granted.
Defendants' motions were ruled upon by the district court without oral argument. [E.R. 7.] The district court's order is reviewed de novo. National Wildlife Federation v. Espy, 45 F.3d 1337, 1340 (9th Cir. 1995).
III.
Statement of Facts
This appeal arises out of the dismissal of plaintiffs Complaint pursuant to defendants' Motions to Dismiss under Rule 12(b)(6) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
In sum, the Complaint alleges that in 2004, Fleishman-Hillard and plaintiff, who was Senior Vice President, Senior Partner and General Manager of Fleishman-Hillard's Los Angeles office, became the subjects of a wide ranging criminal investigation by local and federal prosecutors. [E.R.19-20.] They were also defendants in a civil suit filed by the City of Los Angeles. Both the civil and criminal matters stemmed from allegations that Fleishman-Hillard had submitted inflated bills in connection with its contract with the City of Los Angeles' Department of Water and Power. [E.R. 20-22.] Fleishman-Hillard is a wholly *3 owned subsidiary of defendant Omnicom Group. [E.R. 19.]
At the outset, Fleishman-Hillard directed plaintiff to hire his own attorneys to represent him in connection with all matters arising from the criminal probes and the civil litigation, It specifically recommended the law firm Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher, and made an unqualified promise to plaintiff that it would pay all the firm's bills. [E.R. 20.] In reliance on that promise, plaintiff hired Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher. [E.R. 21.]
When Fleishman-Hillard made that promise to plaintiff, however, it was concerned about its own possible indictment, and to forestall that event it was actively attempting to meet the terms of the January 30, 2003 Justice Department mandate entitled Principles of Federal Prosecution of Business Organizations, the so-called “Thompson Memo.” [E.R. 20.] This was a set of prosecutorial guidelines which were then binding on all United States Attorneys pursuant to the §163 of the U.S. Department of Justice Criminal Resource Manual. Those guidelines included cooperation with the government, waiver of the attorney client privilege, and whether the corporate employer was paying attorneys fees for the target employees. Fleishman-Hillard did not disclose that to plaintiff, however, nor did it disclose that if he was indicted then it would cease paying his attorneys fees. [E.R. 20, 25.] A copy of the Thompson Memo is attached for the *4 convenience of the court.
In June, 2005, plaintiff was indicted by a federal grand jury for actions undertaken in the service of, and on behalf of, Fleishman-Hillard. [E.R. 22.] In an effort to comply with the Thompson Memo, Fleishman-Hillard stopped paying plaintiffs attorneys fees almost immediately upon his indictment.1 By then, he had been fired and was impecunious. Because Gibson, Dunn had represented him throughout his ordeal, however, and was thus intimately familiar with all of the complexities of the matter, and in view of the fact that he was now facing a federal prosecution, plaintiff effectively had no choice but to continue with Gibson, Dunn's representation. [E.R. 22.] He was unable to pay them, however, and he was ultimately convicted of conspiracy and wire fraud, a conviction which is currently on appeal in this court. [E.R. pg. 15 and Statement of Related Case.]
Plaintiff is indebted to Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher for more than three million dollars. [E.R. 23.] In this case, plaintiff seeks indemnity for his post-indictment attorneys fees under California Labor Code §2802, and under common law *5 theories of indenmity, estoppel, and fraud.2 Additionally, plaintiff contends that he is entitled to indemnity pursuant to an unusual provision in the Omnicom Group corporate bylaws.
IV.
Summary of Argument
California Labor Code §2802 requires California employers to indemnify their employees for all loss and expense, including attorneys fees, for actions undertaken in the course and scope of employment. “Course and scope” is broadly defined, and can include criminal activity. The only exception is if that activity was undertaken at the direction of the employer, and the “employee at the time of obeying the directions, believed them to be unlawful.” Plaintiff contends that, standing alone, his criminal conviction does not establish his contemporary subjective belief that his actions were unlawful so as to defeat his Labor Code §2802 claim.
Plaintiff further contends that an employer violates public policy when it relies on the Thompson Memo to repudiate its statutory obligations under Labor Code §2802. A subsequent conviction, facilitated by the employer's deprivation *6 of fees, cannot retroactively justify such a repudiation.
Plaintiff further contends that the acts for which he was indicted, tried, and convicted, all undertaken for the benefit of Fleishman-Hillard, entitle him to indemnity as a matter of equity, and that Fleishman-Hillard is estopped to deny plaintiff the benefit of its promise to pay his fees.
Additionally, Fleishman-Hillard's promise to pay all of plaintiff's attorneys fees was made when it was actively maneuvering to comply with the mandate of the Thompson Memo. Fleishman-Hillard had already decided that it would cut off his attorneys fees if he was indicted, but it did not disclose that information to him. Those facts would have materially affected plaintiffs decision to retain Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher in the first place, but by the time of his indictment, plaintiff was effectively yoked to the firm and had no realistic alternative but to continue with them. Defendant's failure to disclose those facts was fraud.
Finally, the Omnicom by-laws constitute a written contract pursuant to which plaintiff is entitled to indemnity for his attorneys fees. The plain language of the by-laws include plaintiff in the class of individuals entitled to their protection, notwithstanding that he was not a direct employee of Omnicom Group, and despite his subsequent conviction.
*7 V.
Argument
A. Plaintiff Pled Sufficient Facts For Labor Code §2802 Indemnity
Plaintiff was a long time senior executive with defendant Fleishman-Hillard. In June, 2005, he was indicted for his alleged involvement helping Fleishman-Hillard submit inflated bills, principally to the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power. Plaintiffs alleged crimes were indisputably undertaken in the course and scope of his employment, and the Complaint specifically alleges that all of plaintiffs actions were “undertaken on behalf of, with the knowledge of, and with the authorization of defendant Fleishman-Hillard.” [E.R. 21-23.]
California Labor Code §2802 reads as follows:
“An employer shall indemnify his or her employee for all necessary expenditures or losses incurred by the employee in direct consequence of the discharge of his or her duties, or of his or her obedience to the directions of the employer, even though unlawful, unless the employee, at the time of obeying the directions, believed them to be unlawful.” [Emphasis supplied.]
Pursuant to California Labor Code §2802, Fleishman-Hillard did, in fact, *8 pay his fees until he was indicted. Then, in an effort to comply with the Thompson Memo and avoid its own indictment, it suddenly discontinued indemnifying him for the costs of his defense. The trial court ruled that plaintiffs Labor Code §2802 claim was foreclosed as a matter of law by virtue of his subsequent conviction. [E.R. 8-9.]
The court was in error for two reasons. First, it misconstrued the plain language of the statute. More importantly, however, its ruling would tend to encourage employers to deprive their employees of counsel in criminal proceedings, in violation of the Fifth and Sixth Amendments.
Section 2802 was enacted for the protection of employees: “Rather, we decide this case strictly under section 2802, the obvious purpose of which is to protect employees from suffering expenses in direct consequence of doing their jobs.” Grissom v. Vons Companies, 1 Cal.App.4th 52, 60 (1991). It represents fundamental public policy in California, and its broad scope encompasses attorneys fees and costs incurred by an employee sued for conduct arising in the course and scope of his employment. See, Grissom, supra., and Douglas v. Los Angeles Herald-Examiner, 50 Cal.App.3d 449 (1975).
In this case, however, the trial court has effectively held that an employee facing criminal prosecution for actions in the service of his employer is entitled to *9 less protection under §2802 than one facing an ordinary civil lawsuit. That makes no sense.
According to the lower court in this case, an employee facing criminal liability arising out of the performance of his duties has no §2802 protection for attorneys fees until after the trial is over. Then, if he is convicted, the employer can wash its hands of him altogether, notwithstanding that the conviction itself might have been procured with the passive, or even active assistance, of the employer seeking to avoid the financial liability of 2802. Such a perverse outcome undermines the purpose of the statute.
First, depriving an employee of the means to defend himself will typically facilitate a conviction, and it is contrary to the manifest intent of the statute for the courts to give employers an economic incentive to do so. In those instances where the alleged crime was, as in this case, not only clearly within the course and scope of the employment, but actually for the benefit of the employer, the employer's §2802 obligations should not depend, even in small part, on whether it can assist the prosecution in securing a conviction. It is at the outset of criminal proceedings when the employee is most in need of his statutory protections. Otherwise, they are meaningless.
Moreover, it is doubly perverse to enhance the employer's economic *10 incentive to see the employee convicted with the prospect of its own indictment if it does pay fees. Such a result not only subverts the purpose of §2802, but it opens to doubt the very integrity of the criminal proceedings to which the employee is being subjected.
Plaintiff respectfully directs the court's attention to United States v. Stein, 435 F.Supp.2d 330 (S.D.N.Y., 2006), in which the Southern District of New York, in a case of first impression, held that Labor Code §2802 protections apply to criminal proceedings. There, the court observed how effectively the Thompson Memo can be used to bludgeon a corporation to abandon a loyal employee, like plaintiff in this matter, thereby at once causing irreparable harm to the employee as well as corrupting the integrity of the judicial process. The court held that the statute applied unless both of the following conditions were found: that the actions were illegal and the employees believed them to be illegal at the time they performed them:
“Defendants Bickham and Larson were California employees, not partners. To the extent the investigation and indictment arose in consequence of that employment, California statutes require KPMG to advance their defense costs and, unless their actions were *11 both unlawful and ‘believed by them to be unlawful’ at the time, to indemnify them.” 435 F.Supp.2d at 356, fn. 119.
Here, of course, plaintiff alleged that he was unaware of the illegality of the actions he took on behalf of his employer. The trial court observed, and plaintiff naturally concedes, that he was convicted of specific intent crimes, but that simply establishes at most that he intended to inflate the billings. It does not establish that he knew he was committing the crimes of wire fraud and conspiracy.
More to the point, however, by allowing defendant to deny plaintiff the protections of Labor Code §2802, the court below sharply tilted the landscape in favor of the government. While it is true that Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher provided a defense, and undoubtedly a very good one, it should not be necessary to speculate on the defense that the firm would have provided had it been compensated, and assured of compensation, throughout the lengthy criminal proceedings. The Stein court addressed this issue as follows:
“Yet the government has interfered with the ability of the KPMG Defendants to obtain resources they otherwise would have had. Unless remedied, this interference almost certainly will affect what these defendants can *12 afford to permit their counsel to do. This would impact the defendants ability to present the defense they wish to present by limiting the means lawfully available to them. 435 F.Supp.2d at 362
“In these circumstances, demonstrating prejudice after the fact would be all but impossible. In order to show that the trial outcome would have been different had a convicted defendant been able to afford better preparation before trial, the defendant's counsel, after conviction, would have to do the work that the defendant could not afford to have done in the first place. If the defendant could not afford to have the work done in the first place, the defendant certainly could not afford to have it done after conviction. And relying upon the possibility of counsel appointed under the Criminal Justice Act to do so, should a convicted defendant have become indigent, simply would be unrealistic. In any case, assessing the impact of pretrial omissions and *13 errors could require extensive evidentiary proceedings. In consequence, it is difficult to imagine circumstances in which an error more properly could be said to threaten to taint an entire proceeding.” 435 F.Supp.2d at 371-372.
In this case, the court below simply asserted, with almost no analysis, that plaintiff's conviction foreclosed his §2802 rights. An implicit premise of that conclusion is that plaintiff was afforded the same defense he would have received had his attorneys been paid. It is more likely that the conviction was eased by decisions made by unpaid counsel running ever more deeply in the red.
Regardless of the Thompson Memo, the trial court's reliance on the mere fact of plaintiff's conviction to preclude his §2802 claim ignores the plain language of the statute.
The statute mandates indemnity if an employee incurs expenditures or loss in consequence of “obedience to the directions of the employer, even though unlawful, unless the employee, at the time of obeying the directions, believed them to be unlawful.” This language clearly contemplates that employers may be compelled to indemnify employees who engage in criminal activity. The only exception in the statute is narrowly drawn: if the employee commits crimes at the *14 direction of the employer and subjectively believed the directions to have been “unlawful” at the time he followed them. Here, and notwithstanding that Mr. Dowie was convicted of wire fraud, he was not found to have subjectively believed he was following “unlawful directions of the employer.”
The Legislature easily could have written §2802 to exclude indemnification for criminal acts. Or, it could have excluded indemnification upon conviction of felonies, or any crimes. Indeed, it could have excluded whatever it wanted to exclude, under whatever circumstances it deemed appropriate. It did not do so, however, and the court below should not have undertaken that legislative task. That is especially so given that the test for coverage under §2802 is so well settled.
In Jacobus v. Krambo Corp., 78 Cal.App.4th 1096 (2000), the court held:
“The test for recovery under section 2802 is whether the conduct defended against was within the course and scope of employment. [Citation omitted.] In determining whether for purposes of indemnification an employee's acts were performed within the course and scope of employment, the courts have looked to the doctrine of respondeat superior. [Citations omitted.] Under that doctrine, an employer is liable for risks *15 broadly incidental to the enterprise undertaken by the employer is, for an employee's conduct that, in the context of the employer's enterprise, is ‘not so unusual or startling that it would seem unfair to include the loss resulting from it among other costs of the employer's business.’ [Citations omitted.]” 78 Cal.App.4th at 1101.
Here, even accepting the factual bases of plaintiff's conviction at face value, they simply establish over billing by an enterprise which profits by hourly billing. Over billing is, of course, a common problem associated with businesses which engage in hourly billing, and Fleishman-Hillard has already admitted its respondeat superior liability for the alleged over billing by its $5.7 million settlement of Los Angeles' civil lawsuit against it and Mr. Dowie. [E.R. 22.]
Furthermore, it is not dispositive of the respondeat superior issue that plaintiff was convicted of criminal fraud. See Inter Mountain Mortg., Inc. v. Sulimen, 78 Cal.App.4th 1434 (2000) in which the court held:
“And ‘an employee's willful, malicious and even criminal torts may fall within the scope of his or her employment for purposes of respondeat superior, even though the employer has not authorized the employee to commit *16 crimes or intentional torts. ’ (Lisa M., supra, at pp. 296- 297, 48 Cal.Rptr.2d 510, 907 P.2d 358.) The employer is liable not because the employer has control over the employee or is in some way at fault, but because the employer's enterprise creates inevitable risks as a part of doing business. [Citations.] Under this theory, an employer is liable for ‘the risks inherent in or created by the enterprise.’'' (Bailey, supra, at p. 1559, 56 Cal.Rptr.2d 333.) 78 Cal.App.4th at 1440.
The Ninth Circuit has also held that a criminal fraud by an employee can result in respondeat superior liability for the innocent employer if, as alleged in this case, the scheme was undertaken for the benefit of the employer. See, e.g., Brady v. Dairy Fresh Products Co., 974 F.2d 1149 (9th Cir. 1992):
“We hold that an employer that is benefitted by its employee or agent's violations of section 1962(c) may be held liable under the doctrines of respondeat superior and agency when the employer is distinct from the enterprise. Corporations and other employers that have benefitted from their employees or agents' RICO *17 violations will be forced to compensate the victims of racketeering activity. Respondeat superior and agency liability will encourage employers to monitor more closely the activities of their employees and agents to ensure that these agents are not involved in racketeering activities. Thus, respondeat superior and agency liability furthers both the compensatory and deterrent goals of the RICO statute.” 974 F.2d at 1154-1155.
Plaintiff has adequately pled the elements of a Labor Code §2802 claim, and they are not negated by plaintiffs conviction. Unless and until there is a specific factual finding of plaintiff's subjective belief in the criminality of his conduct, his Labor Code §2802 claim should not be dismissed.
B. The Trial Court's Dismissals of the Fraud and Promissory Estoppel Causes of Action Were Error
The trial court dismissed the Fraud cause of action and the Promissory Estoppel cause of action for essentially the same reason, namely that plaintiff failed to allege damages as a consequence of relying on the unqualified promise to pay all of his attorneys fees. The court was wrong.
Plaintiff specifically alleged that Mr. Fred Rohfing, defendant's Chief *18 Financial Officer and the person in charge of managing the legal proceedings against it, knew there was a possibility, if not probability, that plaintiff would be indicted, and that due regard for the Thompson Memo would cause him to cease paying his attorneys fees. [E.R. 20, 25.] When he directed plaintiff to Gibson, Dunn, however, he never disclosed that his promise to pay their fees was qualified, and that he would rescind it if plaintiff was indicted. Instead, he made an unqualfied promise to pay all of the fees reasonably incurred by plaintiff and billed by Gibson, Dunn. [E.R. 20, 21.]
The promise, as alleged in the Complaint, was to pay all of his legal fees, period. It was defendant's silence, its failure to tell him that it would cut off his fees, that constitutes fraudulent concealment under Civil Code § 1710(3). It was a misrepresentation for Fleishman-Hillard not to state that it would cut him off if he was indicted.
The elements of fraud are (a) misrepresentation (like concealment or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of the misrepresentation; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and, (e) resulting damage. See, e.g., Seeger v. Odell, 18 Cal.2d 409 (1941); Harazim v. Lynam, 267 Cal.App.2d 127 (1968). Here, it seems that the trial court did not take issue with the question of fraudulent misrepresentation or justifiable reliance, but simply found that plaintiff benefitted *19 from defendant's largesse. According to the lower court, “Dowie unconvincingly argues that he was damaged by being forced to pay for lawyers he could not afford merely because they had become familiar with the case.” [E.R. 9.] The court went on to say, a little cavalierly, that upon his indictment plaintiff could have used a public defender, or gotten some other, cheaper counsel. [E.R. 9.]
Likewise with respect to promissory estoppel, the trial court correctly set forth the elements of the cause of action citing to Toscano v. Greene Music, 124 Cal.App.4th 685 (2004): “The elements of promissory estoppel are (1) a clear promise, (2) reliance, (3) substantial detriment, and (4) damages measured by the extent of the obligation assumed and not performed.” [E.R.] As with the fraud cause of action, the court did not take issue with the questions of a false promise or reliance, but simply held that plaintiff “fails to allege a substantial detriment caused by his reliance on F-H's alleged promise.” The court erred in both instances.
It is true, of course, that plaintiff did benefit from his pre-indictment representation by Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher, which was paid for by defendant. But, that benefit does not negate the subsequent detriment of the Hobbesian choice caused by his earlier reliance on defendant's unqualified promise that it would pay all of his fees, including post-indictment fees. The trial court's ruling suggests *20 that the earlier benefit conferred on plaintiff somehow offsets the detriment caused when defendant later broke its promise. There is no factual or legal basis for that position.
The Complaint alleges at paragraphs 39 and 40:
“Plaintiff would not have retained said law firm had he known that defendant would stop all payments upon his indictment, at a time when he most needed Gibson, Dunn's services and was most vulnerable. As a consequence of defendant's actions, and the timing of those actions, plaintiff had no practical alternative but to continue being represented by Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher and incur the staggering debt alleged herein. “As a proximate result of plaintiffs reliance on the promises of defendant, plaintiff has been damaged in an amount which will be set forth at time of trial, but which is presently in the approximate amount of $3 million.” [E.R. 27.]
One might reasonably disagree with the allegations, but accepting them as true requires the conclusion that the damage elements of fraud and promissory *21 estoppel have been set forth.
The full weight of the government was behind the criminal proceedings against plaintiff. They were extremely complex, and by the time of his indictment, a full year of investigation and legal wrangling had passed. The stakes, always significant, became considerably more so upon his indictment. By then, it simply was not reasonable for plaintiff to try and get some cheap new counsel as long as Gibson, Dunn attorneys were willing to stick with him. Plaintiffs Complaint adequately set out all of the elements of fraud, deceit and promissory estoppel. The court erred in dismissing those causes of action.
C. Equitable Indemnification Was Well Pleaded; It Was Error To Dismiss That Cause Of Action On A 12(b)(6) Motion
Implied contractual indemnity is a form of equitable indemnity which “may arise from the equities of particular circumstances.” McCrary Construction Co., v. Metal Deck Specialists, Inc., 133 Cal.App. 4th 1528, 1536 (2005). In his Complaint, plaintiff alleged,
“that at all times relevant, defendant Fleishman-Hillard was not only complicit with plaintiff in the actions for which he was indicted, but reaped the benefits of those actions, all of which were undertaken on behalf of, with the knowledge of, and with the authorization of *22 defendant Fleishman-Hillard.” [E.R. 23.]
The trial court, however, essentially disregarded this allegation, suggesting that defendant was itself a victim of plaintiff's criminal conduct. [E.R. 9. Plaintiff concedes that a jury might find that to be the case, but that is hardly the standard for considering a Rule 12(b)(6) motion. The facts alleged support indemnity for all defense costs associated with his defense.
Moreover, to the extent that plaintiff's fees were cut off by defendant as part of a deal in which defendant actively assisted the government in its prosecution of plaintiff, then the equitable considerations are even stronger.
D. Omnicom's Bylaws Covered Plaintiff; The Complaint Properly Alleged Breach of Written Contract
Plaintiff alleged that he was covered by the indemnification provision found at Article IX of Omnicom's corporate by-laws. Omnicom, the corporate parent of defendant Fleishman-Hillard, asked plaintiff to continue to serve the corporation, and as an inducement to do so, it awarded him stock options for Omnicom Group stock. [E.R. 26.] Thus, by the plain wording of the by-laws, plaintiff was in that class of individuals who were entitled to protection under Article IX.
The court below simply misread the by-laws when it held: “By its plain language, Section 1 of Article IX only applies to officers and directors of *23 Omnicom.” [E.R. 11,12.] In fact, the language is much broader than that, covering “any person” who was either a “director or officer of the Corporation, or served such other corporation ...at the request of the Corporation in any capacity,...” [E.R. 30.] In other words, the broad language was clearly intended to cover all directors and officers of Omnicom, of course, but also “any person” who served “in any capacity” one of Omnicom Group's other corporations at Omnicom's request. Plaintiff alleged just that, i.e. that he served Fleishman-Hillard, an Omnicom Group company, at the request of Omnicom. He was covered by the language of the by-laws.
The trial court also ruled, however, that the circumstances of plaintiff's conviction would preclude coverage in any event. The court reasoned that plaintiffs conviction of a crime requiring proof that he “personally possessed the specific intent to defraud” removed him the scope of the by-laws. [E.R. 12.] Plaintiff concedes that his conviction would ordinarily be sufficient to support a finding that he committed acts which would fall within the exception in Article IX, Section 1(a), but not in this case.
As the trial court noted, under the terms of the by-laws plaintiffs conviction does not create a presumption that his conduct fell within the scope of the indemnity exception. Nevertheless, and without any analysis, the court not only *24 indulged in a presumption that plaintiff believed his conduct to be unlawful, but it went on to so find as a matter of law. That was error.
Section 1(b) of the by-laws makes sense only if Omnicom intended persons who would otherwise be entitled to indemnity, but for a criminal conviction, to be able to re-litigate in a civil proceeding some elements of their criminal convictions. Furthermore, it also only makes sense if read such that the company has the burden to prove that plaintiff did, in fact, act “with deliberate dishonesty” or “were committed in bad faith.” That did not happen here. Rather, the court simply paid lip service to the “does not create a presumption” language, and then completely disregarded it.
Plaintiff is in the class of persons entitled to indemnity under the Omnicom by-laws, and he should be given the opportunity to litigate his claim. If Omnicom wishes to argue that his conviction deprives him of any right to indemnity, then it should have the burden of proving that an exception applies, and plaintiffs conviction, though probative, is nothing more than one piece of evidence which it can introduce.
VI.
Conclusion
The issue on this appeal is simply whether plaintiff has adequately pleaded the *25 requisite elements of the enumerated causes of action such that, assuming the truth of the allegations, he has stated claims upon which relief may be granted. Plaintiff submits that he has done so.
For these reasons, it is respectfully submitted that the court should reverse the judgment of dismissal and remand the matter to the trial court with an order that defendants answer the Complaint and that the matter proceed.
