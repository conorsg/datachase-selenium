I. INTRODUCTION
Plaintiff Douglas Dowie appeals the District Court's dismissal of his complaint against his ex-employer Fleishman-Hillard Inc. (“FH”), and Omnicom Group Inc. (“Omnicom”), FH's parent corporation. Mr. Dowie (the former General Manager of FH's Los Angeles office) sought indemnity for defense fees and costs following his federal criminal conviction for conspiracy and fifteen counts of wire fraud, based on his over billing clients (including the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power) for FH's public relations services. On July 17, 2007, the District Court properly found that as a matter of law Mr. Dowie failed to state a valid claim for relief under any of his asserted grounds -California Labor Code section 2802, implied or equitable indemnity, fraud, promissory estoppel, and Ominicom's bylaws.
The District Court properly determined that each of Mr. Dowie's theories of indemnity suffered from fatal flaws. Additional defects in Mr. Dowie's claims for relief further support the District Court's rulings, as do the corporation laws of California, New York, and Delaware - each of which prohibits a corporation from indemnifying employees for engaging in criminal acts they could not reasonably have believed were legal.
II. STATEMENT OF JURISDICTION (CIRCUIT RULE 28-2.1)
Appellees Fleishman-Hillard Inc. and Omnicom Group Inc. agree with Appellant's Statement of Jurisdiction, except that Omnicom Group Inc. is a New York (not Delaware) Corporation. Appellees' Supplemental Excerpt of Record1 79 (“SER”). However, the error in Appellant's Statement of Jurisdiction has no effect on the District Court's subject matter jurisdiction.
III. STATEMENT OF ISSUES AND STANDARD OF REVIEW
FH and Omnicom agree with Mr. Dowie's statement of the issues presented, and his citation of the de novo standard for review of the District Court's order dismissing his Complaint.
Given the de novo standard of review, the rules for deciding a motion to dismiss under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) apply. The truth of plaintiff s factual allegations is assumed by the Court. See Gen. Conference Corp. of Seventh-Day Adventists v. Seventh-Day Adventist Congregational Church, 887 F.2d 228, 230 (9th Cir. 1989); Hal Roach Studios, Inc. v. Richard Feiner & Co., 896 F.2d 1542, 1550 (9th Cir. 1990). However, the Court is not required to accept as true “conclusory allegations of law,” Epstein v. Wash. Energy Co., 83 F.3d 1136, 1140 (9th Cir. 1996), “allegations that contradict matters properly subject to judicial notice or by exhibit,” or “allegations that are merely conclusory, unwarranted deductions of fact, or unreasonable inferences,” Sprewell v. Golden State Warriors, 266 F.3d 979, 988 (9th Cir. 2001). See also Bradley v. Chiron Corp., 136 F.3d 1317, 1324 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (stating that “the district court was not required to accept representations that the court viewed, we think correctly, as ‘nonsensical”’); Kadar Corp. v. Milbury, 549 F.2d 230, 233 (1st Cir. 1977) (“courts do ‘not accept conclusory allegations on the legal effect of the events plaintiff has set out if these allegations do not reasonably follow from his description of what happened ...”) (citation omitted).
Pursuant to Federal Rule of Evidence 201, on a motion to dismiss, the Court may take judicial notice of court records and proceedings. See Lee v. City of L.A., 250 F.3d 668, 688-89 (9th Cir. 2001). It also may consider documents whose contents are alleged in the complaint but which are not physically attached. See Knievel v. ESPN, 393 F. 3d 1068, 1076 (9th Cir. 2005). The Court may also consider documents whose authenticity is not contested and which are “crucial to plaintiffs claims, but not explicitly incorporated in his complaint.” Id. at 706. That rule prevents “plaintiffs from surviving a Rule 12(b)(6) motion by deliberately omitting references to documents upon which their claims are based.” Id.
This Court can affirm the District Court's ruling on any valid ground supported by the record, whether or not presented to or relied upon by the District Court. Atel Financial Corp. v. Quaker Coal Co., 321 F.3d 924, 926 (9th Cir. 2003); Orsay v. U.S. Dept. of Justice, 289 F.3d 1125, 1132 (9th Cir. 2002).
IV. STATEMENT OF THE CASE AND FACTS
This appeal arises from Mr. Dowie's third lawsuit against FH. The third lawsuit, like the previous two, arose from facts relating to Mr. Dowie's employment and subsequent termination from FH, and his federal criminal prosecution, and subsequent conviction, for conspiracy and wire fraud based upon his fraudulently billing of clients for FH's public relations services.
On January 5, 2005, Mr. Dowie's employment with FH was terminated. See SER, 20.2 Shortly thereafter, Mr. Dowie, through his counsel, threatened to embarrass FH if he did not receive a substantial severance payment. See U.S. v. Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196 at *2 (C.D. Cal. Oct. 4, 2006) (copy attached). In March 2005, Mr. Dowie filed his first action against FH in Los Angeles Superior Court alleging claims for breach of implied contract to terminate only for good cause and promissory estoppel.3 See SER, 69. In April 2005, FH removed the action to the District Court and ultimately, FH's motion for summary judgment was granted and judgment was entered in FH's favor. See SER, 8.; Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196 at *2, n. 3. Mr. Dowie did not appeal the judgment.
On June 2, 2005, while Mr. Dowie's wrongful termination action was pending, he was indicted for conspiracy and wire fraud arising from his role in over billing clients for FH's public relations services. See Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196, SER, 8.
In October 2005, while Mr. Dowie's criminal action was pending, he filed his second action against FH in Los Angeles Superior Court. See SER, 87. That lawsuit brought claims for violation of Labor Code section 2802 and declaratory relief based upon Mr. Dowie's claim that FH was obligated to advance Mr. Dowie's attorneys fees for his criminal defense. See id. FH removed the action to the District Court and it was assigned to the Honorable Gary A. Feess, the district court judge to whom Mr. Dowie's criminal case was assigned. See SER, 59, 65. Thereafter, FH moved to dismiss the. action for failure to state a claim, and its motion was granted on January 11, 2006. See SER, 59. The District Court concluded that unless, and until, Mr. Dowie prevailed against the charges of conspiracy and wire fraud, his claim to indemnity under section 2802 was premature. See SER, 61-62. The District Court also explained that if Mr. Dowie were convicted, “[i]ssue preclusion would bar relitigating this issue of Dowie's belief in the lawfulness of his actions since that issue would necessarily have been resolved in the criminal case.” SER, 62. Mr. Dowie did not appeal the dismissal.
In May 2006, Mr. Dowie was convicted of conspiracy and wire fraud. See SER, 64; Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196: After his conviction, but before he was sentenced, Mr. Dowie filed a Motion to Dismiss the Indictment based upon U.S. v. Stein, 435 F. Supp. 2d 330, 356 (S.D.N.Y. 2006), aff'd --- F. 3d ---, 2008 WL 3982104 (2d Cir. Aug. 28, 2008), and his argument that FH chose to terminate payment of Mr. Dowie's attorneys' fees after his indictment in supposed deference to the dictates of the Thompson Memorandum. See Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196 at *1. On October 4, 2006, Judge Feess denied the motion. See Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196. On January 30, 2007, Mr. Dowie was sentenced. See SER, 64.4
In February 2007, notwithstanding his conviction for conspiracy and wire fraud5 he proceeded to file his third action, which is the subject of this appeal. See ER, 18. This action, like his second action, again alleged a claim against FH for violation of Labor Code section 2802, and also alleged claims against FH for implied indemnity, fraud, breach of oral contract and promissory estoppel. See ER, 18. Mr. Dowie's complaint also alleged a claim for breach of written contract against Omnicom Group Inc. (“Omnicom”), FH's parent corporation. See ER, 26.
FH and Omnicom promptly removed the action from Los Angeles Superior Court to the District Court and filed motions to dismiss the action. The motions were granted,6 and Mr. Dowie was granted leave to amend his complaint as to certain claims. See ER, 12. However, Mr. Dowie elected not to file an amended complaint and judgment was entered in favor of FH and Omnicom. See SER, 1-2; ER 4-5. Mr. Dowie then filed this appeal. See ER 1.7
V. SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT
None of Mr. Dowie's causes of action, stated a valid claim for relief.
1. Labor Code section 2802 (First Cause of Action, against FH) does require employers to indemnify employees for legal defense expenses in certain circumstances, but explicitly excludes cases - like this one - in which the employee engaged in unlawful acts he believed were unlawful. Mr. Dowie does not dispute Labor Code section 2802 contains that exclusion. The District Court correctly concluded that Mr. Dowie's.convictions disqualified him from indemnity under the exclusion. Conspiracy and wire fraud (18 U.S.C. sections 317 and 1343, respectively) required proof of an agreement to commit a crimine and participation in a scheme to defraud. See §VI.
Mr. Dowie's stubborn denial that his conviction reflected knowing criminal conduct does not undermine the District Court's decision. Mr. Dowie's interpretation of Labor Code section 2802 would vitiate the exclusion for certain unlawful conduct and conflict with a California public policy against indemnifying intentional wrongs. (The law of Delaware, where FH was incorporated, is the same.)
Mr. Dowie's dire prediction that condoning corporate refusals to advance funds to criminal defendants will tip the scales against defendants is irrelevant: section 2802 does not address (much less require) the advancement of funds.8 In any event, Mr. Dowie admits (and the District Court specifically agreed) that he had excellent criminal defense counsel despite FH's refusal to advance fees, so his public policy argument founders factually as well as legally. Finally, Mr. Dowie cannot rely on the doctrine of respondeat superior to circumvent the exclusion of Labor Code section 2802. That doctrine is not at issue here. FH denies Mr. Dowie is entitled to indemnity not because it denied responsibility for Mr. Dowie's actions, but because his actions were criminal, he knew they were criminal, and those actions disqualified him from indemnity under Labor Code section 2802.
2. Equitable indemnity (Second Cause of Action, against FH) is a right that inures to a person who, without active fault, has been compelled by a legal obligation to pay damages caused by the negligence of another. The District Court correctly concluded Mr. Dowie had been found guilty of fraud (a quintessential form of active fault), and the equities did not favor Mr. Dowie (whose criminal conduct exposed FH to substantial civil liability, but whom FH nevertheless provided legal representation before indictment and in a civil suit). In any event, it would not be equitable for Mr. Dowie to be indemnified by FH in circumstances where indemnity is expressly barred by law. See §VII.
3. Fraudulent concealment .(Third Cause of Action, against FH) requires an omission in the face of a duty to disclose, ' scienter, intent to induce reliance, reasonable reliance, and damage. The District Court correctly concluded that Mr. Dowie failed to allege any actual harm, despite his claim that he was “yoked” to his costly defense attorneys (Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher) for his criminal trial, without the ability to pay their fees. In fact, the District Court concluded Mr. Dowie alleged a windfall, not an injury: FH paid for many months of pre-indictment legal services, and if Mr. Dowie found the firm too rich for his blood upon being indicted, he was free to choose less expensive counsel or seek a public defender. Even more fundamental, Mr. Dowie failed to allege that his supposed harm was the result of any reliance on any misrepresentation or concealment by FH. That is, he admits that he incurred his post-indictment defense fees after learning that FH had no intention to pay them. Finally, Mr. Dowie cannot base his fraud claim on a supposed promise to provide indemnity in circumstances where indemnity is barred by law. See §VIII.
4. Contractual indemnity (Fifth Cause of Action, against Omnicom),9 based on the bylaws of Omnicom, a New York corporation, was also unavailable to Mr. Dowie. Those bylaws extended mandatory indemnity only to directors and officers, and disqualified those whose actions “were committed in bad faith or were the result of his or her active and deliberate dishonesty.” New York's corporations law imposes the same disqualification. The District Court correctly concluded that Mr. Dowie (an employee of FH, a subsidiary of Omnicom) was neither an officer nor a director of Omnicom, and that his criminal conviction for wire fraud “unambiguously disqualifies him for indemnification even if he were an officer or director of Omnicom.” Moreover, unless the indemnitee was a director or officer, indemnity ceased when employment ended. Mr. Dowie was fired by FH before his criminal prosecution commenced. See §IX.
5. Promissory estoppel (Sixth Cause of Action, against FH) requires a clear and unambiguous promise, reliance that is both reasonable and foreseeable, an injury resulting from that reliance, and a need to enforce the promise to avoid injustice. However, the District Court correctly concluded that (as was true of his defective fraud claim) Mr. Dowie failed to allege any cognizable injury. To the contrary, Mr. Dowie alleged a substantial benefit, because he admitted he received free legal representation by competent counsel during the criminal investigation and related civil lawsuit. In addition, Mr. Dowie failed to allege: (a) a clear and unambiguous promise to advance funds for his criminal defense; (b) actual and reasonable reliance; and (c) facts showing that justice required enforcement of the purported promise (particularly since California law affirmatively prohibited indemnity in Mr. Dowie's circumstances). See §X.
The District Court properly dismissed Mr. Dowie's Complaint. The Complaint did not and could not state any valid claim for relief, and Mr. Dowie elected not to amend any portion of his Complaint. Mr. Dowie's conspiracy and wire fraud convictions disqualified him as a matter of law from being indemnified for his criminal defense fees, regardless of the pleading labels he attached to his claims. Thus, the judgment below should be affirmed;
VI. MR. DOWIE FAILED TO STATE A CLAIM UNDER LABOR CODE SECTION 2802.
The District Court dismissed with prejudice the First Cause of Action (indemnity under California Labor Code section 2802). The District Court noted Labor Code section 2802 requires indemnity of an employee for “'obedience to the directions of the employer, even though unlawful, unless the employee, at the time of obeying the directions, believed them to be unlawful.”' ER, 8 (emphasis in original). The District Court concluded that “even if Dowie were to prove that he acted under the direction of his employer in submitting false and inflated bills to Los Angeles clients, his criminal conviction defeats any argument that he believed such direction to be lawful because the conviction required the jury to find that Dowie acted with specific intent to defraud the scheme's victims. United States v. Ciccone, 219 F.3d 1078, 1082-84 (9th Cir. 2000); 18 U.S.C. §1343.”
The District Court was correct in dismissing the First Cause of Action. Indeed, it is not even clear that section 2802 extends to the defense (much less unsuccessful defense) of criminal proceedings. (The District Court had noted that fact in dismissing Mr. Dowie's prior suit based on Labor Code section 2802. SER, 60-61, citing L.A. Police Protective League v. City of L.A., 27 Cal. App. 4th 168, 177, 32 Cal. Rptr. 2d 574, 578-579 (1994) and Chin, et al., California Practice Guide: Employment Litigation, 3-A, 3:4 (2005).)10
In any event, Mr. Dowie admits that if he believed his acts were criminal, his claim under Labor Code section 2802 fails. See AOB at 5 (“[s]tanding alone, his criminal conviction does not establish, his contemporary subjective belief . that his actions were unlawful so as to defeat his Labor Code §2802 claim.”); AOB at 17 (“Unless and until there is a specific factual finding of plaintiff's subjective belief in the criminality of his conduct, his Labor Code §2802 claim should not be dismissed.”); AOB, 10 (section 2802 applies “unless both of the following conditions were found: that the actions were illegal and the employees believed them to be illegal at the time they performed them” (citing Stein, 435 F.Supp.2d at 356 [emphasis in original]).
Mr. Dowie was convicted of conspiracy (18 U.S.C. §371) and multiple counts of wire fraud (18 U.S.C. §1343) arising out of his conduct as the general manager of FH's Los Angeles office. See SER, 64; Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196 at *1. Those convictions required proof that he knew his actions were unlawful. See, e.g., U.S. v. Boone, 951 F.2d 1526, 1543 (9th Cir. 1991) (“Conspiracy is established when there is an agreement to accomplish an illegal objective, coupled with one or more overt acts in furtherance of the illegal purpose.”); Ciccone, 219 F.3d at 1083-84 (“[t]o sustain a conviction under the mail and wire fraud statutes, there must be sufficient evidence to show that the defendant willful[ly] participate[d] in a scheme with knowledge of its fraudulent nature and with intent that these illicit objectives be achieved.' [citation omitted].” Thus, the District Court correctly concluded that Mr. Dowie's conspiracy and wire fraud convictions defeat any claim that Mr. Dowie did not believe his actions were unlawful.
Notwithstanding the clear terms of section 2802, and the elements of his convictions, Mr. Dowie raises several intertwined arguments intended to circumvent the exclusion within the statute. When the arguments are untangled, Mr. Dowie's position unravels.
First, and most fundamentally, Mr. Dowie insists that he was entitled to indemnity under Labor Code section 2802 because notwithstanding his wire fraud conviction, “he was not found to have subjectively believed he was following ‘unlawful directions of the employer.”’ AOB at 14 (emphasis in original). See also AOB at 11 (stating that Mr. Dowie “was unaware of the illegality of the actions he took” and that “he was convicted of specific intent crimes, but that simply establishes at most that he intended tó inflate the billings. It does not establish that he knew he was committing the crimes of wire fraud and conspiracy.”).
Mr. Dowie offers no factual or legal support for his hair-splitting analysis of section 2802 and the facts established by his conviction, nor could he. His convictions for conspiracy and wire fraud established more than a mere “intent to inflate” billings; they established more than “over billing.” They established that Mr. Dowie entered into an agreement to accomplish an “illegal objective,” see Boone, 951 F.2d at 1543, and willfully participated in a scheme to defraud FH clients, with ““knowledge of its fraudulent nature and with intent that these illicit objectives be achieved,”' Ciccone, 219 F.3d at 1083-84 (citation omitted). In short, Mr. Dowie knew he was breaking the law.
The District Court properly rejected Mr. Dowie's blanket denial of the scienter established by his criminal convictions. Even on a motion to dismiss, the court is not required to accept as true “allegations that contradict matters properly subject to judicial notice or by exhibit” or “allegations that are merely conclusory, unwarranted deductions of fact, or unreasonable inferences.” Sprewell, 266 F.3d at 988.
Moreover, under Mr. Dowie's view the express exclusion of Labor Code section 2802 would be effectively eliminated. Any employee would qualify for indemnity so long as he had the temerity to insist - notwithstanding the wrongfulness of his actions, notwithstanding his criminal convictions, and notwithstanding proof beyond a reasonable doubt of his scienter - that he did not believe his actions were unlawful. Eliminating the exclusion clause is a matter for the legislature, as Mr. Dowie knows. See AOB at 14 (stating that the legislature “could have excluded whatever it wanted to exclude, under whatever circumstances it deemed appropriate. It did not do so, however, and the court below should not have undertaken that legislative task.”). What Mr. Dowie refuses to see is that the California legislature has excluded certain actions from the scope of indemnity for employees - specifically, actions that are unlawful and that the employees believed were unlawful. The District Court was bound to apply that exclusion to Mr. Dowie's participation in a conspiracy to commit wire fraud against FH's clients.11
Second, Mr. Dowie claimed that his reading of Labor Code section 2802 comported with a “fundamental public policy in California” to protect employees. AOB at 8. But Mr. Dowie's view of Labor Code section 2802 sharply conflicts with California public policy, as reflected in other laws precluding the indemnification of intentional wrongs. For example, California Corporations Code section 317(b) provides in pertinent part:
(b) A corporation shall have power to indemnify any person who was or is a party or is threatened to be made a party to any proceeding (other than an action by or in the right of the corporation to procure a judgment in its favor) by reason of the fact that the person is or was an agent of the corporation, against expenses, judgments, fines, settlements, and other amounts actually and reasonably incurred in connection with the proceeding if that person acted in good faith and in a manner the person reasonably believed to be in the best interests of the corporation and, in the case of a criminal proceeding, had no reasonable cause to believe the, conduct of the person was unlawful. [Emphasis added.]12
In H.R. Plate v. Sun-Diamond Growers of Cal., 225 Cal. App. 3d 1115, 275 Cal. Rptr. 667 (1991), rev. den., the California Court of Appeal overturned an award of indemnity pursuant to section 317(b) after a jury found defendants liable for intentional interference with contract and prospective economic advantage. The court explained that section 317(b) “prohibits indemnification in cases of bad faith or intentional wrongdoing, because: Indemnification, if permitted too broadly, may violate ... basic tenets of public policy.”' Id. at 1124, 275 Cal. Rptr. at 672 (citation omitted). That is, “‘[i]t is inappropriate to permit management to use corporate funds to avoid the consequences of wrongful conduct or conduct involving bad faith. A director, officer, or employee who acted wrongfully or in bad faith should not expect to receiveassistance from the corporation for legal or other expenses .... Any other rule would tend to encourage socially undesirable conduct.”’ Id., 275 Cal. Rptr. at 672 (citation omitted). See also Cal. Amplifier, Inc. v. RLI Ins. Co., 94 Cal. App. 4th 102, 116, 113 Cal. Rptr. 2d 915, 926 (2001) (“[California] Insurance Code section 533 embodies a public policy to discourage willful wrongdoing. Liability arising from intentional and inherently or predictably harmful conduct cannot be covered by liability insurance.”) (citations omitted); J.B. Aguerre, Inc. v. Am. Guarantee & Liab. Ins. Co., 59 Cal. App. 4th 6, 14, 6 Cal. Rptr. 2d 837, 841 (1997) ( “California public policy bars indemnification against punitive damage awards.” [Emphasis in original.]).
In sum, the District Court's interpretation of Labor Code section 2802 's exclusion for unlawful acts that employees believed to be unlawful was based on the plain language of the statute and was fully consistent with a broad public policy in California that bars the indemnification of intentional wrongdoing. Mr. Dowie's interpretation effectively nullifies this exclusion within Labor Code section 2802 and flies in the face of that public policy. The District Court did not err in dismissing Mr. Dowie's First Cause of Action based on conspiracy and wire fraud convictions that fell within the exclusion of Labor Code section 2802.
Third, Mr. Dowie claims (without elaboration or authority) that the District Court's interpretation of section 2802 was wrong because it gives an employee facing criminal prosecution less protection under section 2802 than one facing “an ordinary civil lawsuit,” and in fact gives no protection for defense fees “until after the [criminal] trial is over.” AOB at 9 (emphasis in original).13 The District Court's ruling, however, did not place Mr. Dowie in a worse position than a civil defendant. In an ordinary civil suit, it is well settled that California employers have no duty whatsoever under Labor Code section 2802 to advance defense fees. Employers can sit back and wait “until after the trial [of their employee] is over” to see if any obligation to indemnify will arise or whether - as with Mr. Dowie the employee is found to have engaged in unlawful actions that preclude indemnity. Cassady v. Morgan, Lewis & Bockius LLP, 145 Cal. App. 4th 220, 237, 238, 51 Cal. Rptr. 3d 527, 540 (2006) (“An employer may obviate the need to indemnify for attorney's fees by providing counsel under some circumstances, but it is not required to do so” [internal citation omitted]; “if the Legislature wished to impose a duty to defend as well as the duty to indemnify, it knew how to do so.”). See also Plancarte v. Guardsmark, LLC, 118 Cal. App. 4th 640, 648-49, 13 Cal. Rptr. 3d 315, 321-322 (2004) (“Unlike an insurer, the employer is not mandated to defend the employee whenever there is a potential for liability.”; an employer can defend an employee or “await the outcome of the trial”; “[U]ntil the underlying action against the employee is resolved, the employer cannot be deemed ‘fully informed’ of what was done in the employer's name.”); Grissom v. Vons Cos., Inc.; 1 Cal. App. 4th 52, 57, 1 Cal. Rptr. 2d 808, 811 (1991) ( “Section 2802 does not say that an employer must ‘defend’ an employee. The word ‘defend’ does not appear in section 2802. The statute merely requires the employer to indemnify the employee ...” [emphasis in original]).
Fourth, Mr. Dowie appears to believe, that he is entitled to indemnity under Labor Code section 2802 if he can show that his trial defense was not as good as it could have been if his attorneys had been getting paid by FH. See AOB at 9 (“depriving an employee of the means to defend himself will typically facilitate a conviction”); AOB at 11 (“it should not be necessary to speculate on the defense that the firm would have provided had it been compensated”) (emphasis in original); AOB at 13 (denial of indemnity under section 2802 implicitly assumes that “plaintiff was afforded the same defense he would have received had his attorneys been paid. It is more likely that the conviction was eased by decisions made by unpaid counsel running ever more deeply in the red.”). This argument is a non-starter, because (as just shown above) FH had no duty to advance defense fees to Mr. Dowie under section 2802 and, thus, no responsibility for the quality of the criminal defense he chose to present. Mr. Dowie offers no authority to the contrary.14
The fact that Labor Code section 2802 does not address advancement of defense fees or the quality of legal defense also disposes of Mr. Dowie's reliance on U.S. v. Stein, 435 F. Supp. 2d 330, 356 (S.D.N.Y. 2006), aff'd --- F. 3d ---, 2008 WL 3982104 (2d Cir. Aug. 28, 2008). See AOB at 4, 10-13. In Stein, the Second Circuit identified Fifth and Sixth Amendment violations based on the district court's findings that the defendants' trial preparations were hampered because their employer succumbed to government pressure and limited the advancement of attorneys' fees. See 2008 WL 3982104 at *16, n. 10, *22. Neither the district court nor the circuit court in Stein ever decided whether the defendants were entitled to indemnity (although the district court did state they would not be, if “their actions were both unlawful and ‘believed by them to be unlawful’ at the time ..., “ 435 F.Supp.2d at 356, n. 119). Here, in contrast, Mr. Dowie seeks indemnity (not advancement) under Labor Code section 2802, after suffering multiple convictions that plainly disqualify him from indemnity. Stein, therefore, provides no support to Mr. Dowie' endeavor.
Nonetheless, it bears noting that Mr. Dowie failed to allege a single respect in which his criminal defense was actually hampered by the lack of advancement. In fact, Mr. Dowie admits his defense attorneys “were willing to stick with him.” AOB at 21. He further admits “it is true that Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher provided a defense, and undoubtedly a very good one ...” AOB at 11. Thus, neither the law nor the .facts of Stein are applicable to Labor Code section 2802 or to Mr. Dowie's situation.15
Fifth, Mr. Dowie argues that “it is not dispositive of the respondeat superior issue that plaintiff was convicted of criminal fraud.” AOB at 15-16. Mr. Dowie notes that FH admitted its respondeat superior liability for Mr. Dowie's fraud against the City of Los Angeles, because it settled the City's suit for $5.7 million. Id at 15. But respondeat superior is not the issue here. FH does not deny Mr. Dowie indemnification under Labor Code section 2802 on the ground that it was not liable to third parties for his actions; it denies indemnification on the ground that he engaged in - and was convicted of -actions that he knew were unlawful and that disqualified him from indemnity under that statute.
In summary, the District Court correctly dismissed (for the second time) Mr. Dowie's claim for indemnity under California Labor Code section 2802. Neither the letter nor the spirit of that statute supported Mr. Dowie's request to be indemnified for his unsuccessful criminal defense to federal wire fraud and conspiracy charges.
VII. MR. DOWIE FAILED TO STATE A CLAIM FOR EQUITABLE INDEMNITY.
The District Court dismissed with prejudice Mr. Dowie's Second Cause of Action against FH (implied equitable indemnity) on the ground that equitable indemnity may be available to a person “‘who, without active fault on his part, has been compelled by reason of some legal obligation, to pay damages occasioned by the initial and active negligence of another.”’ ER, 9, quoting San Francisco Examiner Division v. Sweati, 248 Cal. App. 2d 493, 497, 56 Cal. Rptr. 711, 713-14 (1967). The District Court also noted, id., that:
Dowie can hardly claim to be without active fault in this matter, or that the equities otherwise favor him in this action. Dowie received the benefit of excellent lawyering pre-indictment and was reimbursed for the cost of those services. No case supports his contention that, having now been convicted of a serious crime that has also exposed F-H to substantial civil liability, he should now be reimbursed for his costs of defense.
The District Court was correct. Mr. Dowie's lone authority below and on appeal, McCrary Construction. Co. v. Metal Deck Specialists, Inc., 133 Cal. App. 4th 1528, 35 Cal. Rptr. 3d 624 (2005) (AOB at 21), certainly does not support his purported claim for relief. McCrary was a contractual indemnity case, which noted only in passing that another form of indemnity “may arise from the equities of particular circumstances.” Id. at 1536, 35 Cal. Rptr. 3d at 630 (citing San Francisco Examiner). The unusual circumstances in Mr. Dowie's case are far from those qualifying for equitable indemnity under San Francisco Examiner. Mr. Dowie was not compelled to pay damages (or attorneys' fees) for the negligence of another; and he was guilty of very “active fault” himself - criminal wire fraud. There is nothing inequitable about Mr. Dowie's remaining responsible for the expenses attendant to that criminal conduct, including those relating to his unsuccessful defense.
On appeal, Mr. Dowie's one-page analysis and defense of his equitable indemnity claim rests on allegations in the Complaint that FH was “complicit” with Mr. Dowie in “the actions for which he was convicted” and “reaped the benefits” of those actions, which were undertaken “on behalf of, with the knowledge of, and with the authorization of” FH. AOB at 21-22, quoting ER 23, ¶15. Mr. Dowie laments that the District Court seemed to disregard his allegations, and instead viewed FH as “a victim of plaintiff's criminal conduct.” AOB at 22.16 That corporate complicity theory failed to state a claim for equitable indemnity under San Francisco Examiner, since it still necessarily concedes Mr. Dowie was guilty of active fault - which disqualifies him from equitable indemnity. Mr. Dowie's allegations also run afoul of the limits of Labor Code section 2802 and Corporations Code section 317(b), which preclude indemnification of employees who engage in criminal actions such as Mr. Dowie's conspiracy and wire fraud. See §VI, above. It cannot plausibly be maintained that “equitable” indemnity should extend to situations where indemnity is prohibited as a matter of law on public policy grounds.
Mr. Dowie also offhandedly opines that “to the extent that plaintiffs fees were cut off by defendant as part of a deal in which defendant actively assisted the government in its prosecution of plaintiff, then the equitable considerations are even stronger.” AOB at 22.17 But Mr. Dowie does not provide any legal support for the bizarre proposition that “equitable considerations” would somehow favor a dishonest employee convicted of defrauding clients over an employer that cooperated in his successful prosecution. It bears remembering (as the District Court did, ER, 9, 12) that: FH covered a year's worth of Mr. Dowie's legal expenses pre-indictment and in the related civil suit brought against them by the City of Los Angeles as a result of Mr. Dowie's fraudulent over billing scheme (ER, 21-22, ¶10-14); FH paid about $6 million to settle that civil suit for itself and Mr. Dowie (ER, 22, ¶13); Mr. Dowie was convicted of a “serious crime” based on his over billing scheme (ER, 9; SER, 64); FH was not indicted for anything (see ER, 20, ¶ ER 23, ¶15); and the “impecunious” Mr. Dowie incurred his substantial defense fees after learning that FH was neither inclined nor required to advance them (ER, 22, ¶14 and §VI, supra).
In short, there was and is nothing equitable about Mr. Dowie's demand that FH pay another $3 million to cover the cost of his failed criminal defense.
VIII. MR. DOWIE FAILED TO STATE A CLAIM FOR FRAUD.
The District Court dismissed without prejudice Mr. Dowie's Third Cause of Action against FH (fraudulent concealment) on the ground that Mr. Dowie failed to allege any cognizable harm that was caused by his reliance on FH's alleged promise to pay his criminal defense fees. Specifically:
The only result of Dowie's reliance on the alleged misrepresentations was that Dowie received competent private defense counsel free of charge for the entire pre-indictment investigation period. This was a benefit to Dowie. Dowie unconvincingly argues that he was damaged by being forced to pay for lawyers he could not afford, merely because they had become familiar with his case. Like many other defendants, when F-H stopped paying for Dowie's attorneys, Dowie had a choice regarding his counsel. He could have chosen a public defender, if he qualified. He could have chosen other private defense attorneys. Instead, he chose to continue with Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher. Understandably, this may have been a difficult decision. But without F-H's earlier assistance, Dowie would have more quickly faced difficult decisions regarding representation in addition to a larger legal bill. Dowie's decision to incur $3 million in defense costs was entirely his own; he has failed to allege any damages that resulted from the purported fraud.
ER, 9-10. The District Court properly dismissed the fraud claim, and Mr. Dowie chose to stand on his defective claim rather than try to plead any additional facts. On appeal, he presents no basis for concluding that any fraudulent misrepresentation or concealment by FH induced him to retain Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher for his criminal defense despite his inability to pay for them. Nor does Mr. Dowie show that the District Court erred in finding that FH's cessation of advances for that defense was not a cognizable harm. Mr. Dowie does not dispute the District Court's conclusion that he had the right to seek the assistance of a public defender (Mr. Dowie claims he was “impecunious,” AOB at 4; ER, 22, ¶14) or to retain different private counsel that he could afford.18 Mr. Dowie's argument based on Stein did not undermine that conclusion either, because Stein is both legally and factually inapposite. See §VI, supra.
In fact, Mr. Dowie's Third Cause of Action is not a fraud claim at all, because he utterly failed to plead any harm caused by his reliance on an alleged misrepresentation or concealment. Detrimental reliance is the essence of fraud, and a plaintiff can never be excused “from the requirement of pleading reliance, since specific pleading is necessary to ‘establish a complete causal relationship’ between the alleged misrepresentations and the harm claimed to have resulted therefrom.” Mirkin v. Wasserman, 5 Cal.4th 1082, 1091, 23 Cal.Rptr.2d 101, 105-06 (1993). See also Buckland v. Threshold Enterprises, Ltd., 155 Cal. App. 4th 798, 808, 66 Cal. Rptr. 3d 543, 549 (2007) (“In order to justify recovery, the recipient of a misrepresentation must rely upon the truth of the misrepresentation itself, and his reliance upon its truth must be a substantial factor in inducing him to act or to refrain from action. It is not enough that, without belief in its truth, he proceeds to enter into the transaction in the expectation that he will be compensated in an action for damages for falsity.” [Citations omitted.]).
Mr. Dowie claims the District Court “did not take issue with the question of fraudulent misrepresentation or justifiable reliance” AOB at 18. In fact, the District Court never addressed either of those elements of Mr. Dowie's fraud claim. However, the record is clear that neither was properly alleged, and this Court can affirm on any valid ground in the record. Atel Financial, Orsay, supra.
Mr. Dowie alleges that beginning in May 2004, long before his indictment, FH began paying his fees in the criminal investigation and related civil litigation brought by the City of Los Angeles. See ER, 20-21, ¶¶8-11. Mr. Dowie's attorneys, Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher, sent FH invoices each month and FH paid the law firm directly. ER, 21, ¶11. But “[u]pon his indictment [on June 9, 2005], however, [FH] suddenly and without warning ceased paying his attorneys fees,” and he “immediately” began to incur a “monumental debt.” ER, 21, ¶14.19 A possible fraud theory might have been pleaded if Mr. Dowie had alleged that he did not learn until after his trial that FH would not pay his defense fees. But Mr. Dowie admits he incurred his $3 million of defense fees only after FH made unmistakably clear - immediately after his indictment - that it would not pay post-indictment fees. In short, Mr. Dowie's defense debt was not incurred as a result of his reasonable reliance on the truth of FH's alleged promise to pay; it arose after he disregarded FH's unequivocal refusal to pay -i.e., after he knew the supposed promise was not true. See Buckland, 155 Cal. App. 4th at 808, 66 Cal. Rptr. 3d at 549 (“It is not enough that, without belief in [a statement's] truth, [plaintiff] proceeds to enter into the transaction ...”). Quite simply, FH did not induce Mr. Dowie to incur the fees of Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher. As the District Court found, Mr. Dowie chose to do so.20
In any event, no valid fraud claim could possibly be pleaded in this instance, because FH could not lawfully indemnify Mr. Dowie for his criminal fraud. Indemnity under Labor Code section 2802 does not include knowingly unlawful acts of employees (such as Mr. Dowie's conspiracy and wire fraud), and the corporations laws of California and Delaware also affirmatively prohibited FH from indemnifying Mr. Dowie in such circumstances. See §VI, supra. Thus, FH could not agree to provide indemnity for Mr. Dowie's fraudulent acts, and Mr. Dowie cannot circumvent that restriction by labeling his claim “fraud,” “false promise,” or anything else. See, e.g., Hydrotech Sys., Ltd. v. Oasis Waterpark, 52 Cal. 3d 988, 1002, 277 Cal. Rptr. 517, 526 (1991) (“However artful the pleadings, if the primary fraud alleged is a promise to pay for unlicensed construction work, and the primary relief sought is compensation for the work, section 7301 [of California Business & Professions Code] bars the action.”); Lawler v. Jacobs, 83 Cal. App. 4th 723, 737-38, 100 Cal. Rptr. 2d 52, 62-63 (2000), rev. den. (“‘a contract against public policy or against the mandate of the statute, may not be made the foundation of any action”’ (citation omitted); a home seller's contract claim cannot be recast as a “false promise” to pay, because “[a]s a matter of law it is unreasonable to rely on an illegal waiver [of California Code of Civil Procedure §580b, the “anti-deficiency” statute] ...”).
In short, Mr. Dowie failed to plead a valid fraud theory based on a supposed promise by FH to indemnify his criminal defense fees. The District Court correctly concluded that Mr. Dowie failed to plead a cognizable harm, and Mr. Dowie failed to plead actual and reasonable reliance on any alleged misrepresentation or concealment by FH.
IX. MR. DOWIE FAILED TO STATE A CLAIM UNDER OMNICOM'S BYLAWS.
The District Court dismissed without prejudice Mr. Dowie's Fifth Cause of Action against Omnicom (FH's parent corporation), for breach of written contract (bylaws) to indemnify him for his legal expense. The District Court concluded that the mandatory indemnification portion of Omnicom's bylaws on which Mr. Dowie relied applied only to officers and directors of Omnicom, and Mr. Dowie had not alleged he held such status. ER, 11-12. The District Court also ruled that the intentional nature of the fraud leading to Mr. Dowie's criminal convictions disqualified him for indemnity under the by-laws. ER, 12.
The District Court was correct. First, Mr. Dowie never claimed to be an officer or director of Omnicom; he admits that he “served Fleishman-Hillard.” AOB at 23; ER, 19, ¶5. As a result, Mr. Dowie was not covered by Omnicom's mandatory indemnity provision pertaining to its officers and directors only.
Article IX, Section 1(a) of the Omnicom bylaws (attached to Mr. Dowie's Complaint, ER, 30) provides that Omnicom “shall” provide indemnity in three kinds of actions or proceedings: those brought by third-parties (including criminal cases), those brought “by or in the right of the Corporation (i.e., including derivative actions), and those brought “by or in the right of any other Corporation [or other entity], which any director or officer of [Omnicom] served in any capacity at the request of [Omnicom].” However, indemnify in the foregoing actions is available only to “any person” who is “made, or threatened to be made, a party ... by reason of the fact that he, his testator or intestate, was a director or officer of [Omnicom], or served such other corporation [or other entity] at the request of [Omnicom] in any capacity.”21 [Italics added.] Thus, only Omnicom's officers and directors qualified for mandatory indemnity, although they could qualify even if they were also serving in a non-officer, non-director capacity at another corporation at the request of Omnicom.
Mr. Dowie has misread the text of the bylaws. He latched on to the phrase “any person,” linked it to the phrase “served such other corporation... at the request of [Omnicom],” and concluded that he was entitled to indemnity because Omnicom supposedly requested him to serve FH as an employee in its Los Angeles office. AOB at 22-23. But Mr. Dowie overlooked the key qualifying phrase italicized in the preceding paragraph: “any director or officer of [Omnicom] [who] served in any capacity at the request of [Omnicom].” Properly understood (as the District Court did), the Omnicom bylaws extend mandatory indemnity only to officers and directors, not to employees (let alone employees of subsidiaries such as FH).
The District Court's proper reading is confirmed by two other facts. First, the bylaws contain a separate provision addressing discretionary indemnity for those who are neither officers nor directors. Section 2 of Article IX of Omnicom's bylaws provides that: “The Corporation may, to the fullest extent permitted by law, indemnify or advance the expenses of any other person, including agents and employees to whom the Corporation is permitted by law to provide indemnification or advancement of fees.” ER, 30. Thus, Section 1(a) does indeed apply only to Omnicom officers and directors. Since, as noted just above, Mr. Dowie does not (and cannot) claim he was an officer or director of Omnicom, the District Court correctly concluded he was not eligible for mandatory indemnity under Section 1(a) of Omnicom's bylaws.
Second, the District Court's reading of Section 1(a) is supported by the fact that the mandatory indemnity language of Omnicom's bylaws is almost a verbatim restatement of portions of New York Business Corporation Law section 721 (“Nonexclusivity of statutory provisions for indemnification of directors and officers”) and section 722 (“Authorization for indemnification of directors and officers”) (emphases added).22 See also Pilipiak v. Keyes, 286 A.D.2d 231-32, 729 N.Y.S.2d 99, 100 (2001) (“Pursuant to Business Corporation Law § 721, captioned, ‘Nonexclusivity of statutory provisions for indemnification of directors and officers,’ an officer or director may be entitled to indemnification ...”).
Thus, Mr. Dowie (not the District Court) misinterpreted the Omnicom bylaws. To be eligible for Omnicom's mandatory indemnity provision, Mr. Dowie had to be an officer or director. He was neither.23
Second, the District Court correctly concluded that “Dowie's conviction for wire fraud unambiguously disqualifies him for indemnification even if he were an officer or director of Omnicom.” ER, 12. Section 1(a) of Article IX stated: “no indemnification may be made to or on behalf of such person if (i) his or her acts were committed in bad faith or were the result of his or her active and deliberate dishonesty and were material to such action or proceedings ...” ER, 30. Mr. Dowie's convictions for conspiracy and wire fraud established that he had acted in “bad faith” as a result of “active and deliberate dishonesty.” See §VI, supra. (Mr. Dowie's actions were certainly “material” to the criminal “proceedings” filed against him; they underlay the counts of conviction.)
Mr. Dowie “concedes that his conviction would ordinarily be sufficient to support a finding that he committed acts which would fall within the exception in Article IX, Section 1(a), but not in this case.” AOB at 23. He insists that Omnicom's bylaws intended that convicted felons like Mr. Dowie, who seek indemnity for their legal expenses, should be “able to re-litigate in a civil proceeding some elements of their criminal convictions.” AOB at 24. But the District Court's ruling addressed and disposed of that argument: “Although the By-Laws indicate that ‘a conviction’ following a criminal action does not create a presumption that an officer or director believed his or her conduct to be unlawful [see Section 1(b), ER, 30], Dowie is not simply faced with ‘a conviction.’ Dowie himself was convicted, at a trial before a jury, of a crime that requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt that he personally possessed the specific intent to defraud.” ER, 12.
Again, the District Court was correct. Section 1(b) of Omnicom's indemnity bylaw was based on a nearly identical provision in New York Business Corporation Law section 721. That provision has been interpreted - in a fraud case - precisely as the District Court interpreted Section 1 (b) of the Omnicom bylaws:
Pursuant to Business Corporation Law § 721, ... an officer or director may be entitled to indemnification, provided there has been no judgment which necessarily determines that the officer or director acted in bad faith or was deliberately dishonest. ... While the statute makes no presumption that the judgment proves that the director or officer acted wrongfully toward the corporation, reimbursement would be precluded as a matter of law, if the judgment or conviction necessarily includes a finding of deliberate dishonesty or bad faith (Business Corporation Law § 722)...[B]y the time the motion for reargument and renewal had been decided, Mr. Keyes had been convicted of grand larceny and scheme to defraud in the first degree (Penal Law § 190.65). Thus, it was unlawful for the corporation to indemnify Mr. Keyes for the expenses he incurred in defending the criminal action inasmuch as the elements of intent under the applicable provisions of the Penal Law require a finding of deliberate dishonesty.
Pilipiak, 286 A.D.2d at 231, 729 N.Y.S.2d at 100 (emphasis added, internal citations omitted). Thus, Mr. Dowie's fraud convictions did indeed disqualify him from indemnification under the bylaws of Omnicom (even assuming, arguendo, he had the required officer or director status).
X MR. DOWIE FAILED TO STATE A CLAIM FOR PROMISSORY ESTOPPEL.
In dismissing without prejudice the Sixth Cause of Action against FH (promissory estoppel), the District Court reiterated the essence of its ruling as to the fraud claim: “Dowie fails to allege a substantial detriment caused by his reliance on F-H's alleged promise. Instead, it is apparent from his allegations that Dowie only received a substantial benefit - free legal representation by competent counsel during the criminal investigation and the related civil lawsuit.” ER, 12.
Mr. Dowie's brief treats his promissory estoppel and fraud claims together, AOB at 17-21, but the estoppel theory suffers from even more incurable defects. The elements of promissory estoppel are: 1) a clear and unambiguous promise; 2) actual reliance by plaintiff; 3) reasonable and foreseeable reliance by plaintiff; 4) injury to plaintiff caused by his reliance; and 5) justice requires enforcement of the promise. See U.S. Ecology, Inc. v. Cal., 129 Cal. App. 4th 887, 901-902, 28 Cal. Rptr. 3d 894, 905 (2005). The remedy for a claim of promissory estoppel is “equitably molded ad hoc for each case according to the dictates of good faith, conscience, and justice.” Id. at 28 Cal. Rptr. 3d 905, 907. See also Kajima/Ray Wilson v. L.A. County MTA, 23 Cal. 4th 305, 317-18, 96 Cal. Rptr. 2d 747, 755 (2000) (stating that in determining the proper remedy, “it is incumbent on this court to consider the broad-ranging social consequences of the chosen remedy”).
Mr. Dowie again claims the District Court “did not take issue with the question of a false promise or reliance ...” AOB at 19. In fact, the District Court never addressed either of those elements of Mr. Dowie's estoppel claim, because it dismissed for failure to plead cognizable injury. But as the analysis of his fraud claim showed (§ VIII, above), Mr. Dowie did not allege actual (let alone reasonable) reliance upon any alleged promise of FH to indemnify him for the costs of his failed criminal defense.
Nor did Mr. Dowie plead a “clear and unambiguous” promise. Putting aside that the claimed promise was patently a fiction, Mr. Dowie actually alleged a theory of concealment rather than affirmative misrepresentation.24 AOB at 18 (stating that it was FH's “silence, its failure to tell him that it would cut off his fees, that constitutes fraudulent concealment.... It was a misrepresentation for [FH] not to state that it would cut him. off if he was indicted.”) (emphasis in original). Even if Mr. Dowie could have pleaded a proper concealment theory, he offers no authority for the proposition that concealment can constitute the “unambiguous promise” required to state a valid claim of promissory estoppel.25
In any event, even if Mr. Dowie surmounted the foregoing fatal defects in his promissory estoppel claim, it would be inherently unjust and inequitable to require FH to pay for Mr. Dowie's unsuccessful criminal defense. Mr. Dowie was convicted of defrauding FH clients; he cost FH millions in litigation and settlement costs; and Labor Code section 2802 (as well as the corporations laws of California and Delaware) makes clear a strong public policy against indemnity for knowingly unlawful acts like Mr. Dowie's conspiracy and wire fraud. In short, considering the “broad-ranging social consequences” (Kajima/Ray Wilson), there was no good reason to apply the doctrine of promissory estoppel to require FH to indemnify Mr. Dowie for the cost of his failed criminal defense.
XI. CONCLUSION
The District Court did not err in dismissing Mr. Dowie's Complaint for failing to state any valid claim for relief. The District Court's decision should be affirmed.

After the jury returned its multiple guilty verdicts against Mr. Dowie, he filed a motion to dismiss the indictment based on the “Thompson Memorandum” and Stein. The District Court (which presided over both of Mr. Dowie's indemnity actions against FH and his criminal case) denied the motion on October 4, 2006: “the Court is in a position to determine that no constitutional violation, and therefore no prejudice, has occurred. In this case, F-H's decision to cease paying Defendant Dowie's legal bills did not result in a change of counsel. On the contrary, four attorneys from Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher continued to vigorously defend that case on Dowie's behalf, appearing daily in court during the entire trial. There was no evidence of any corner-cutting either. Counsel conducted lengthy, thorough cross-examination of all Government witnesses in ways that repeatedly demonstrated that they were intimately familiar with the massive number of documents relevant to the charges against their client. In short, Defendant Dowie received as good a defense as anyone in his circumstances could have possibly received.” Stodder, 2006 WL 3066196 at *2. See attached copy.
The District Court also found that “F-H ceased paying for Dowie's legal fees after Dowie had threatened to embarrass the company if he did not receive a substantial severance and after he had filed a civil suit against F-H for wrongful termination. At the same time, F-H continued to pay for Defendant Stodder's legal defense despite the supposed in terroram effect of the Thompson Memorandum. Thus, the record indicates that it was Defendant Dowie's strategy and tactics in dealing with F-H, and not the Thompson Memorandum, that motivated F-H to cease funding his criminal defense.” Id. at *2.
