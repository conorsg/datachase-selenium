*1 INTRODUCTION
Plaintiff Scott Minkler responds to the amicus brief filed by the American Insurance Association (“AIA”) and its related amici. Much of the AIA's brief presents arguments already raised by Safeco and addressed by Minkler in his opening and reply briefs. Minkler will not reiterate those arguments here, and will endeavor to address only new issues raised by the AIA.
The AIA, like Safeco, fails to apply the fundamental principles of California insurance-policy interpretation. Specifically, like Safeco, the AIA concedes that at the first step of the process -- application of the “plain meaning” of the disputed policy terms -- its argument fails. In fact, the AIA elides this step from its discussion of the applicable rules of policy construction.
The AIA argues that Minkler's construction of the severability clause in Safeco's policy would create “absurd” results in four hypothetical examples (including one so hypothetical that it is not even based on the language in Safeco's policy.) In essence, the AIA urges the Court to avoid these results by reading the severability clause out of the policy.
Minkler does not dispute that the hypothetical problems that the AIA points to would not exist if Safeco's policy did not contain a severability clause. But it does contain a severability clause. Hence, pretending that the clause does not exist is not a viable way to construe the policy. The tension between the promise in the severability clause that the policy's coverage will extend to every insured as if they are the only person covered by the policy, and other provisions of the policy that appear to take their meaning from the *2 existence of multiple insureds, does not help Safeco. Rather, it shows that Safeco's policy is internally inconsistent, and is therefore ambiguous.
ARGUMENT
A. The AIA's brief does not apply California insurance-interpretation principles
1. The AIA, like Safeco, concedes that its preferred construction of the policy is not supported by the plain meaning of the policy language
As noted in Minker's reply brief at page 5 through 7, Safeco has admitted that its construction of the policy is not based on the plain meaning of the policy language. Instead, it depends on an understanding of the history of the insurance industry's use of severability clauses, and the industry's intent based on that history.
The AIA's brief contains the same concession, but the AIA seeks to camouflage it. On page 5, the AIA purports to discuss the basic principles of insurance-policy construction under California law. The AIA cites the standard insurance-construction cases: Bank of the West v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1254; AIU Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1990) 51 Cal.3d 807; and Bay Cities Paving & Grading, Inc. v. Lawyers' Mut. Ins. Co. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 854, 866.
But its discussion of the basic approach to insurance-policy construction laid out in these cases is incomplete. The AIA simply omits any mention of the first step of the familiar 3-step analysis -- the plain meaning of the words used in the policy. (See, e.g., AIU, 51 Cal.3d at pp. 821-822.) Instead, it skips directly to step 2 -- resolution of ambiguities by interpreting the provision “in the sense *3 that the promisor (i.e., the insurer) believed that the promisee understood it at the time of contract formation.” (AIA brief at 5, citing Bank of the West, 2 Cal.4th at p. 1265.)
Nowhere else in the AIA's discussion of the rules of insurance-policy construction does it mention the plain-meaning step of the process. Accordingly, it never undertakes any effort to apply that step in order to resolve the policy-construction dispute before this Court. The AIA's approach, like Safeco's approach, therefore concedes that its preferred construction of the policy is based on something other than the plain meaning of the words the insurer chose to use in the policy.
2. The AIA asks this Court to choose which part of the Safeco policy to ignore
The AIA understandably urges the Court to follow the “majority” rule, which subordinates the meaning of the severability clause to the meaning of a collective exclusion. (AIA brief at 3-5.) But in commending this approach to the Court, the AIA does not acknowledge that the only way to achieve its preferred outcome is to either re-write the severability clause so that it has a meaning entirely unrelated to the words it contains, or to ignore it outright.
By contrast, Minkler's preferred construction of the policy does not do linguistic violence to the language in the policy -- nor even to the language of the intentional-acts exclusion. This is because once the policy is construed in the manner stated in the severability clause -- that is, as if the insured was the only insured under the policy -- the reference to “an” insured in the exclusion refers to that insured.
*4 In other words, when there is only one insured, the terms “an” insured and “the” insured are synonyms. If Betty Schwartz had lived alone and there were no other insureds under her policy, the reference in the exclusion to “an” insured would mean “the” insured -- her, and her alone.
Hence, there is nothing ungrammatical about Minkler's construction of the policy. While it concededly does not give the article “an” in the intentional-acts exclusion its full potential sweep if there are multiple insured, this is because of the way the severability clause functions when it is given its plain meaning.
The AIA simply urges this Court to ignore that meaning, and to construe the exclusion as if the severability clause did not exist. While several courts have chosen this approach, Minkler's briefs show that it is not an outcome that is consistent with the way this Court has instructed California courts to interpret insurance policies.
3. The AIA relies on abstract hypotheticals rather than the policy terms actually at issue
One of the cardinal rules of California insurance-contract interpretation is that insurance-policy provisions cannot be found to be ambiguous in the abstract. (Bay Cities Paving & Grading, Inc., 5 Cal.4th at p. 867.) Phrased slightly differently, “[t]here cannot be an ambiguity per se, i.e. an ambiguity unrelated to an application.” (California State Auto. Assn. Inter-Ins. Bureau v. Superior Court (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 855, 859, fn. 1.)
In practice, this rule means an insured is not permitted to try to create an ambiguity in the policy by pointing out potential ways that the provision in question might be ambiguous in circumstances that *5 are not related to the actual dispute in question. But this is precisely what the AIA does in its brief when it argues that Minkler's construction of the policy would have absurd consequences.
For example, at page 14 of its brief, the AIA argues that a non-insured location exclusion would be negated “in certain vicarious liability situations arising out of property owned by one spouse-insured, but not the other.” Even if this were true, it does not speak to the coverage dispute at issue here, involving the tension between the severability clause and a collective exclusion for intentional acts.
The hypothetical nature of the AIA's arguments is highlighted by the fact that only three of the four examples advanced by the AIA are actually based on the language of Safeco's policy. The fourth, relating to “business pursuits” is based on a discussion of a generic exclusion “found in homeowner's policies” that “typically exclude claims” arising from an insured's business pursuits. (AIA brief at 16.) No attempt is made to connect this example to the actual language in the policy.
Minkler has never suggested that Safeco's policy can be seamlessly construed. Rather, his argument is that the policy is poorly drafted, and that its severability clause is in tension with the intentional-acts exclusion when that exclusion is given the broadest construction that its language allows. It does not undermine Minkler's argument to point out that there is tension in other aspects of the policy that can only be resolved by reading out one portion of the policy.
*6 B. The AIA's examples of “absurd results” that occur when the severability clause is enforced merely highlight the ambiguity in Safeco's policy
As explained in Minkler's opening brief, one way that insurance policies are ambiguous is when they are not internally consistent. (St. Paul Mercury Ins. Co. v. Frontier Pacific Ins. Co. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1234, 1244, citing Delgado v. Heritage Life Ins. Co. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 262, 271; and Smith Kandal Real Estate v. Continental Cas. Co. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 406, 416.) This is the vice of Safeco's policy -- not all parts of its policy are consistent with the promise in the severability clause that the insurance applies separately, as if the insured were the only insured covered by the policy.
As Safeco and the AIA have pointed out, some courts have chosen to resolve the ambiguity by choosing the meaning that they believe is the most reasonable, or the correct one in light of the history of the insurance industry's use of severability clauses. There are two problems with this approach.
First, none of the courts that have adopted this approach have acknowledged that the history they cite generally refers to the first-generation clause that specifically referred to “the insured,” while the clauses in the policy that they construe are second-generation clauses that are phrased entirely differently. (Please see, Minkler's reply brief at 9-14.)
Second, under California law, if a court decides that a policy provision is ambiguous, the court does not attempt to determine which of the two (or more) reasonable meanings is the most reasonable, nor *7 does it try to determine which is the “correct” interpretation of the policy. (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 466.) Rather, the existence of the ambiguity that cannot be resolved using the first two steps in the process results in a finding of coverage. (Id.)
Here, the existence of tension between the severability clause and other parts of the policy if the former is given meaning does not justify reading the severability clause out of the policy. Rather, it highlights a drafting problem caused by Safeco's use of incompatible policy language.
For this reason, the adverse consequences that would supposedly befall California consumers if the Court were to find the policy ambiguous are greatly overstated. Severability clauses are not mandatory. And based on the insurers' explanation of their role, they are wholly unnecessary, and perhaps contrary to the insurers' own intent. Accordingly, as illustrated in Minkler's briefs, some insurers have eliminated them.
Nor does the existence of a severability clause automatically create ambiguity in the policy. That ambiguity only occurs when the clause is included in a policy in which other provisions operate in a way that makes the rights of one insured depend on the conduct of another insured. By drafting their exclusions more clearly, to relate specifically to the excluded risk, as opposed to who creates the risk, insurers have it within their power to exclude any risk they desire.
*8 CONCLUSION
The AIA's brief does not demonstrate that Safeco's policy is clear and unambiguous. It simply asks this Court to select which part of Safeco's policy that should not be construed in accordance with its terms. This approach to insurance-policy construction is not consistent with California law, and should be rejected.
