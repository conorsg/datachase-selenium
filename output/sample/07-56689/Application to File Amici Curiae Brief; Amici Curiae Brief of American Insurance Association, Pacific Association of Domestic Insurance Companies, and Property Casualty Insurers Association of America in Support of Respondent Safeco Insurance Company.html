*1 AMICI CURIAE BRIEF
I. INTRODUCTION.
Liability insurance policies typically provide exclusions for the intentional acts of “an insured” or “any insured.” The terms “an insured” or “any insured” are used in virtually all homeowners and commercial general liability policies. Such terminology is not only used in exclusionary clauses of standard insurance policies, but is also commonly found in the insuring agreements, definitions and conditions of such policies. As a result, the issues raised by the Ninth Circuit's certified question extend far beyond the exclusion for intentional acts, and implicate a myriad of other exclusions which utilize similar language. Since the words “an insured” are used 96 times in the Safeco policy, how the Court interprets these words will impact not just the intentional acts exclusion, but other parts of the policy where the identical words are used.
Minkler argues that the severability of insurance (or separation of insureds) clause should be interpreted to provide each insured with separate coverage, as if each were separately insured with a distinct policy. (Reply Brief, at pp. 6-7.) Minkler contends that the severability clause dictates that the intentional acts exclusion should bar coverage only for the insured committing the intentional act, and not for another insured whose complicity or other conduct may have contributed to the victim's injuries.
The concern for amid is that this interpretation will nullify not only the intentional acts exclusions, but other exclusions that employ similar language. As discussed in this brief, an interpretation of the words “an insured” that would limit the application of the exclusion to the insured making the claim for coverage will lead to absurd results in other parts of the policy--both exclusions and insuring clauses--where the identical terminology *2 is used. These policy distortions will occur in the interpretation of the typical “automobile” exclusion, the “insured location” exclusion, and the “business” exclusion, among others, all of which use the identical “an insured” terminology. Consequently, this Court should not adopt Minkler's strained and unworkable interpretation without recognizing that the unintended consequences of such a construction would be significant.
The argument that the severability clause creates separate and distinct insurance policies for each insured is fundamentally flawed. The severability clause merely provides that “[t]his insurance applies separately to each Insured except with respect to the Limit of Liability.” It does not provide a separate insurance policy for each insured, and more importantly, does nothing to alter the language of exclusions or other provisions in the policy.1 The idea that the severability clause creates separate insurance policies for each insured makes no sense because, for purposes of policy interpretation, there is but one policy and the terms of the policy as a whole govern. An insurance policy is a single, integrated written instrument with terms that are interrelated. Ultimately coverage must be examined in reference to the policy itself and the context in which the words appear. The notion that the policy can be “severed” as to certain categories of insureds without regard to how the language of the policy impacts the interpretation of the policy flies in the face of California's rules of contract interpretation. These rules mandate that words be analyzed in the context of the policy as a whole and with regard to their intended function in the policy.
*3 II. THE COURT IS URGED TO FOLLOW THE MAJORITY RULE THAT A SEVERABILITY CLAUSE AFFECTS ONLY EXCLUSIONS THAT REFER TO “THE INSURED.”
A substantial majority of cases in other jurisdictions have concluded that the severability clause only affects exclusions referring to “the insured,” not “any insured” or “an insured.” (Chacon v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co. (Colo. 1990) 788 P.2d 748, 751; Argent v. Brady (App. Div. 2006) 386 N.J. Super. 343, 901 A.2d 419, 426-27.) Exclusions with “any insured” or “an insured” language exclude coverage for the acts of all insureds even if committed by another-the so-called “innocent” insured. Only where an exclusion refers to the conduct by “the insured,” when coupled with a severability clause, is the exclusion to be read independently as to each insured.
Unlike the phrase “the insured,” the terms “an insured” or “any insured” clearly and unambiguously express an intent to preclude coverage for all insureds where the excluded conduct is committed by any insured. (Chacon, supra, 788 P.2d at p. 752 [exclusion for property damage “ ‘which is expected or intended by any insured’... clearly and unambiguously expresses an intention to deny coverage to all insureds when damage is intended or expected as a result of the actions of any insured.”]; Argent, supra, 901 A.2d at p. 424 [no ambiguity is created by the use of the phrase “an insured,” and exclusion was doubtlessly intended to exclude from coverage any liability resulting from the business pursuits of a covered person, regardless of whether the person sued was engaged in those business pursuits.]; Allstate Ins. Co. v. Stamp (1991) 134 N.H. 59, 588 A.2d 363, 365 [“We find that Allstate's use of the indefinite article ‘an,’ rather than the definite ‘the,’ before ‘insured’ is a *4 clear reference to any insured who commits an intentional act resulting in damages, regardless of whether or not he is the particular insured seeking coverage.”]; Pawtucket Mut. Ins. Co. v. Lebrecht (1963) 104 N.H. 465, 468, 190 A.2d 420, 422-23 [the use of “the definite expression ‘the Insured’ in certain provisions of the policy and the more indefinite or general expression ‘any Insured’ or ‘an Insured’ in other provisions is intended to cover differing situations.... [T]he provisions excluding from liability coverage injuries intentionally caused by ‘the Insured’ was meant to refer to a definite, specific insured, namely the insured who is involved in the occurrence [that] caused the injury and who is seeking coverage under the policy.”]; Johnson v. Allstate Ins. Co. (Me. 1997) 687 A.2d 642, 644 [“by excluding coverage for damages intentionally caused by ‘an insured person,’ Allstate unambiguously excluded coverage for damages intentionally caused by any insured person”]; Travelers Ins. Co. v. Blanchard (La. App. 1983) 431 So.2d 913 [exclusion applicable to acts committed by “an insured,” unambiguously excluded coverage as to all insureds].)
It is also important to note that a severability clause does not operate independently to create coverage and therefore cannot be construed to negate plainly worded exclusions. (Mutual of Enumclaw Ins. Co. v. Cross (2000) 103 Wash.App. 52, 62 [“clear and specific language in an exclusion prevails over a severability clause...[A]n exclusion is not negated by or rendered ambiguous by a severability clause”].) The purpose of a severability clause is simply to provide each insured with coverage up to the policy limits. It is not designed to modify plainly worded exclusions. (California Casualty Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1682, 1697 [“the purpose of severability clauses is to afford each insured a full measure of coverage up to the policy limits, not to negate bargained-for and plainly worded *5 exclusions.”]; Allstate Ins. Co. v. Kim (D. Haw. 2000) 121 F.Supp.2d 1301, 1308 [severability clause “clearly was intended to afford each insured a full measure of coverage up to the policy limits, not to negate the policy's intentional acts exclusion”].)
As explained in the next section, application of California's rules of policy interpretation compels the same conclusion as in the majority of cases across the country.
III. THE SEVERABILITY CLAUSE IN SAFECO'S POLICY DOES NOT RENDER THE INTENTIONAL ACTS EXCLUSION AMBIGUOUS.
A. California's Rules Of Insurance Policy Construction.
As this Court has made clear since approximately 1990, perceived ambiguities in insurance policies are not automatically construed against the insurer. (Bank of the West v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1254; AIU Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1990) 51 Cal.3d 807; Bay Cities Paving & Grading, Inc. v. Lawyers' Mutual Ins. Co. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 854, 866-867.) In order to ascertain whether there is an ambiguity in an insurance policy, the disputed policy language must first be examined in context with regard to its intended function in the policy. This requires a consideration of the policy as a whole, and the circumstances of the case in which the claim arises. (Bank of the West, supra, 2 Cal.4th at p. 1265.) An ambiguity is resolved by interpreting the provision in the sense the promisor (i.e., the insurer) believed the promisee understood it at the time of contract formation. (AIU, supra, 51 Cal.3d at p. 822.) “This rule... protects not the subjective beliefs of the insurer but, rather, ‘the objectively reasonable expectations of the insured.’ ” (Bank of the West, supra, 2 Cal.4th at p. 1265.)
*6 Only if these rules do not eliminate the uncertainty, may a court construe the applicable language against the drafter of the uncertain language. (Id.) “While insurance contracts have special features, they are still contracts to which the ordinary rules of contractual interpretation apply.” (Id. at p. 1264.) Ambiguity is construed against the insurer only as a last resort, after other principles of interpretation have been exhausted. (Id, at p. 1272; Civ. Code § 1654.) “If an asserted ambiguity is not eliminated by the language and context of the policy, courts then invoke the principle that ambiguities are generally construed against the party who caused the uncertainty to exist (i.e., the insurer) in order to protect the insured's reasonable expectation of coverage.” (La Jolla Beach & Tennis Club, Inc. v. Industrial Indemnity Co. (1994) 9 Cal.4th 27, 37.)
Although exclusionary clauses are strictly construed against the insurer, courts are not free to give them a strained interpretation in order to find coverage. (Id. [“ ‘courts will not adopt a strained or absurd interpretation in order to create an ambiguity where none exists.’ ”].)
There is no room under the above rules to treat an insured as being “severed” from the policy, and as having a separate insurance policy, while disregarding other provisions of the policy that impact the interpretive analysis of the policy. (These distortions of coverage are illustrated in more detail in Section IV of this brief.)
B. The Severability Clause Must Be Construed In The Context Of The Entire Policy.
Minkler argues that the severability clause should be construed to mean that the policy provides each insured with separate coverage as if each were separately insured by a distinct policy. This argument, however, ignores the fact that there is only one policy and the policy must be construed as a *7 whole. The policy itself is not physically “severed”--there is still only one policy. The rule of construction that the policy must be construed in its entirety, with each clause interpreted in relation to the others, remains in complete effect. (Bank of the West, supra, 2 Cal.4th at p. 1265; Civ. Code § 1641 [whole contract must be taken together to give effect to every part, and each clause should help interpret the others].)
An insurance policy consists of a series of words and component parts that are drafted to function as a single, integrated written instrument. Thus, the notion that the policy must be applied separately to each insured as if each insured had a separate policy is inconsistent with interpreting the policy as a whole. The idea that each insured is to be regarded as having a separate interest does not alter the rules of policy interpretation or the fact that there is still a single policy to be interpreted, not multiple policies.
The evaluation of the coverage obligations to each insured must look to the language of the policy itself, which must be interpreted as a whole and in context. In fact, the severability clause plainly does not create separate and distinct policies for each insured. The severability clause states innocuously that “[t]his insurance applies separately to each insured.” It is mistaken to argue, as does Minkler and a minority of courts across the country, that the clause creates distinct policies. It is one thing to say that the policy confers separate status to each insured, and quite another to claim that the severability clause creates two or more separate insurance policies.
The significance of the severability clause in relation to exclusions and other provisions in a policy is somewhat overstated. (See Ristine v. Hartford Insurance Co. of the Midwest (Ore.App. 2004) 195 Ore.App. 226, 97 P.3d 1206, 1209-1210 [“There is nothing in the wording of the severability provision itself that remotely suggests that it affects the *8 substance of any provisions concerning coverage or exclusions.”].) As courts in the majority have ruled, “the purpose of severability clauses is to spread protection, to the limits of coverage, among all of the... insureds. The purpose is not to negate bargained-for exclusions which are plainly worded.” (Northwest G.F. Mut. Ins. Co. v. Norgard, 518 N.W.2d 179, 183; Argent, supra, 901 A.2d at pp. 426-27.)
California's rules of policy interpretation only strengthen this argument. As a more generally worded provision, the severability clause must take a subordinate position to a more specific exclusion. It is a basic rule of contract interpretation that a specific provision controls over a more general one. (Kavruck v. Blue Cross of California (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 773, 781 [“a specific provision relating to a particular subject will govern in respect to that subject, as against a general provision, even though the latter, standing alone, would be broad enough to include the subject to which the more specific provision relates.”]; Continental Casualty Co. v. Zurich Ins. Co. (1961) 57 Cal.2d 27, 35 [“when general and specific provisions of a contract deal with the same subject matter, the specific provisions, if inconsistent with the general provisions, are of controlling force.”].)
In this case, Safeco's more specific intentional acts exclusion controls over the more general severability provision.
Furthermore, by using both “the insured” and “an insured” terminology, the policy language itself already recognizes that there is a difference between treating insureds collectively and individually. There is no need to resort to the broader severability clause to accomplish what the policy does in more direct terms.
*9 Moreover, given the distinct usage of the terms “an insured” and “the insured” in the Safeco policy, it cannot be assumed that the two terms were intended to have the same meaning. Such an interpretation blurs the distinction between those phrases and is contrary to the rule that contractual provisions may not be construed in a way that would render other provisions surplusage. “If possible, the court should give effect to every provision. An interpretation which renders part of the instrument to be surplusage should be avoided.” (Appalachian Ins. Co. v. McDonnell Douglas Corp. (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 1, 14.) “Courts must interpret contractual language in a manner [that] gives force and effect to every provision, and not in a way [that] renders some clauses nugatory, inoperative or meaningless.” (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 473.)2
The minority of judicial opinions that have adopted the “severability” approach advanced by Minkler are premised on a simplistic and flawed analysis. First, those cases describe severability as “a widely recognized doctrine that acknowledges the separate and distinct obligations the insurer undertakes to the various insureds, named and unnamed.” (See, e.g., American Nat'l Fire Ins. Co. v. Estate of Fournelle (Minn. 1991) 472 N.W.2d 292, 294). However, the analysis breaks down when the minority cases make the unfounded leap in logic that each insured must be treated as if each were *10 “separately insured with a distinct policy.” (Id,) Because there is still but a single policy, this conclusion is not tenable.
The cases in the minority falter even further when they conclude that “severability demands that policy exclusions be construed only with reference to the particular insured seeking coverage.” (Ibid.) This contention is not grounded in the language of the policy. For example, nothing in the Safeco policy equates “an insured” with “the insured making a claim for coverage.” The policy says no such thing. Furthermore, the general language of the severability clause is hardly a proper vehicle to draw this conclusion-in effect modifying the intentional acts exclusion.
Nor does the claim that an ambiguity exists between the intentional acts exclusion and the severability clause solve the problem. An insurance policy provision is ambiguous only when it is capable of two or more interpretations, both of which are reasonable. (Bay Cities Paving, supra, 5 Cal.4th at p. 867.) However, because of its general nature, the severability clause cannot be reasonably read to mean that each insured is entitled to a distinct policy.
The cases in the minority are premised on the conclusion that an ambiguity can exist in the abstract, which is contrary to California's rules of contract interpretation. “ ‘[L]anguage in a contract must be construed in the context of that instrument as a whole, and in the circumstances of that case, and cannot be found to be ambiguous in the abstract.’ ” (Bank of the West, supra, 2 Cal.4th at p. 1265.) The differences between the severability clause and the words “an insured” in the intentional acts exclusion can do nothing more than create an abstract ambiguity, which is not enough to allow the former to trump the latter.
*11 By comparison, the fact that the Safeco policy differentiates between the term “the insured” (which is used 39 times in the policy) and “an insured” (which is used 96 times) demonstrates that when the policy seeks to treat insureds in a collective sense, it uses the phrase “an insured.” It also shows that the policy does not utilize the severability clause to narrow the treatment of specific insureds.
Grammatically, “an insured” is an indefinite article that describes a larger set of insureds in a collective sense, than “the insured.” (Allstate Ins. Co. v. Freeman (Mich. 1989) 432 Mich. 656, 698 [“ ‘[a]’ or ‘an’ is an indefinite article often used in the sense of ‘any’ and applied to more than one individual object; whereas ‘the’ is an article which particularizes the subject spoken of.”)3
There is simply no need to resort to the severability clause when the policy itself accomplishes the same thing by using more specific “the insured” terminology. Notably, the cases in the minority do not address the significant linguistic distinction between “the insured” and “an insured,” or how those terms are used in the context of the policy.
*12 IV. MINKLER'S PROFFERED INTERPRETATION WILL LEAD TO ABSURD RESULTS THROUGHOUT THE POLICY.
Because the Safeco policy uses the phrase “an insured” nearly 100 times, this term must be interpreted consistently throughout the policy where the same language is employed. (See Caminetti v. Pacific Mut. Life Ins. Co. (1943) 22 Cal.2d 344, 358 [words used in a certain sense in one part of an instrument are deemed to have been used in the same sense in another].)
Minkler would read the exclusion in the Safeco policy as if to state “which is expected or intended by [the insured making the claim for coverage].” Thus, Minkler would equate “an insured” with “the insured making the claim for coverage.” If Minkler is correct, then this same construction of “an insured” must be inserted wherever those words are used in the policy. This approach does not work. As explained below, Minkler's interpretation, if adopted, will lead to absurd results in various parts of the policy where the term “an insured” is used. In other words, Minkler's argument collapses under its own weight because his proffered definition is not in accord with the plain language of the policy, and does not comport with the overall purpose of homeowners policies in general, which will lead to unwarranted distortions of the entire policy.
A few examples follow.
A. Automobile. Aircraft And Watercraft Exclusions. “Typically, liability policies other than automobile policies contain an exclusion for bodily injury or property damage arising out of the use, ownership, or maintenance of an automobile.” (Windt, Insurance Claims and Disputes (5th ed. 2007) § 11:22.) The purpose of these exclusions is obvious-automobile liability risks are not encompassed by a homeowner's policy. Accordingly, Safeco's policy excludes coverage for bodily injury or *13 property damage arising out of the ownership, maintenance or use of motor vehicles, aircraft or watercraft owned or operated by or rented or loaned to an insured. (ER 76.)
Notwithstanding the plain intent of these exclusions, if “an insured” were to refer to the insured seeking coverage, as Minkler argues, these exclusions would not apply in situations where the automobile (or boat or plane) is owned or operated by another insured. These exclusions would not bar coverage for the insured who did not own, rent or operate the vehicle.
Minkler's view of the severability clause would destroy these exclusions in vicarious or negligent supervision situations even though homeowners policies are not intended to cover these kinds of risks. For example, if a parent failed to properly supervise a son or daughter living in the same household (thus, qualifying as an insured under the definition of an insured),4 and the son or daughter was involved in an auto accident while driving his or her own car, the exclusion would not bar a negligent supervision claim because the insured-parent against whom the claim was made, and who was seeking coverage, did not own, rent or operate the vehicle.
Another example would occur where an insured allowed an intoxicated spouse (a co-insured) to get behind the wheel of his or her own car and was involved in an accident. The insured seeking coverage would claim that the exclusion did not apply because he or she did not own, operate or maintain the other spouse's automobile.
*14 If adopted, Minkler's interpretation of the severability clause would create a gaping hole in the automobile, aircraft, and watercraft exclusions. The Court should reject such an unreasonable construction.
B. Non-Insured Location Exclusion.
Another example where absurd results can be expected concerns the interpretation of the “insured location” exclusion, which also uses the words “an insured.” The “insured location” exclusion excludes claims for bodily injury or property damage “arising out of any premises owned or rented to an Insured which is not an Insured location.” (ER 76, Exclusion d [emphasis added].) The purpose of the “insured location” exclusion is to prevent an insured from obtaining liability insurance for multiple properties while paying a premium calculated on the liability exposure for only one location. (Safeco Ins. Co. v. Hale (1983) 140 Cal.App.3d 347, 353.) A reasonable insured would not expect liability insurance coverage for property damage or bodily injury with respect to property which an insured neither owns, resides in or uses. (Id.)
Nevertheless, if the severability provision were to dictate that “an insured” refers only to the particular insured seeking coverage, the “insured location” exclusion would be negated in certain vicarious liability situations arising out of property owned by one spouse-insured, but not the other. The spouse-insured seeking coverage would contend that the exclusion did not apply to him or her because the property in question was not owned by the insured making the coverage claim, but by the other spouse.5
*15 C. Insured Location Definition.
In fact, the interpretation of “insured location” discussed above raises a two-edged sword because the terra is not only used in the “insured location” exclusion, it is also used in defining which properties are covered by the policy in the first place. That is, the definition of “insured location” extends coverage not only to the principal insured residence, but also to other locations, such as: (1) “premises not owned by an Insured but where an insured is temporarily residing;” (2) “vacant land owned by or rented to an insured,” or (3) “land owned by or rented to an insured on which a one or two family dwelling is being constructed as a residence for an insured” (ER 66.)
Thus, coverage for these “insured locations” turns on the interpretation of the words “an insured.” However, because Minkler's interpretation of the term “an insured” would refer only to the insured making the claim for coverage, this would narrow the scope of coverage for “insured locations.” In contrast, the interpretation proposed by Safeco would broaden coverage for “insured locations” since it would not strictly define coverage in reference to the insured seeking coverage.
Minkler cannot have it both ways and claim that the phrase “an insured” means something different in an exclusionary provision of the policy, but not elsewhere in the policy.
The point of these examples, besides illustrating that absurd results will flow from that approach, is also that an insurance policy is not truly severable, but constitutes a single integrated legal instrument. Homeowners *16 policies are not issued to disparate individuals, but to single households and relatives who reside in the same household. The Safeco policy defines “Insured” essentially to include the spouses and relatives who reside in the same household. (ER 65.) Because homeowners policies insure a residence and a household, not separate and unrelated individuals, the argument that the severability clause creates separate insurance policies for each insured is unworkable.
D. Business Exclusions.
Another exclusion found in homeowners policies is the “business” or “business pursuits” exclusion. Business exclusions typically exclude claims “arising out of or in connection with a business engaged in by an insured....” (See, e.g., Argent, supra, 901 A.2d at p. 422 [emphasis added].)
Thus, if the severability clause were interpreted to exclude coverage only for the insured seeking coverage, a claim arising out of a business owned by a different co-insured, such as a spouse or other family member, would be covered even though homeowners policies were never intended to provide coverage for business risks, and it is beyond the reasonable expectations of an insured to believe that a homeowners policy would cover business risks that are properly addressed by commercial general liability policies. Rather, a homeowner's policy is
“designed to insure primarily within the personal sphere of the policyholder's life and to exclude coverage for hazards associated with regular income-producing activities ... [which] involve different legal duties and a greater risk of injury or property damage to third parties than personal pursuits. Business activities can be insured by other types of policies. Their exclusion from personal liability policies avoids areas requiring specialized underwriting, prevents unnecessary *17 coverage overlaps, and helps keep premiums low.”
(Frazier, The Business-Pursuits Exclusion Revisited (1977) Ins. L.J. 88, 89.)6
Premiums for all policyholders would rise uncontrollably if homeowners insurance policies were suddenly construed to cover business risks. (See Luneau v. Peerless Insurance Co. (Vt. 2000) 750 A.2d 1031, 1035, 170 Vt. 442, 448 [“It is undesirable to force homeowners' policy premiums to rise to cover a major part of the business tort risk of those homeowners who conduct a business in or out of the home but choose not to purchase insurance to cover their business.”]; LeBlanc v. Broussard (La.App.3d Cir. 1981) 396 So.2d 535, 536 [“The purpose of the ‘business pursuits' exclusion in a homeowner's policy is to lower rates by removing coverage which is not essential to the purchasers and which would require specialized rating and underwriting.”]; Blue Ridge Ins. Co. v. Newman (La. 1984) 453 So.2d 554, 556 [“The apparent purpose of the ‘business pursuits' exclusion from personal liability coverage in a homeowner's policy is to eliminate the high cost of insuring the risks attendant to commercial enterprises, thereby making homeowner's insurance available at lower rates.”]; Grossman v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co. (Minn. App. 1990) 461 N.W.2d 489, 496 [“Premiums for homeowner's policies would be inflated unreasonably if the homeowner's insurance pool were required to assume risks attendant upon commercial ventures....”].)
*18 A differential application of the typical business exclusion as proposed by Minkler would result in coverage being indirectly afforded to business ventures. For example, if a wife operated a daycare center out of the family residence and the husband-insured was accused of molesting one of the children under the wife's care, the husband would claim that the business exclusion did not apply to him because he did not own or operate the business-rather the wife did.
The correct interpretation of the severability clause in the context of a business exclusion is illustrated by the New Jersey case of Argent v. Brady, supra, 901 A.2d 419, where the homeowner operated a daycare center out of her home. The homeowner's son's dog bit a child to whom the homeowner was providing daycare. After the son was sued, he argued that the business exclusion did not apply to him because he was not involved in operating the business. Even though there was no evidence that the son, who owned the dog, was engaged in the daycare center business operated by his mother, the court correctly held that the business exclusion applied to the son, “as it was doubtlessly intended, to exclude from coverage any liability resulting from the business pursuits of a covered person, regardless of whether the person sued was engaged in those business pursuits.” (Id. at p. 424.)
Construing the policy language as proposed by Minkler would mean that the typical business exclusion in homeowners policies would not apply unless the insured against whom the claim was made owned or operated the business that led to the liability claim, even though it is clear that the business exclusion was intended to exclude all business-related claims against all insureds. Minkler's proffered interpretation would not be consistent with the insured's reasonable expectations.
*19 This also illustrates that the contention that the policy can be “severed” and each insured treated as if having a separate, discrete policy is artificial and fallacious. Homeowners' policy premiums would skyrocket if each insured under a homeowners policy were treated as if having a separate policy-as if no other insured existed or mattered. This undermines the basic purpose of homeowners policies and is inconsistent with the insured's reasonable expectations.
V. CALIFORNIA COURTS OF APPEAL HAVE CORRECTLY INTERPRETED POLICIES REFERRING TO “AN INSURED” OR “ANY INSURED” SO AS TO EXCLUDE COVERAGE FOR ALL INSUREDS IF ANY ONE INSURED ENGAGED IN THE EXCLUDED CONDUCTS.
Established California case law already correctly holds that where an intentional acts exclusion bars coverage for the act of “an insured” or “any insured,” there is no coverage for any insured regardless of which insured engaged in the excluded activity. (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Altieri (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1352, 1361; Western Mutual Ins. Co. v. Yamamoto (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1474, 1486; Allstate Ins. Co. v. Gilbert (9th Cir. 1988) 852 F.2d 449, 451.) The words “an insured” or “any insured” are functionally equivalent terms and refer to all persons, named or unnamed insureds under the policy. (Allstate Ins. Co. v. Condon (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 148, 153; see also, California State Auto. Ass'n Inter-Ins. Bureau v. Warwick (1976) 17 Cal.3d 190 [“the term ‘any insured’ unmistakably refers to any person insured under the policy”].)
*20 By way of illustration, in Altieri, a homeowner's insurance policy excluded coverage for bodily injury “caused intentionally by or at the direction of an insured, or...resulting from any occurrence caused by an intentional act of an insured person.” (Altieri, supra, 235 Cal.App.3d at p. 1360.) The court of appeal held that the intentional acts exclusion barred coverage for the claims against the wrongdoing minor, as well as the claims for negligent supervision brought against his parents. In addressing the negligent supervision claim against the parents, the Altieri court held that where the policy excludes coverage for bodily injury intended or expected by “an” or “any” insured, coverage is not available for the claims against all insureds, including negligent supervision claims. (Id. at p. 1361.)
Similarly, in Yamamoto, a minor used his father's gun to shoot the victim. The father was sued for negligent supervision of his son. The policy excluded coverage for injury “expected or intended by an insured.” (Yamamoto, supra, 29 Cal.App.4th at p. 1486.) The court held that this phrase effectively excluded coverage for all claims against any insured, including claims of negligent supervision against the minor's parents.
Likewise, in Gilbert, the wife of a child molester was sued along with her husband. After determining that Insurance Code section 533 barred coverage for the claims against the husband, the court held that the negligence claim against the wife was also not covered because the policy contained an exclusion for “[b]odily injury or property damage which may reasonably be expected to result from the intentional or criminal acts of an insured person or which are in fact intended by an insured person.” (Gilbert, supra, 852 F.2d at p. 451.) The court concluded that the term “an insured” referred to all insureds under the policy, and that excluding coverage for injury or damage intentionally caused by “an insured person” unambiguously excluded coverage *21 for damages caused by the intentional wrongful acts of any insured. (See also, Medill v. Westport Insurance Corp. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 819, 832 [“California cases have determined that the phrases ‘any insured’ or ‘an insured’ unambiguously preclude coverage to all persons covered by the policy if any one of them engages in excludable conduct.”])
By comparison, in American States Ins. Co. v. Borbor (9th Cir. 1987) 826 F.2d 888, 894-895 the policy excluded coverage for intentional acts of “the insured,” and the court held that the wife of a molester was entitled to coverage for negligent supervision of her husband, co-owner of a childcare center.
Minkler concedes he has “no quarrel” with the Altieri line of cases. (Opening Brief, p. 2.) Instead, he argues that the severability provision should alter this result. However, the reasoning of the Altieri line of cases is sound and should not be disturbed. While these cases admittedly did not consider the effect of a severability clause, one can surmise that the reason for this is that these courts did not view such a provision as having the effect of modifying the intentional acts exclusion. It must be assumed that the policies in those cases contained severability clauses, but that none of these courts viewed the severability clause as sufficient to negate the intentional acts exclusion or to change their conclusion that the words “an insured” or “any insured” should be read to defeat coverage for derivative liability claims.
VI. THE DRAFTING HISTORY OF THE SEVERABILITY CLAUSE SUPPORTS SAFECO'S INTERPRETATION. The drafting history of the severability clause supports the conclusion that it was not intended to modify the meaning of exclusions referring to “an insured” or “any insured.” This drafting history can properly *22 be considered by this Court. “[T]he presence of standardized industry provisions and the availability of interpretive literature are of considerable assistance in determining coverage issues. Such interpretive materials have been widely cited and relied on in the relevant case law and authorities construing standardized insurance policy language.” (Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Admiral Ins. Co. (1995) 10 Cal.4th 645, 670-671 [citations omitted].)
The history of the severability clause demonstrates that it was only intended to address instances where the policy used the term “the insured.” This history was described in Department of Transp. & Pub. Facilities v. Houston Casualty Co. (Alaska 1990) 797 P.2d 1200. In his concurring opinion, Justice Matthews of the Alaska Supreme Court explained that the severability of interests clause, which became operative in 1955, was intended to clarify that the term “the insured” in an exclusion refers merely to the insured claiming coverage. In the absence of a severability clause, insurance policies were deemed ambiguous in using the term “the insured” in an exclusion. (Id.) As a result, a number of courts had found there was an ambiguity and that the term “the insured” had to refer only to the particular insured who was seeking coverage. (Id.)
The term “the insured,” without further qualification, could be regarded as a class term, collectively embracing all who might qualify within the term. If so construed the result would be to exclude coverage for all insureds if coverage is excluded for any one of them. That is not the intent. Accordingly, the definition of “insured” specifically states that the insurance afforded applies separately to each insured against whom claim is made or suit is brought. The one exception is with respect to limits of liability. It is not intended that the limits apply separately to each insured, and that reservation is stated not *23 only in the “insured” definition but also in the limits of liability provision.
(George H. Tinker, Comprehensive General Liability Insurance - Perspective and Overview (Spring 1975) 25 Fed'n Ins. Couns. Q. 217, 237-238.)
In using the term “the insured,” there was no intent to exclude coverage for all insureds if the policy excluded coverage for one insured. (Id.)
On the other hand, a different conclusion was reached where policies used “an insured” or “any insured” terminology. As the court in Bituminous Cos. Corp. v. Maxey (Tex. App. 2003) 110 S.W.3d 203 noted:
“If the exclusion clause uses the term ‘the insured,’ application of the separation of insureds clause requires that the term be interpreted as referring only to the insured against whom a claim is being made under the policy.... If, however, the exclusion clause uses the term ‘any insured,’ then application of the separation of insureds clause has no effect on the exclusion clause; a claim made against any insured is excluded. To hold that the term ‘any insured’ in an exclusion clause means ‘the insured making the claim’ would collapse the distinction between the terms ‘the insured’ and ‘any insured’ in an insurance policy exclusion clause, making the distinction meaningless.”
(Id. at p. 214 [citations omitted].)
The policy interpretation advanced by Safeco in this case is consistent with the drafting history of the severability clause, and is thus useful in interpreting the policy.
VII. CONCLUSION.
The Court's interpretation of the severability clause and the term “an insured” will impact not just the intentional acts exclusion in Safeco's policy, but other parts of standard homeowners policies, not to mention general liability policies which use similar language. If the severability clause and the exclusion are construed to refer to the insured “making the claim for coverage” as Minkler argues, then such an interpretation must be consistently and reasonably applied throughout the policy. However, as discussed above, that interpretation falls apart when it is applied in other parts of the policy where those terms are used. Since the term “an insured” appears 96 times in the Safeco policy, the Court cannot be assured that the interpretation proffered by Minkler will be in conformity with its intended usage in all parts of the policy. This is not possible under the policy construction advanced by Minkler. The only reasonable construction of the intentional acts exclusion is the one proposed by Safeco, which is that the exclusion applies to all insureds when any one insured engages in the excluded conduct.
