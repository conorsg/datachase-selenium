*3 INTRODUCTION
This Court has emphasized the principle that while insurance contracts may have special features, they are contracts first and foremost and contract law governs them.
“While insurance contracts have special features, they are still contracts to which the ordinary rules of contractual interpretation apply. [Citations.] The fundamental goal of contractual interpretation is to give effect to the mutual intention of the parties. [Citation.] Such intent is to he inferred, if possible, solely from the written provisions of the contract. [Citation.] If contractual language is clear and explicit, it governs. [Citation.]” (Foster-Gardner vs. National Union Fire Ins. (1998) 18 Cal.4th 857, 868.)
*4 “SECTION II - CONDITIONS” in Safeco's policy contains a “Separation of Insureds” or “Severability of Interests” provision in paragraph 2 thereof. (Vol. 2, Excerpts of Record, p. 78, hereafter 2 ER 78.)
“2. Severability of Insurance. This insurance applies separately to each insured. This condition will not increase our limit of liability for any one occurrence.”
It is a policy “condition,” so an individualized construction and interpretation of the entire policy is required. This includes the application of an exclusion to a coverage grant. The effect of such paragraph is that coverage is provided for each insured as though it were the only insured. With an interpretation that David and Betty each have their own “policy,” exclusions which might apply to him under his cannot apply to her under hers.
This follows for two reasons: (1) Safeco's placement of the Clause in Section II -- Conditions part of its policy means that it is a contractual rule of construction for the entire policy, including the Section II -- Liability Coverages part of the policy and its correlative exclusions; and (2) As a matter of California contract law, Civil Code §14311 provides Safeco's obligations are several to each insured.
*5 The parties have not focused on this aspect of the case, and instead deal with insurance law theories as if the Section II -- Conditions part of the policy must be reconciled or construed with the Section II -- Liability Coverages part. Applicant submits that is not the case: Section II Conditions apply to all Section II Liability Coverages.
ARGUMENT
A. The Severability Clause Creates Separate Policies.
Policy conditions are not the same as coverage parts of a policy; they impose conditions on coverage. “Every insurance policy imposes conditions on coverage provided by the policy. . . ¶ As a general rule, conditions neither confer nor exclude coverage for a particular risk . . . .” (Croskey, et al., 1 Cal Practice Guide: Insurance Litigation (Rutter Group 2009) §3.158, p. 3-47.)
Such sections in a policy provide what rules or procedures the parties have agreed to utilize when a claim arises. “Conditions are terms of the policy that explain some of the responsibilities the parties have to each other and govern the manner in which the claim is administered under the policy.” (2 Calif. Liability Insurance Practice (CEB 2009) §3.27, p. 90.) They “do not define coverage but they can affect the coverage provided by the policy.” (Ibid.)
*6 The insurance industry introduced such provisions into automobile and general liability policies in 1955. Prior thereto, the clause was unnecessary because most business general liability policies only insured the named insured and those persons integrally connected to the business, as long as they were acting within the scope of their business duties. But there was an increased demand for additional insured status, i.e., in 1955, real estate managers were also added as additional insureds under most policy forms. (Malecki, Ligeros and Gibson, The Additional Insured Book (5th Ed. 2004), p. 138.)
Their purpose was to eliminate the confusion resulting from courts attempting to interpret policies having multiple insureds when the insurer applied an exclusion was to an “innocent” insured (whose own conduct was not excluded) who was seeking benefits under the policy.
“Commentators indicate that severability of interest clauses were inserted in standard forms for various liability policies in the mid-1950s in order to make ‘it clear and certain that the named insured and the omnibus or additional insureds are to be treated separately, and that the exclusions or other coverage tests should apply to the particular insureds seeking coverage.’ [Citations.]” (Ryder Truck Rental vs. St. Paul F. & M. Ins. (1982) 540 F.Supp. 66, 72-73.)
“Severability of interests provisions were adopted by the insurance industry to define the extent of coverage afforded by a policy issued to more than one insured. [Citation omitted] Where a policy contains a severability of interests clause, it is a recognition by the insurer that it has a separate and distinct obligation to each insured under the policy. . . .” (Sacharko vs. Center Equities Ltd. Ptp. (Conn.App. 1984) 479 A.2d 1219, 1222.)
*7 An additional purpose of the clause was to “discourage insureds from requesting separate policies or endorsements that had the effect of incorporating wording that signified that the policy was several and not collective.” (Malecki, supra, The Additional Insured Book at p. 138.)
Thus it is a “policy provision clarifying that, except with respect to the coverage limits, insurance applies to each insured as though a separate policy were issued to each.” (Glossary of Insurance and Risk Management Terms (9th Ed. 2004) Int'l. Risk Management Inst., p. 205.) This principle was applied to disallow an insurer from picking and choosing which of its two insureds would receive policy benefits. (Shell Oil vs. National Union (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1633, 1644-1645.)
When an insured is sued, the insurer's first step is to compare complaint with policy provisions. (Montrose Chemical Corp. vs. Superior Court (1993) 6 Cal.4th 287, 293: “The determination whether the insurer owes a duty to defend usually is made in the first instance by comparing the allegations of the complaint with the terms of the policy.”) As a condition, the clause means once a trigger of coverage occurs, Safeco is first required to separate Betty from David in reading its policy in order to determine what coverage is available to her. The provision is a condition precedent to interpreting the policy.
*8 But conditions and promises are sometimes difficult to distinguish, and it is possible this Court will consider it a promise.
“The Restatement of Contracts, section 261, provides:‘Where it is doubtful whether words create a promise or an express condition, they are interpreted as creating a promise;...’ and Professor Williston in his work on Contracts (section 665) says thereof: ‘Such an interpretation protects both parties to the transaction and also does not involve the consequences that a slight failure to perform wholly destroys all right under the contract.’ ” (Div. Labor Law Enf. vs. Ryan Aero. Co. (1951) 106 Cal. App. 2d Supp. 833, 836.)
If so, then Safeco promised that the “insurance [for Betty] applies separately to each [of her and David].” And like any other promise, she is entitled to enforce it.
B. Civil Code §1431 Provides Betty's Rights Are Several.
Section 1431 (at note 1, ante) specifies that “a right created in favor of several persons, is presumed joint, and not several... This presumption, in the case of a right, can be overcome only by express words to the contrary.” Safeco's clause does that.
In Petroleum Midway vs. Moynier (1928) 205 Cal. 733, a multiparty lease in question stated “Each well shall be drilled wholly on one lot, and the owner of that lot shall be the only participant in the royalty from such well, ....” (Id. at 738.) This Court held such language overcame the presumption of a joint right in all lessees and that the rights were several. (Id. at 740-741, 744.)
*9 In May vs. Nevada Irrigation District (9th Cir. 1979) 600 F.2d 1280, a water rights contract provided a party would sell “water for irrigation purposes only... for each acre or fraction thereof irrigated or to be irrigated of the lands owned by (the landowners) at the date hereof.” (Id. at 1282.) The court held this created several rights within §1431 's exception. “Under the agreement water was to be allocated to each landowner on a per acre basis. It follows that the parties intended that each landowner would be dealt with individually. Moreover, as the district court observed, it seems highly unlikely that one landowner could be held responsible for payment if another failed to pay for his water.” (Id. at 1283.)
It is hornbook insurance law that all insurance policies issued in California are “governed by the statutory and decisional law in force at the time the policy is issued. ‘Such provisions are read into each policy issued thereunder, and become part of the contract with full binding effect upon each party.’ ” (Inter Ins. Exch. vs. Ohio Casualty (1962) 58 Cal.2d 142, 149.) Section 1431 's provisions regarding joint or several rights has been on the books since 1872, and is a part of Safeco's policy.
Safeco's language is clear that Betty's rights are separate from David's and are not conditioned on his acts or omissions to act. Had Safeco wished to condition one insured's rights to coverage based on the acts of another insured, it could said so. But this Court, facing a similar argument from Safeco, stated it is inappropriate to rewrite its policy.
*10 “As we have seen, that definition is inappropriate here because it rewrites the policy by inserting what has been omitted. Drawing a distinction, as the Court of Appeal did, between negligent acts and grossly negligent acts exceeds the bounds of construction by rewriting the policy. The word ‘illegal’ is not a synonym for ‘gross negligence:’ ” (Safeco Ins. vs. Robert S., supra, 26 Cal.4th 758, 765.)
In plain English, Safeco is arguing that the first sentence of the clause be interpreted as if it read: “This insurance applies separately to each insured, but not when applying any exclusions.” Rewriting its policy here is as wrong as it was in Robert S.
CONCLUSION
A “condition” in a policy is a private rule of construction to which the parties agreed. As a condition precedent to the insurer's analysis of any claim when there are multiple insureds, or as a promise, the insurer is required to read its one policy as if it were duplicated into as many separate policies as there are insureds claiming coverage. It must then interpret each of those policies for that particular insured without regard to the facts applicable to any other insured. This private rule of construction mandates each insured is covered or excluded separately.
*11 While the language of Safeco's exclusion won't change, still “an” insured will only reference Betty because under “her” policy,2 she is the only insured. Thus any facts applicable to David are immaterial and cannot be used as to her “separate” policy. Neither Betty nor David is deemed “any insured” as respects one another.
