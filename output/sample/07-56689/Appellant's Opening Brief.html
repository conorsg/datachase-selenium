PRELIMINARY STATEMENT
This appeal involves the construction of a homeowner's insurance policy. The plaintiff and appellant is Scott Minkler, who is suing as the assignee and judgment creditor of Betty Schwartz, against whom he holds a judgment in excess of $5 million. The judgment is based on Schwartz's negligent failure to stop her adult son, who was Minkler's baseball coach, from sexually molesting Minkler when he was a young teen.
Defendant and appellee Safeco Insurance Company is Schwartz's insurer. It declined to defend or indemnify both Schwartz and her son in Minkler's underlying lawsuit against them. It relied on the intentional-acts exclusion in its policy, which bars coverage for the intentional acts of “an” insured. Since Schwartz's son resided with her, he was an insured under the policy, and therefore Safeco maintained that Schwartz had no coverage, even though there was no allegation of any intentional conduct on her part.
This Circuit has already determined, consistent with California law, that an exclusion that bars coverage for the acts of “n” insured is effective to bar coverage for all insureds under the policy. Allstate Ins. Co. v. Gilbert, 852 F.2d 449, 453 (9th Cir. 1988); Fire Ins. Exch. v. Altieri, 235 Cal.App.3rd 1352, 1360, 1 Cal.Rptr.2d 360 (1991). Minkler has no quarrel with this rule.
But the issue presented in this case is whether that rule is operative when the policy contains a “severability-of-insurance clause,” which states that, “This insurance applies separately to each insured.” The Safeco policy at issue here contains such a clause.
Courts across the nation have split on the effect of a severability clause on exclusions that apply to all the insureds collectively, such as exclusions referring to the acts of “an” insured or “any” insured. Some have held that severability clauses make the policy ambiguous;1 others have taken the contrary view.2 Since this case is controlled by California law, the key issue is how the California Supreme Court has, or would, rule on the issue.
To date, it has not considered the issue. And there is only one published appellate decision to do so, California Cas. Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co, 48 Cal.App.4th 1682, 1695-1697, 56 Cal.Rptr.2d 434 (1996) ( “Northland ”). Northland concluded, in dictum, that the severability clause in that policy would not have an impact on the exclusion for the acts of “an” insured.
But in the concurring portion of his concurring and dissenting opinion in Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Robert S., 26 Cal.4th 758, 778, 110 Cal.Rtpr.2d 844 (2001), Justice Baxter of the California Supreme Court pointed out several flaws in the Northland analysis, and explained that under California's rules of insurance-policy construction, a severability clause would create an ambiguity when juxtaposed against an exclusion withdrawing coverage for the acts of “an” insured. This is because the clause would indicate to the policyholder that any claim for coverage would be treated as if there was only one insured, a view that is at odds with an exclusion that speaks to conduct by “an” or “any” insured.
In ruling on Safeco's motion to dismiss Minkler's case, the district court elected to follow Northland. Finding that Safeco's policy afforded no coverage, it granted the motion and dismissed Minkler's case. Minkler contends this was error because the district court's analysis, though thorough, did not apply the fundamental rule that, in California, insurance policies must be construed in the way a lay policyholder with no legal training or expertise in insurance would understand them.
Justice Mosk of the California Supreme Court once admonished that, “It is not easy for lawyers and judges to read a legal document as a layperson would, but we must make the effort.” (Weil v. Federal Kemper Life Assurance Co., 7 Cal.4th 125, 156-157 (1994)(Mosk, J., dissenting.) Few, if any of the cases that have held that a severability clause has no impact on collective exclusions reflect any attempt to undertake that effort.
Instead of asking “How would an average layperson read this policy in light of these terms?,” they ask a different question: “What does this policy mean?” To answer that question, these cases describe the purpose of such clauses, trace the insurance industry's use of the clauses dating to the 1950s, examine the placement of the clauses in the policy, and rely on various legal doctrines the courts employ to construe contracts - all of which would be far beyond the ken of the ordinary lay insured.
These decisions may be correct in jurisdictions that are less protective of policyholder rights than California, but because they fail to analyze the issue under the rules a California court is required to apply, they reach the wrong result. By contrast, Justice Baxter's concurring opinion focuses on the key issue - how the insured would be likely to understand the coverage provided by the policy in light of the severability clause. Because Justice Baxter's approach is the most consistent with the way that the California Supreme Court would approach and decide the issue, the district court should have adopted it, and denied Safeco's motion.
STATEMENT OF JURISDICTION
A. Federal subject-matter jurisdiction
This lawsuit was filed in the California Superior Court in May 2007, and removed by Safeco on July 5, 2007, based on diversity of citizenship. 1 ER 2. Minkler is a California resident; Safeco is a Washington corporation with its principal place of business in Washington. 2 ER 26. Minkler's complaint originally named Patricia Orris, a Safeco employee, as a defendant. 2 ER 24. But after Safeco filed its motion to dismiss, the parties stipulated to dismiss her from the action. 1 ER 2. Jurisdiction in the district court was proper under 28 U.S.C. § 1332 because the parties were diverse and the amount in controversy exceeded $75,000.
B. Appellate jurisdiction
After granting Safeco's motion to dismiss on October 16, 2007, the district court entered a judgment dismissing the action the same day. 1 ER 2. Minkler filed a timely notice of appeal on November 13, 2007. 2 ER 19. This Court has jurisdiction to hear an appeal from a final judgment of the district court. 28 U.S.C. § 1291(a).
ISSUE PRESENTED
Whether the California Supreme Court would be likely to hold that Safeco's insurance policy is ambiguous because of an inconsistency between the severability-of-insurance clause and the intentional-acts exclusion, which refers to the acts of “an” insured.
STATEMENT OF FACTS
A. Factual summary
In 2003 Minkler filed a lawsuit in the California Superior Court against Betty Schwartz and her son, David Schwartz. 1 ER 2. Minkler alleged that in 1987 and 1988, when he was a minor, he was sexually abused by David Schwartz, who was his baseball coach. 1 ER 3. Some of the acts occurred in the home of Betty Schwartz, where David Schwartz lived. Id. The complaint alleged claims against David Schwartz for, inter alia, sexual battery. Id. A negligence claim was asserted against Betty Schwartz. Id.
During the time the incidents of abuse occurred, Betty Schwartz was insured by Safeco under a series of homeowner's policies; 1 ER 3. In February 2004, David Schwartz tendered the defense of the action to Safeco, acting on his own behalf and on behalf of his mother. Id.
Safeco denied the tender, citing the fact that the Safeco policies excluded claims for bodily injury “... which is expected or intended by an insured or which is the foreseeable result of an act or omission intended by an insured.” 1 ER 2-3; 2 ER 92. Safeco asserted that David Schwartz was an insured under the policy, and that his intentional acts caused Minkler's damages. 1 ER 4; 2 ER 92. Accordingly, it denied coverage (both an indemnity and a defense) under the Intentional Acts Exclusion. 1 ER 4.
Minkler subsequently took a default judgment against Betty Schwartz for $5,020,612.20. 1 ER 4. He then obtained an assignment of rights against Safeco. Id. Based on his status as the assignee and judgment creditor of Betty Schwartz, Minkler filed this action against Safeco for breach of contract and tortious breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Id.
B. Relevant policy provisions
Safeco concedes that the relevant terms in the series of policies that it issued to Betty Schwartz were the same. 2 ER 24. The 1993 policy, which was one of the policies attached to Minkler's complaint in this action, is included in the excerpts of record as an exemplar. 2 ER 61-89.
The policy defines an “insured” in this way:
“Insured” means you and the following residents of your household:
a. your relatives;
b. any other person under the age of 21 who is in the care of any person named above. 2 ER 65.
The liability coverage in the policy is in Section II. Coverage E is titled “Personal Liability.” 2 ER 74. The insuring clause promises that, “If a claim is made or a suit is brought against an insured for damages because of bodily injury or property damage caused by an occurrence to which this coverage applies, we will: (1) pay up to our limit of liability for the damages for which the insured is legally liable; and (2) provide a defense at our expense by counsel of our choice....” 2 ER 74-75.
The intentional-acts exclusion is contained in the Section II exclusions. It provides:
1. Coverage E - Personal Liability ... [does] not apply to bodily injury and property damage: (a) which is expected or intended by an insured or which is the foreseeable result of an act or omission intended by an insured.” 2 ER 76.
The Section II Conditions portion of the policy contains the following relevant provisions:
1. Limit of Liability. Our total liability under Coverage E for all damages resulting from any one occurrence will not exceed the limit of liability for Coverage E stated in the Declarations. This limit is the same regardless of the number of insured, claims made or persons injured.
2. Severability of insurance. This insurance applies separately to each insured. This condition will not increase our limit of liability for any one occurrence. 2 ER 78.
PROCEDURAL SUMMARY
Minkler filed this action in the California Superior Court in May 2007. 2 ER 26. Safeco removed it based on diversity of citizenship on July 5, 2007. Id. Although the complaint named Patricia Orris, a Safeco employee who resides in California, Safeco argued in its removal notice that since she was a Safeco employee, she was a sham defendant. 2 ER 27. Orris was dismissed by stipulation. 1 ER 2.
Safeco then filed its 12(b)(6) motion, arguing that its policy provided no coverage to Betty Schwartz, as a matter of law, and therefore Minkler could not state any claim against it based on his status as her judgment creditor or assignee. On October 16, 2007, the district court granted the motion, issuing a 17-page written order. 1 ER 2-18.
The district court noted that courts had split on the effect of a severability clause, and that the divergent outcomes reflect, in large measure, different views regarding the purpose of such a clause. 1 ER 12. Some courts found that the clause was designed to ensure that each insured receive the full benefit of coverage limits; while others found that the clause requires the insurer to consider each insured in isolation when evaluating coverage. 1 ER 13.
The court noted that even under this broader view, it does not inexorably require that collective exclusions be applied only to the insured whose conduct gives rise to the claim. Id. The court cited a 1990 unpublished memorandum disposition by this Court, in Employer's Mutual Co. v. G.D., 894 F.2d 409, 1990 WL 4854 (9th Cir. Jan. 24, 1990), decided under Montana law, which held that a severability clause did not have an impact on an exclusion pertaining to an exclusion relating to claims that “an insured” sexually molested a child at a day-care center.
The district court then noted that the history of the use of severability clauses in the insurance industry supported this view. 1 ER 14. It also noted that because the clause was placed in the Conditions portion of the policy, and not in the portion of the policy relating to coverage, its function was intended to be procedural, not substantive, and that a reasonable person would understand this. 1 ER 15.
Finally, the court noted that an interpretation of the policy that rendered some terms ambiguous or superfluous should be avoided, and that the severability clause could not be read to do this. 1 ER 16.
STATEMENT OF THE STANDARD OF REVIEW
This Court reviews a dismissal pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) for failure to state a claim de novo. Navarro v. Block, 250 F.3d 729, 731-732 (9th Cir. 2001). The meaning and interpretation of an insurance contract is a question of law reviewed de novo. HS Services, Inc. v. Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co., 109 F.3d 642, 644 (9th Cir. 1997), citations omitted.
A Rule 12(b)(6) motion tests the legal sufficiency of a claim. A claim may be dismissed only if “it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief.” Navarro v. Block, supra, citing Conley v. Gibson, 355 U.S. 41,45-46, 78 S.Ct. 99,2 L.Ed.2d 80 (1957); and Cahill v. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co., 80 F.3d 336, 338 (9th Cir. 1996).
Minkler is cognizant that the “no set of facts” standard drawn from Conley v. Gibson was abrogated by the Supreme Court in Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 127 S.Ct. 1955, 1968 (2007), at least to the extent that it could be read to permit a plaintiff to survive a motion to dismiss whenever the pleadings “left open the possibility that a plaintiff might later establish some ‘set of [undisclosed] facts' to support recovery.” Id., brackets in original. Since Minkler is not relying on some undisclosed facts that might support his claim, the Bell Atlantic decision does not apply in this case.
SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT
Courts across the United States have split on whether a severability clause like the one in Safeco's policy renders ambiguous an exclusion that applies to the policy's insureds on a collective basis. As explained above, one view holds that a severability clause communicates to policyholders that the policy's coverage will be determined as if they were the only insured under the policy. See, e.g., Worcester Mut. Ins. Co. v. Marnell, 398 Mass. 240, 496 N.E.2d 158, 159 (1986). Juxtaposed with an exclusion withdrawing coverage for the acts of “an” insured, these Courts find the policy ambiguous, and therefore will find coverage. Under this view, there would be coverage for Minkler's claims here.
The contrary view holds that a severability clause is neither intended to, nor communicates to an insured that a clear exclusion in the policy, which bars coverage for the acts of “an” or “any” insured, means anything other than what the exclusion says. Hence, these courts find no ambiguity, and enforce such collective exclusions as written, regardless of the severability of coverage clause. See, e.g., Chacon v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co., 788 P.2d 748, 752 (Colo.1990).
The issue in this appeal is how the California Supreme Court - which has not addressed the issue - would rule. The only published authority from California consists of a concurring opinion by Justice Baxter of the California Supreme Court, in Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Robert S., 26 Cal.4th 758, 778, 110 Cal.Rtpr.2d 844 (2001), and the decision in California Cas. Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co., 48 Cal.App.4th 1682, 1695-1697, 56 Cal.Rptr.2d 434 (1996) ( “Northland”).
Justice Baxter's concurring opinion advances the view that under California's rules for the interpretation of insurance policies, which focus on the manner in which the insured would read and understand the policy, the severability clause renders the policy ambiguous. Northland, decided five years before Justice Baxter considered the question, reached the opposite conclusion.
Minkler believes that the decisions that take the Northland approach fail to give sufficient attention to the issue of how a lay insured, who possesses no legal training or special understanding of insurance, would understand the policy. Because Justice Baxter's concurrence does address this issue, and because the California Supreme Court as presently constituted is very protective of policyholders' rights, Minkler believes that the Supreme Court would be likely to adopt Justice Baxter's approach.
This Court may wish to certify the question to the California Supreme Court, for a definitive resolution.
ARGUMENT
A. Controlling California principles of policy interpretation
In a diversity action the substantive law of the forum state - here, California - controls. See, e.g., Homedics Inc. v. Valley Forge Ins. Co., 315 F.3d 1135, 1138 (9th Cir. 2002). “Where the state's highest court has not decided an issue, the task of the federal courts is to predict how the state high court would resolve it.” Ticknor v. Choice Hotels Intern., Inc., 265 F.3d 931, 939 (9th Cir. 2001), citing Dimidowich v. Bell & Howell, 803 F.2d 1473, 1482 (9th Cir.1986), modified at 810 F.2d 1517 (9th Cir.1987). The decisions of the state's intermediate appellate courts are data that a federal court must consider in undertaking this analysis. Air-Sea Forwarders, Inc. v. Air Asia Co., Ltd., 880 F.2d 176, 186 (9th Cir.1989).
Although insurance contracts have special features, they are still contracts and are therefore subject to California's ordinary rules of contract interpretation. Conestoga Services Corp. v. Executive Risk Indemnity, Inc., 312 F.3d 976, 981 (9th Cir. 2002). As with any contract, the fundamental goal of contractual interpretation is to give effect to the mutual intent of the parties. Id. This intent is to be inferred, if possible, solely from the written provisions of the policy contract. PMI Mortg. Ins. Co. v. American Intern. Specialty Lines Ins. Co., 394 F.3d 761, 764 (9th Cir. 2005).
The terms of the policy are interpreted in the context of the policy as a whole, with each clause helping to interpret the other. Palmer v. Truck Ins. Exchange, 21 Cal.4th 1109, 1115, 90 Cal.Rptr.2d 647 (1999).
An insurance policy is ambiguous when it is capable of two or more constructions, both of which are reasonable. Conestoga Services Corp., 312 F.3d at 982. Ambiguity in an insurance policy can result from contradictory or inconsistent provisions in the policy. Elwood v. Aid Ins. Co., 880 F.2d 204, 208 (9th Cir. 1989). “Even language that appears to be ‘plain and clear’ may be ambiguous when read in the context of the policy as a whole and the circumstances of the case.” Croskey, Heeseman, & Popik, California Practice Guide - Insurance Litigation (Rutter 2007 rev.) § 4:30, (“Insurance Litigation”).
California follows a three-step approach to resolve claimed ambiguities in insurance policies. First, the court looks to the “clear and explicit” meaning of the terms at issue, used in their “ordinary and popular sense.” Second, if this step does not resolve the issue, the disputed terms must be interpreted in the sense the insurance company reasonably believed the insured understood them when the policy was issued; i.e., in accordance with the insured's “objectively reasonable expectations.” Finally, if the ambiguity remains, it is construed against the party who caused it to exist - generally the insurer. In re K F Dairies, Inc. & Affiliates, 224 F.3d 922, 925 (9th Cir. 2000).
The rules become more stringent when a court is construing policy terms that limit the scope of promised coverage. PMI Mortg. Ins. Co. v. American Intern. Specialty Lines Ins. Co., 394 F.3d at 765 n. 5. Exclusionary clauses are strictly construed, while coverage clauses are interpreted broadly so as to afford the greatest possible protection to the insured. HS Services, Inc. v. Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co., 109 F.3d at 645.
Once reasonable alternative constructions of a provision are presented, establishing that it is ambiguous, the Court does not decide which one is the most reasonable. PMI Mortg. Ins. Co. v. American Intern. Specialty Lines Ins. Co., 394 F.3d at 765, n.5. Hence, if any reasonable interpretation of the policy would result in coverage, a court must find coverage even if other reasonable interpretations would preclude coverage. Bodell v. Walbrook Ins. Co., 119 F.3d 1411, 1413 (9th Cir. 1997).
“The policy should be read as a layman would read it and not as it might be analyzed by an attorney or an insurance expert.” Id., citing Crane v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Co., 5 Cal.3d 112, 95 Cal.Rptr. 513, 514 (1971); Haynes v. Farmers Ins. Exchange, 32 Cal.4th 1198, 1209, 13 Cal.Rptr.3d 68 (2004).
B. Under these principles, the California Supreme Court would be likely to find that Safeco's policy is ambiguous
1. The nationwide split of authority on the impact of severability clauses
In California, if an exclusion refers to acts of “the” insured, and there are multiple persons insured under the policy, the exclusion will be construed to refer only to the acts of the particular insured seeking coverage. See, e.g., State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Jacober, 10 Cal.3d 193, 202, 110 Cal.Rptr. 1 (1973). The Jacober court reached this result because it viewed the exclusion as ambiguous in the context of the policy. Other jurisdictions have reached the same result, but have based their conclusion on the presence of a severability clause, concluding that the clause creates an ambiguity because it suggests that the coverage applies separately to each insured. See, e.g., Sacharko v. Center Equities Ltd. Partnership, 2 Conn.App. 439, 444, 479 A.2d 1219, 1222 (1984).
As the court in Michael Carbone, Inc. v. General Acc. Ins. Co., 937 F.Supp. 413, 418 (E.D.Pa., 1996), explained, “[T]he vast majority of jurisdictions which have addressed the issue ... hold that the severability doctrine or a separation of insureds clause modifies the meaning of an exclusion phrased in terms of ‘the insured.”’ Id. (collecting cases).
The consensus breaks down, however, if the exclusion is drafted with reference to the acts of ”an” insured, or “any” insured. In Wisconsin, for example, the courts find that a severability clause will render an exclusion referring to the intentional acts of “an” insured ambiguous, but not an exclusion referring to the acts of “any” insured. Compare Northwestern Nat. Ins. Co. v. Nemetz, 135 Wis.2d 245, 256, 400 N.W.2d 33, 38 (App., 1986)(”an insured” ambiguous); with Taryn E.F., by Grunewald v. Joshua M.C., 178 Wis.2d 719, 505 N.W.2d 418, 420 (App., 1993) (severability language did not prevail over clause excluding coverage for malicious acts of “any” insured).
a. The cases that find ambiguity
The courts that have held that a severability clause renders exclusions that refer to the acts of “an” or “any” insured ambiguous generally apply the analysis relied on by the Wisconsin Court of Appeals in Nemetz: “We conclude that this contract is ambiguous because the severability clause creates a reasonable expectation that each insured's interests are separately covered, while the exclusion clause attempts to exclude coverage for both caused by the act of only one.” Id., 135 Wis.2d at 256.
Similar analysis appears in Worcester Mut. Ins. Co. v. Marnell, 398 Mass. 240, 496 N.E.2d 158, 159 (1986), one of the most widely-cited cases by courts that take this view of severability clauses. In Marnell, the parents' homeowners policy included their son as an insured. The policy excluded coverage for liability arising from the operation or ownership of a motor vehicle by “any” insured, but also contained a severability clause. Id. at 159. The son held a party at his parents' house, and served alcohol. While driving some friends home after the party, he stuck and killed a pedestrian. The parents were sued on a theory of negligent supervision. Citing the motor-vehicle exclusion, the carrier denied coverage. The trial court held that the carrier was required to defend. The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court affirmed.
The court conceded that the exclusion, standing alone, would bar coverage for the parents. Id., 496 N.E.2d at 160-161. But the court held that the severability clause changed this result, because it “requires that each insured be treated as having a separate insurance policy.” Id., at 161. As a result, “the term ‘insured’ as used in the motor vehicle exclusion refers only to the person claiming [liability] coverage under the policy.” Id. Because the parents did not own or operate the car involved in the accident, the exclusion did not bar coverage for the claim against them. Id.
The Marnell court conceded that its construction of the policy could be seen as rendering the term “any” in the phrase “any insured” superfluous, but it believed that this was preferable to the insurer's construction, which would render the entire severability clause meaningless. Id.
Courts adopting a similar view include American Nat. Fire Ins. Co. v. Fournelle Est., 472 N.W.2d 292, 294 (Minn.1991)(holding that severability clause required coverage for claims of minor children who were insured as household residents despite household-resident exclusion for “any” insured); State Farm Fire & Cas. Ins. Co. v. Keegan, 209 F.3d 767, 768-770 (5th Cir.2000)(under Texas law, where husband-wife homeowners policy with severability clause defined additional insured to include “member of your household” and excluded bodily injury coverage for “an” insured, husband, who had moved from marital household, was covered for negligent injury to child who remained with wife as household resident); and Brumley v. Lee, 265 Kan. 810, 963 P.2d 1224 (1998) (holding that because a severability provision gives each insured separate coverage, a homeowners policy clause excluding liability coverage for intentional acts by “any” insured was ambiguous and did not bar coverage for an insured husband, sued on grounds he negligently failed to prevent his coinsured wife from inflicting an intentional, and fatal, blow upon a child.)
b. The cases rejecting that view
An equal or greater number of cases take a contrary view. Their decisions are grounded on a variety of reasons. Some cases rely on the fact that the particular exclusion at issue is very specific and clearly communicates that the policy will not provide coverage for the type of claim at issue, under any circumstances. A good example of this approach is Northwest G.F. Mut. Ins. Co. v. Norgard, 518 N.W.2d 179, 181 (N.D.1994).
There, the policyholders were a husband and wife who operated a day-care center. They were sued by the parents of a child who attended the center, whom they alleged had been molested by the husband. The issue in the case was whether the policy would cover claims against the wife, who was not alleged to have been involved in the improper activity, but who negligently allowed it to occur.
The court considered the conflicting authorities on the effect of a severability-of-insurance clause, and concluded that on the facts of the case before it, there was no ambiguity created by the interplay between the policy's exclusion and the severability clause because of the wording of the exclusion. This was because the policy in question contained additional coverage for the day-care center for an additional premium, and it also contained a sexual-molestation exclusion that withdrew coverage for sexual molestion “inflicted upon any person by or at the direction of an insured, an insured's employee, or any other person involved in any capacity in the day care enterprise.” Id., 518 N.W.2d at 180.
The court explained that, “Determinative here is the unique language of this exclusion; the exclusion does not pertain only to the acts of an insured, but also to the acts of ‘an insured's employee or any other person involved in any capacity in the day-care enterprise.” Id. at 183. The court concluded that this clause was clear on its face that the policy would provide no coverage for child molestation “where anyone connected with the operation of the day-care center commits an act of sexual molestation on one of the children.” Id.
In the court's view, the specificity of this clause, which was tailored to withdraw any coverage for sexual molestation occurring in the day-care operation, regardless of who was the perpetrator, would control over a more general provision concerned with who is covered. Id. at 183. Since the parties expressly contemplated that there would be no coverage for sexual molestation, the court found no ambiguity and no coverage.
Other courts simply reject outright the idea that the effect of a severability clause is to require that each insured be treated as if they were the only insured. For example, in the view of the Kentucky Court of Appeals in National Ins. Underwriters v. Lexington Flying Club, Inc., 603 S.W.2d 490, 492 (Ky.App. 1979), “The purpose of severability clauses is to spread protection, to the limits of coverage, among all of the named insureds.” It is not, under this view, “to negate bargained-for exclusions which are plainly worded.” (Id.)
A majority of the courts that reject the view that severability clauses make collective exclusions ambiguous simply do not see any ambiguity, and conclude that the Marnell approach re-writes the policy by eliminating “any” or “an” from the exclusion. See, e.g., United Fire & Casualty Co. v. Reeder, 9 F.3d 15, 18 (5th Cir.1993); Chacon v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co., 788 P.2d 748, 752 n. 6 (Colo.1990); State Farm Fire & Cas. Co. v. Guccione, 171 Ill.App.3d 404, 525 N.E.2d 595, 596-97 (1988); American Family Mut. Ins. Co. v. Moore, 912 S.W.2d 531, 534-535 (Mo.App.1995); and Taryn E.F., by Grunewaldv. Joshua M.C., 178 Wis.2d 719, 505 N.W.2d 418, 420-21 (App. 1993).
2. California authorities considering the impact of severability clauses
a. Northland and Bjork
The California Supreme Court has not considered the issue. Two published California decisions have discussed it, California Cas. Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co., 48 Cal.App.4th 1682, 1695-1697, 56 Cal.Rptr.2d 434 (1996) ( “Northland”), and Bjork v. State Farm Fire and Cas. Co., 157 Cal.App.4th 1, 11, 68 Cal.Rptr.3d 405, 413-414 (2007).
Bjork is easily dispensed with because it did not actually take a position on the issue. Instead, it noted the conflicting approaches and concluded that regardless of how the severability issue might be resolved, the policy would nevertheless not provide coverage. Id., 157 Cal.App.4th at 11, n. 11.
Northland, did, however, reach a conclusion, albeit in dictum. It concluded that the decisions rejecting the view that severability clauses create ambiguity that precludes the enforcement of collective exclusions are more persuasive, relying most heavily on Norgard. More specifically, Northland concerned an exclusion for liability arising from the operation of personal watercraft. The policyholders, identified in the opinion as “Mr. and Mrs. Joe Harmer” were operating a Waverunner watercraft on the Colorado River, when they collided with and seriously injured Memorie Yessian, who was operating a Jet Ski. Id., 48 Cal.App.4th at 1687, 1688. The Harmers had 3 policies, but only the coverage with California Casualty (“CCIC”) is relevant here.
CCIC defended the Harmers in the underlying action under a reservation of rights. It then filed a declaratory-judgment action against them and Yessian seeking to establish that it had no coverage. The CCIC policy expressly excluded liability arising from the use of a watercraft with inboard motor power, such as the Waverunner. Id. at 1687. The trial court granted summary judgment for CCIC, rejecting the policyholders' claim that the exclusion did not apply to a Waverunner because it was powered by a jet pump, and not a propeller. Id. at 1689. The policyholders appealed, and the Court of Appeal affirmed.
The court first held that the exclusion for watercraft with inboard motor power applied to the Waverunner, which all parties conceded has an inboard engine - that is, an engine mounted inside the craft's hull. Id. at 1691.
The court then turned to the policyholders' contention that even if the watercraft exclusion barred coverage for Mr. Harmer, CCIC had a separate duty to cover Mrs. Harmer, who was also an insured under the policy. Id. at 1695. They noted that under California law, Mrs. Harmer's community-property interest could be held liable for a judgment against Mr. Harmer. Because of this, they reasoned that such liability would not arise from Mrs. Harmer's use of the watercraft, but her liability for Mr. Harmer's torts under community- property law. Id.
The Northland court was not receptive to this argument. First, it noted that nothing in the complaint against the Harmers indicated that Yessian sought to hold Mrs. Harmer liable on any theory not based on the ownership or use of the watercraft. The complaint, after all, alleged that both Mr. and Mrs. Harmer owned the Waverunner, and that each was operating and controlling it at the time of the accident. Id.
Next, the court found that a spouses' community-property interest could not provide an independent basis for insurance coverage. Id. Here, the court relied on the cases explaining that exclusions that withdraw coverage for claims arising from “an” insured's activities generally bar coverage for anyone insured under the policy; not just the insured involved in the accident. Id. at 1696.
Finally, having already disposed of the issue of coverage, the court reached the contention that the severability-of-coverage clause in the policy operated to require that coverage for each insured be considered independently, as if each has a separate policy. The court noted that there were no California cases in point, and that the courts in other jurisdictions had split on the issue. Id., 48Cal.App.4th at 1696.
The Northland court stated that it found the decisions rejecting the view that the severability clauses created coverage more persuasive than those on which Yessian relied. Id.48 Cal.App.4th at 1697. The court relied heavily on Norgard, noting:
The court in Norgard acknowledged but rejected all three of Yessian's authorities. (518 N.W.2d at p. 182.) It reasoned that a clause excluding liability for specific conduct should prevail over a more general severability provision. It also noted the purpose of severability clauses is to afford each insured a full measure of coverage up to the policy limits, not to negate bargained-for and plainly-worded exclusions. Id., at p. 183. Northland, 48 Cal.App.4th at 1697.
The Northland court then explained that it reached the same conclusion, “at least in the context of this case, in which coverage is urged on the basis of the community properly laws.” Id at 1697, emphasis added. This was because the court feared that acceptance of Yessian's position would effectively nullify exclusions from coverage in any case involving married coinsureds and a policy with a severability provision. Id. at 1797-1708. The court explained:
A spouse could always demand a defense based on potential liability of his or her community property interest, regardless of whether the act giving rise to the liability was excluded. A spouse could make that demand even where, as here, he or she was expressly alleged to have participated in the tortious act. It is inconceivable that parties to a policy would include clauses specifically excluding coverage for claims based on certain types of conduct, but intend those exclusions to have no effect in any case involving claims against coinsured spouses. Id., 48 Cal.App.4th at 1698.
b. Justice Baxter's concurrence in Safeco v. Robert S.
In Robert S., the policyholder's son shot and killed his friend. The son was convicted in juvenile court of a felony for the shooting. The victim's family sued the parents and their son for wrongful death. Safeco denied coverage based on an exclusion in the policy for “illegal acts.” A five-justice majority of the California Supreme Court held that the exclusion was unenforceable because it purported to bar coverage for any act that violated any provision of California law, including the provision in the Civil Code which forms the basis for negligence liability in California, Civil Code section 1714.
Justice Baxter and Justice Brown did not join the majority. In their view, the illegal-acts exclusion should be enforced to bar coverage for the son who had pulled the trigger. Accordingly, they dissented on this basis to the extent the majority held that the policy provided coverage for the son.
But they also believe that the severability-of-insurance clause in Safeco's policy (which is identical to the clause at issue here) operated to create coverage for the parents, who did not commit the wrongful fact, but who may have negligently allowed it to occur.
Justice Baxter canvassed the cases that have held that a severability clause demanded that coverage be construed only with reference to the particular insured seeking coverage. Id., 26 Cal.4th at 774. He discussed more than the three cases identified in Northland, including several decided in the five years after Northland had been decided. Id. He also noted that several courts had reached the contrary conclusion. Id. at 775, n.3.
Justice Baxter then noted that in California, only Northland, had discussed the issue. He noted that the Northland court there saw no indication that the wife's liability was vicarious only, in light of the allegations in the underlying complaint that she jointly owned, operated, and controlled the watercraft at the time of the accident. Id., 26 Cal.4th at 776.
He then examined the Northland court's discussion of the severability issue, and distinguished the case in this way:
The severability discussion in [Northland] is arguably dictum, since the Court of Appeal appeared to believe the exclusion applied directly to the wife by virtue of her personal conduct. Moreover, it is unclear the extent to which the court's narrow construction of the severability provision stemmed from its particular concern that exclusionary clauses might be nullified in actions against the community for the torts of a single spouse. Such concerns are not present here.” Id. at 776.
He noted that any liability on the parents for the shooting death was not merely derivative or vicarious; it was premised on an entirely separate, independent theory of negligent supervision and control. Id. For this reason, he explained that he was persuaded by the cases that hold that the effect of a severability clause “... is to extend both the policy's coverage, and its exclusions, individually to each insured, as if he or she were the only insured, subject to policy limits.” Id. at 776, 777, emphasis in text.
He added that, under this approach, “exclusions from coverage are personal and may not be imputed from one insured to another, even where, as here, language internal to an exclusionary clause, viewed in isolation, could be read to withdraw coverage from all insureds for the excludable conduct of one.” Id. at 776, 777, emphasis in text.
Where Justice Baxter's view differs materially from the cases that have held that severability clauses have no impact on exclusion, is that it is grounded on California's rules for insurance-policy construction. Id. at 777. In particular, he focused on the reasonable expectations of the insured, who would conclude based on reading the severability clause that the policy apply only to him or her, and who would not conclude that by allowing their coverage to extend to a child, it would reduce the insurance available to them for their own negligent acts. Id. at 778.
3. The California Supreme Court would be likely to adopt Justice Baxter's approach because it is the most consistent with California's approach to insurance-policy construction
A term in an insurance policy is ambiguous when it is subject to more than one reasonable interpretation. The Tenth Circuit put the issue at hand quite succinctly in West American Ins. Co. v. AV&S, 145 F.3d 1224, 1229 (10th Cir. 1998), when it predicted that the Utah Supreme Court would find that a severability clause created coverage, “Because we find the relationship between Separation of Insureds clause and the Auto Exclusion clause to be ambiguous at best and capable of two different interpretations....”
Minkler would concede that the existence of conflicting judicial opinions on the impact of a severability clause does not automatically establish ambiguity. Northland made this point, noting that “even if we assume the exclusion may be ambiguous in some factual contexts, it does not follow it is ambiguous in all contexts.” Id., 48 Cal.App.4th at 1694. Interestingly, this observation was made in the context of its discussion of whether the Waverunner at issue had an inboard motor - not in the context of its discussion of the severability clause.
Most of the cases that discuss the impact of a severability clause arise on similar facts; that is, one insured does something to generate liability and the issue is whether the other insureds, who did not commit the excluded act, but who stand to be liable in some way as a result of it, have coverage. It is therefore difficult to brush aside the conflicting decisions as the result of different factual contexts.
It is also difficult to conclude that the courts that have found an ambiguity - including the highest courts of Massachusetts, Kansas, and Minnesota, are not only wrong, but so wrong that their construction does not even rise to the level of reasonable.
Because this case presents an issue of insurance-policy construction, the question this Court must answer is not whether the courts that have concluded that severability clauses have no impact have the better argument; it is whether an insured - with no legal training or expertise with insurance - would be likely to understand how to reconcile the statement in the policy that the coverage applies separately to each insured, with an exclusion for the acts of “an” insured.
Few, if any, of the cases actually analyze the issue from the standpoint of a lay insured. The court in Norgard did, explaining that “We doubt that a layperson would agree that the choice of articles [between “the” insured and “an” insured] alone renders either exclusion free from ambiguity.... We decline to base our construction of the sexual molestation clause on the distinction between the articles.”
But for the most part, the cases apply the tools that lawyers and judges use to resolve knotty legal issues, as the district court did here. It examined the historical use of severability clauses dating from the 1950s, and the insurer's intent in including them in the policy. It looked at the policy's structure, to determine that a severability clause was intended to be “procedural” and not “substantive” because it was contained in the part of the policy titled “Conditions.” It examines the insurer's purpose in including the clause. Regardless of how acurate or scholarly this analysis was, it does not reflect a lay insured's reading of the policy.
Whatever one might think of the California Supreme Court's recent approach to insurance-policy construction, it cannot be denied that the Court has consistently been very protective of the rights of policyholders.
Its 2001 decision in Safeco v. Robert S., held that an “illegal acts” exclusion did not preclude coverage for a shooting, even though the shooter had pleaded guilty to a felony. Two years later, in MacKinnon v. Truck Ins. Exchange, 31 Cal.4th 635, 648, 3 Cal.Rptr. 2d 228 (2003), the Court held that the standard pollution exclusion could not be broadly construed to cover all “releases” of toxic chemicals. Hence, the Court held that the pollution exclusion did not bar coverage for the spraying of a pesticide, even though the Court conceded that the literal terms of the exclusion applied to the claim. Id., 31 Cal.4th at 653.
Next came E.M.M.L Inc. v. Zurich American Ins. Co., 32 Cal.4th 465, 472, 9 Cal.Rptr.3d 701, 707 (2004), which held that a theft exclusion in a policy requiring a courier to be “in or upon” a vehicle at the time of a theft in order for the loss to be covered did not bar coverage where the courier was outside of the vehicle, standing behind it and examining its rear bumper.
On the heels of E.M.M.I came Haynes v. Farmers Insurance Exchange, 32 Cal.4th 1198,1204,13 Cal.Rptr.3d 68 (2004), which stressed that language limiting coverage had to displayed in the policy in a way that was clear and conspicuous to the policyholder, and must also be phrased in terms that were unmistakable. Haynes held that limitations on the amount of uninsured-motorist coverage that the policy provided did not meet these standards and were therefore unenforceable.
Most recently, the Supreme Court decided TRB Investments, Inc. v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co., 40 Cal.4th 19, 26, 50 Cal.Rptr.3d 597, 602 (2006), another decision protecting the insured's expectations of coverage. The policy there withdrew coverage for buildings that were vacant, but provided that a building that was “under construction” was not considered vacant. The appellate court adopted the insurer's position - that the reference to “under construction” did not apply to a building that was being renovated, but only to new construction. The Supreme Court reversed, finding that the term “under construction” contemplated all building endeavors, whether new construction or renovation, that required substantial and continuing presence of workers at the premises. Id.
These decisions show that the California Supreme Court consistently applies the rule that, “insurance coverage is interpreted broadly so as to afford the greatest possible protection to the insured, [whereas] exclusionary clauses are interpreted narrowly against the insurer.” TRB Investments, Inc., 40 Cal.4th at 27.
The application of that principle here makes it very likely that should the Supreme Court be called on to consider the impact of a severability-of-insurance provision, it would adopt Justice Baxter's view, which focuses less on what is the “correct” solution to the issue, and more on how a lay policyholder would read the policy.
C. This Court should consider certifying the question to the California Supreme Court
Rather than attempting to predict how the California Supreme Court would rule on the issue presented in this appeal, it may make more sense for this Court to ask the Supreme Court to decide the issue. Rule 8.548 of the California Rules of Court permits a United States Court of Appeals to request that the California Supreme Court decide a question of state law, on two conditions: (1) if the decision could determine the outcome of the matter pending in the circuit court; and (2) there is no controlling precedent.
Those conditions are met here. This appeal presents a pure issue of insurance coverage, decided on a motion to dismiss. The case therefore turns on a legal issue, with no fact finding necessary to reach an ultimate resolution. Second, there is no controlling California precedent. Northland is the sole published California decision, and even its discussion was dictum. The issue is plainly important to the parties to this appeal, but also is broadly applicable to all California insureds who have liability coverage, since severability clauses are routinely included in liability policies.
According to the authors of the Rutter Treatise on Ninth Circuit practice, there is no fixed time or procedure for making a request to this Court to certify a question to a State Supreme Court. Goelz and Watts, California Practice Guide: Federal Ninth Circuit Civil Appellate Practice (Rutter 2008) § 6:405. Rather, the authors state that the request can be made at any time. Id. They note that such requests seldom acted on by a motions panel, but instead are routinely resolved by the merits panel. Id. For that reason, they suggest that one approach to making such a request is to include it in a party's brief, as Minkler has done here. Id. at § 6.406.
Should this Court be inclined to certify the question, Minkler stands ready at the Court's request to provide a draft order requesting certification.
CONCLUSION
If it were to consider the question, the California Supreme Court would likely conclude that the severability clause in Safeco's policy renders the policy's intentional-acts exclusion ambiguous. The order dismissing Minkler's action should therefore be reversed, and this action should be remanded. This Court may wish to certify the question to the California Supreme Court for decision.
