*1 PRELIMINARY STATEMENT
Minkler has no quarrel with the first 13 pages of Safeco's brief. He concedes that, absent the severability-of-insurance clause in the policy, that Safeco could have no duty to defend Betty Schwartz in the underlying action.
Minkler does, however, take issue with Safeco's attempt to show that the California Supreme Court would be more likely to adopt its position concerning the impact of the severability clause than the one articulated by Justice Baxter in his concurring and dissenting opinion in Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Robert S., 26 Cal.4th 758, 778, 110 Cal.Rtpr.2d 844 (2001).
In his opening brief, Minkler sought to show that cases that have come down on Safeco's side of this split of authority have relied on a policy- construction approach that was not consistent with the approach taken by California courts. In particular, the courts tended to construe the policy without regard to how a lay policyholder would read the language in question.
Safeco's response does not show that Minkler's analysis was incorrect. Indeed, although it cites 15 cases from other jurisdictions that support its view, it only points to one of those cases as even taning into account the way a reasonable policyholder would construe the policy language. In sum, Safeco relies on the number of cases it can marshal, without attempting to show that they decided the issue the way a California court would.
*2 Safeco also notes that it can point to two California appellate decisions that have gone its way: Calfornia Cas. Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co., 48 Cal.App.4th 1682,1695-1697, 56 Cal.Rptr.2d 434 (1996) (“Northland”) and the unpublished decision in Sitthiso v. Farmers Ins. Exch., 2002 WL 31151628 (copy attached to Safeco's appellee's brief as Exh. A.) It argues that since these decisions were rendered by California courts, they necessarily employed California's rules of insurance-policy construction.
But in his opening brief, Minkler showed that Northland did not actually apply the standard California approach to the construction of the severability clause. Nothing in Safeco's brief suggests any inaccuracy in Minkler's discussion of the Northland decision.
And the Sitthiso decision provides only the barest support for Safeco's position, since the policy language in question was different, and the entire discussion of the severability clause was dictum because the court had already concluded that the claims at issue fell outside the scope of the policy's insuring clause.
*3 ARGUMENT
A. Safeco fails to negate the central premise of Minkler's brief - that the so-called “majority” position concerning the impact of a severability-of-insurane clause has failed to apply the rules of California insurance-policy construction
1. Neither of the California cases cited by Safeco analyzes the issue from the standpoint of a reasonable lay insured
Safeco understandably places heavy reliance on Northland, which is the only published California appellate decision to consider the impact of a severability-of-insurance clause on an exclusion that refers to multiple insureds. It also cites the unpublished decision in Sitthiso.
Nothing in Safeco's discussion of Northland at pages 13 through 15 of its appellee's brief contradicts Minkler's analysis of that case. Specifically, Safeco cannot point to where the Northland court analyzed the meaning severability-of-insurance clause from the standpoint of a lay policyholder, because there is no such analysis in its decision.
The decision in Sitthiso does not materially undermine Minkler's argument or assist Safeco's. While the court did not adopt Justice Baxters view concerning the impact of a severability-of-insurance clause, it did not adopt the Northland view either. In fact, it does not even cite Northland.
But as in Northland, the court's discussion of the severability clause was dictum, because the court had already concluded that there was no coverage. Specifically, the court held that because the policyholder's conduct was *4 intentional, it could not constitute an “accident” and therefore fell outside of the policy's insuring clause. (Sitthiso, at *5.) Because there was no coverage, the court need not have even discussed the applicability of any of the policy exclusions.
Sitthiso also resembles the decision on which the Northland court placed its principal reliance, Northwest G.F. Mut. Ins. Co. v. Norgard, 518 N.W.2d 179,183 (N.D.1994) (“Norgard”), because as in Norgard there was a specific exclusion in the policy for sexual molestation. In Norgard, the court explained that this exclusion made it clear that, regardless of any other general language in the policy, that there would be no coverage for any claim arising out of sexual molestation at the insured child-care center.
In Sitthiso, the policy not only contained a general “intentional-acts” exclusion, but it also contained a specific exclusion for child molestation by “any” insured. (Id. at *6.) The court explained that, in view of this exclusion, and prior California law holding that an exclusion referring to the acts of “any” insured was clear and unambiguous, the severability-of-insurance clause did not create an ambiguity in the application of the child-molestation exclusion. (Id. at *8, citing California State Auto. Ass 'n. Inter-Ins. Bureau v. Warwick (1976) 17 Cal.3d 190, 195 [holding that an exclusion referring to the act of “any” insured was not ambiguous].)
*5 The Sitthiso court did refer to and describe Justice Baxter's concurring and dissenting opinion in Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Robert S., 26 Cal.4th 758, 778,110 Cal.Rtpr.2d 844 (2001). (Id. at *7, 8.) But it did not apply his approach to the policy before it. In particular, the court made no finding that a lay insured would understand that a severability clause would result in coverage being analyzed as if he or she were the only insured.
Instead, the court noted that the exclusion at issue in Robert S. (as here) referred to the acts of “an insured,” while the exclusion before the court referred to the acts of “any” insured. (Id. at *8.) The court noted that Justice Baxter's dissent explained that the term “an insured” would be construed in the singular, like “the insured;” whereas “any” insured has a plural connotation. (Id., at *7.)
The court said that, in light of Warwick's holding approving an exclusion referring to the acts of “any” insurer, it could not find that the severability clause overcame the language of the exclusion before it. (Id. at *8.)
Sitthiso does not show that in a case like this one, where there is no specific exclusion relating to the particular conduct on which liability is based, and where the general intentional-acts exclusion refers to the acts of “an” insured, that Justice Baxter's analysis of the impact of a severability clause should not control.
*6 2. Safeco can only point to a single non-California decision that applied the California policy-construction approach
Safeco notes that 15 published cases from other jurisdictions have decided that severability clauses do not trump collective exclusions. (Appellee's brief at 17, n. 20.) But it does not discuss the analysis employed in these cases. It cites a single sentence from one of these 15 cases, Chacon v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co., 788 P.2d 748, 752 (Colo. 1990), where the court states that policy construction employs an objective analysis, focusing on what a reasonable person would understand the contract to mean. (Appellee's brief at 22, n. 23.) But beyond this sentence, no analysis of any of these cases is provided.
Safeo therefore concedes Minkler's contention that the cases that adopt Safeco's approach fail to apply California's approach to insurance-policy interpretation, which focuses on how a lay insured, with no special expertise or understanding of insurance, would reconcile the policy language at issue.
Safeco also argues that because there are more courts that have adopted its view than have rejected it, the California Supreme Court would be likely to adopt the majority position. But simply tallying up the number of cases on each side of the issue, without examining whether the analysis employed by the majority is consistent with California's approach to insurance-policy *7 construction, does not yield an accurate prediction about how the California Supreme Court would decide the issue.
B. The other arguments made by Safeco do not show that the California Supreme Court would be likely to adopt its position
Safeco first argues that the “history and intent” behind the severability- of-insurance clause supports its position. This argument fails to address Minkler's point that a lay insured would have no awareness of the history of these clauses, or the insurer's intent in including them. Instead, a lay policyholder would simply read the clause and attempt to reconcile it with the other terms of the policy.
Safeco next argues that because the severability clause is contained in the “conditions” section of the policy, and not in the provisions that detail the policy's coverage, a reasonable insured would not be likely to conclude that the severability clause would modify the coverage clauses. Minkler does not believe that this argument realistically describes how a lay policyholder, with no special training in how to construe an insurance policy, would approach the issue. A lay insured would be likely to read the severability clause as meaning that the policy applies to him or her as if they were the only insured under the policy - in other words, so that the conduct of another person insured under the policy would not vitiate their coverage. (Safeco v. Robert S., 26 Cal.4th at 776, Baxter, J., dissenting.)
*8 Safeco's next argument is that because the 5-justice majority in Safeco v. Robert S. did not join Justice Baxter's dissent, they would be unlikely to adopt the views expressed therein. In making this argument Safeco overlooks the dynamics of the decision in Robert S. There, the majority held that the policy's “illegal acts” exclusion was ambiguous, and therefore the policy provided coverage to both Robert S. - the boy who accidently shot his friend - as well as to Robert's parents.
Justice Baxter and Justice Brown disagreed. They found that the shooting was within the scope of the illegal-acts exclusion, and therefore the policy provided no coverage for Robert. But they also found that the severability-of-insurance clause would operate to provide coverage for his parents.
The majority therefore had no reason to disagree with the portion of Justice Baxter's concurring and dissenting opinion that reached the same conclusion about coverage for Robert's parents as the majority did.
Finally, Safeco argues that the majority approach it espouses is more in tune with California's rules of insurance-policy construction than Minkler's approach, because Minkler's approach renders the term “an insured” meaningless. What this argument misses is that Safeco's approach has the same vice. By applying the exclusion for the intentional act of”an” insured to all *9 insureds collectively, it reads the severability clause out of the policy. See, e.g., Worcester Mut. Ins. Co. v. Marnell, 398 Mass. 240,496N. E.2d 158, 161 (1986) [holding that it was preferable to render the term “any insured” superfluous and to preserve the meaning of the severability clause, than to give “any” insured meaning and read the entire severability clause out of the policy].
CONCLUSION
Justice Baxter's approach is the most consistent with the manner in which California courts construe insurance policies, and is therefore likely to foreshadow how the California Supreme Court would decide the issue. The order dismissing Minkler's action should therefore be reversed, and this action should be remanded. This Court may wish to certify the question to the California Supreme Court for decision.
