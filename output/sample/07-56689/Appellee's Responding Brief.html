PRELIMINARY STATEMENT
This is an insurance bad faith suit arising out of an underlying case in which Scott Minkler (“Minkler”) claimed that David Schwartz sexually molested him and that David Schwartz's mother, Betty Schwartz, was negligent in failing to stop that alleged molestation (“the Underlying Action”). Safeco Insurance Company of America (“Safeco”), Betty Schwartz's homeowner's insurance carrier, denied Betty Schwartz a defense against the Underlying Action on the basis of an exclusion in Safeco's policies for harm intentionally caused by “an insured.” After obtaining a default judgment against Betty Schwartz, Minkler sued Safeco as a judgment creditor and as an assignee of Betty Schwartz's potential claims against Safeco. The trial court granted Safeco's motion to dismiss.
On appeal, Minkler and Safeco agree that the sole issue before this Court is the proper interpretation of the “Severability of Insurance” clause (“the Severability Clause”) in Safeco's policies and how that clause affects the exclusion in those policies for harm intentionally caused by “an insured” (“the Intentional Acts Exclusion”). The parties also agree that, absent the Severability Clause, which states that “[t]his insurance applies separately to each insured,” Safeco would have had no duty to defend Betty Schwartz in the underlying action and that Minkler would have no basis for recovery against Safeco in the present action. However, the parties disagree on how the Severability Clause affects that analysis.
Minkler argues that the Severability Clause should negate the application of the Intentional Acts exclusion in cases, such as the Underlying Action, where an insured is sued for negligently failing to prevent the intentional act of another insured. As support for that position, Minkler cites a concurring opinion signed by two California Supreme Court Justices, only one of whom is still on the Court. Minkler also argues that all of the cases that have issued rulings supporting Safeco's position have failed to apply California's laws regarding policy construction.
It is Safeco's position that the Severability Clause does not affect the application of the Intentional Act Exclusion in cases such as the one presently before the Court. As support for its argument, Safeco relies on the Northland decision, which is a California Appellate opinion with a ruling in accord with Safeco's position, and the only published California opinion discussing the effect of a Severability Clause on a coverage exclusion based on the actions of “an insured.” Safeco also relies upon an unpublished California case that is factually identical to the current case; cases from the majority of the jurisdictions to rule on this issue; and the history and policy rational behind including the Severability Clause clauses in liability policies. Finally, Safeco clarifies that, contrary to Minkler's argument, several courts that have ruled on the issue presently before this Court have applied California's laws of policy construction and issued rulings consistent with Safeco's position.
Minkler's argument is simply untenable given the lack of support for his position and the abundance of support for Safeco's. This is especially true in light of the Northland decision, which this Court is required to follow in the absence of convincing evidence that the California Supreme Court would issue a contrasting ruling. In re Bartoni-Corsi Produce, Inc., 130 F.3d 857, 861 (9th Cir. 1997). Because no such evidence exists, this Court should hold that the Severability Clause did not require Safeco to defend Betty Schwartz in the Underlying Action, and affirm the District Court's decision to dismiss Minkler's complaint with prejudice.
JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT
A. Subject Matter Jurisdiction
This action was originally filed in Los Angeles Superior Court on May 10, 2007.1 Safeco removed the action to Federal District Court for the Central District of California on July 10, 2007, based on diversity of citizenship under 28 U.S.C. § 1332.2 Minkler is a citizen of California, while Safeco is a corporation that is incorporated in Washington and has its principal place of business in that state. While the complaint originally named claims adjuster Patricia Orris, a citizen of California, as a defendant, Safeco asserted that Orris was named as a fraudulent “sham” defendant, and the parties stipulated to dismiss her from the action.3 Accordingly, the complete diversity requirement of 28 U.S.C. § 1332 is met in this matter. Because Minkler is seeking in excess of $75,000 in recovery, the amount in controversy requirement of 28 U.S.C. § 1332 is also satisfied.
B. Appellate Jurisdiction
The District Court granted Safeco's motion to dismiss Minkler's complaint on October 16, 2007, and the Court entered the judgment that same day.4 On November 13, 2007, Minkler timely filed notice of the present appeal. Accordingly, this court has proper appellate jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1291(a).
ISSUE PRESENTED
Is there convincing evidence that the California Supreme Court would hold that the Severability Clause in Safeco's policy required Safeco to defend Betty Schwartz against the Underlying Action, given that California law is clear that, absent that clause, Safeco would have had no such duty?
STATEMENT OF FACTS
In 2003, Minkler filed a complaint against David Schwartz and his mother, Betty Schwartz.5 The complaint alleged that over the course of two years, David Schwartz sexually molested Minkler, and that the molestation occurred in the home in which Betty and David Schwartz lived.6 The complaint contained several causes of action against David Schwartz, including battery and intentional infliction of emotional distress, and a single claim of negligence against Betty Schwartz. The claim against Betty Schwartz was based on the theory that she failed to take reasonable steps to stop the molestation from occurring.7
At all relevant times, Safeco insured Betty Schwartz under a series of homeowners insurance policies.8 David Schwartz qualified as an insured under those policies because he lived in Betty Schwartz's home.9 Safeco's policies required Safeco to defend an insured against actions alleging bodily injury or property damage, subject to certain exclusion.10 Among those was the Intentional Acts Exclusion, which excluded coverage for:
‘bodily injury’ ...which is expected or intended by an insured or which is the foreseeable result of an act or omission intended by an insured ...”11
David and Betty Schwartz tendered the defense of Minkler's complaint to Safeco under the relevant homeowners insurance policies. After a thorough investigation, Safeco denied both claims on the basis that the alleged harm was intentionally caused by “an insured,” namely David Schwartz, and that the Intentional Act Exclusion therefore precluded coverage for all insureds.12
Minkler subsequently obtained a default judgment against Betty Schwartz in the amount of $5,020,612.20.13 In exchange for a covenant not to execute on his judgment, Betty Schwartz assigned to Minkler any claims that she may have against Safeco, including potential claims for breach of contract and breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.14
On May 10, 2007, Minkler filed the present action against Safeco Insurance Company.15 Minkler's theory of liability is based on the Severability Clause, a condition in Safeco's policies which states that:
This insurance applies separately to each insured. This condition will not increase our limit of liability for any one occurrence.16
Minkler acknowledges that, absent the Severability Clause when a complaint alleges that one insured intentionally caused harm, and that a second insured negligently failed to stop that harm, California law is clear that the Intentional Acts Exclusion precludes coverage for both insureds. However, he claims that under the Severability Clause, the fact that one insured committed an intentional act cannot be the basis of denying coverage to the second insured.
On July 10, 2007, Safeco filed a motion to dismiss Minkler's complaint pursuant to Rule 12(b)(6) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. In a lengthy and thoroughly reasoned opinion, the District Court granted Safeco's motion, and Minkler timely appealed.17
STANDARD OF REVIEW
The interpretation of an insurance contract is a matter of law to be reviewed de novo. Stanford University Hosp. v. Federal Ins. Co., 174 F.3d 1077, 1083 (9th Cir. 1999). However, when interpreting state law on an issue that has not been ruled upon by the state supreme court, a federal court is obligated to follow the decisions of the state's intermediate courts in the absence of convincing authority that the state supreme court would rule to the contrary. In re Bartoni Corsi Produce, Inc., 130 F.3d 857, 861 (9th Cir. 1997). As is discussed in more detail below, the California Appellate decision of California Cas. Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co., 48 Cal.App.4th 1682 (1996), is the only published California decision on-point, and that decision supports Safeco's position. Accordingly, this Court should affirm the District Court's decision in the absence of convincing evidence suggesting that the California Supreme Court would rule in favor of Minkler.
SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT
Because the only published California Appellate case on-point issued a ruling consistent with Safeco's position, this Court should rule for Safeco unless it finds “convincing evidence” supporting Minkler's position. No such evidence exists. Two California cases, one published and one not, have held that Safeco's interpretation of its insurance policy is correct. This is also true of the majority of opinions from elsewhere in the country that have ruled on the issue presently before this Court. Moreover, Safeco's position is supported by the history and policy rationale behind the inclusion of the Severability Clause in liability policies, as well as by the fact that the Severability Clause was included as a policy condition and not a coverage provision.
Minkler's presents two basic arguments in support of his position, each of which must fail. First, Minkler argues that a concurring opinion written by Justice Baxter that speaks favorably of Minkler's position proves that the California Supreme Court would rule in his favor. However, that concurring opinion was only signed by a single Justice currently sitting on the Court while four current Justices refused to sign it. If anything, this suggests that the California Supreme Court would rule in favor of Safeco, and not Minkler.
Second, Minkler argues that all the decisions from around the country that rule against him are inapplicable because they do not employ California's rules for insurance policy construction. He also accuses the District Court in this case of misapplying those rules. However, both California courts that have issued rulings favorable to Safeco utilized California's rules for policy construction, and it is clear from the text of Judge Morrow's decision in this case that she also properly employed those rules. Several cases from around the country that have issued rulings consistent with Safeco's position have also explicitly noted that they used the policy construction rules utilized by California courts. Accordingly, Minkler's arguments are without merit and this Court should affirm the decision of the District Court.
ARGUMENT
A. California Law Requires that an Insurance Contract Be Interpreted According to the Intent of the Parties.
California's law regarding insurance policy construction was succinctly described by the California Supreme Court in Bank of the West v. Superior Court, 2 Cal.4th 1254, 1264-1265 (1992). In Bank of the West, the Court noted that although insurance policies have special features, “they are still contracts to which the ordinary rules of contractual interpretation apply.” Id. at p. 1264. The fundamental goal of insurance policy interpretation, according to the Court, is to effect the intent of the parties. Ibid. If the contractual language is clear, it governs. Ibid. However, if it is not clear, a court should interpret the policy provisions in the way that a reasonable insured would have understood the provisions at the time the policy incepted. Id. at pp. 1264-1265. Finally, only if the previous inquiries do not clarify the intended meaning of a provision will courts resolve the dispute against the insurer. Ibid. see also AIU Ins. Co. v. Superior Court, 51 Cal.3d 807, 822(1990).
In addition to the construction canons noted by the Bank of the West court, California courts interpret insurance policies, “as a whole, with each clause lending meaning to the others.” Titan Corp. v. Aetna Cas. & Surety Co., 22 Cal.App.4th 457, 473-474 (1994). Courts “should interpret contract provisions in a manner which gives force and effect to every clause rather than one that renders clauses nugatory.” Ibid.
B. An Insurer Has No Duty to Defend its Insured When None of the Allegations Against the Insured Are Potentially Covered Under the Policy
Under California law, an insurer has a duty to defend an insured against a complaint if the alleges damages that are potentially covered by its policies. Gray v. Zurich Ins. Co., 65 Cal.2d 263, 275-277 (1966). However, the duty to defend is not unlimited; it is measured by the kinds of risks covered by the policy. Ringler Associates, Inc. v. Maryland Cas. Co., 80 Cal.App.4th 1165, 1186-1187 (2000). If the complaint does not present the potential that any covered damages could be levied against the insured, the duty to defend is not triggered. Montrose Chem. Corp. v. Superior Court, 6 Cal.4th 287, 295-297 (1993).
C. Absent the Severability Clause, Safeco Would Have No Duty to Defend Betty Schwartz in the Underlying Action.
Absent the Severability Clause, California law is clear that the Intentional Acts Exclusion in Safeco's policies would eliminate any duty to defend Betty Schwartz in the Underlying Action. Because David Schwartz lived in Betty Schwartz home at the time of the alleged abuse, he qualified as “an insured” under Safeco's policies.18 Moreover, it is clear that Minkler's allegations against him constitute “bodily injury' ... which is expected or intended by an insured...” as referenced in the Intentional Acts Exclusion. See J.C. Penney Cas. Ins. Co. v. M.K., 52 Cal.3d 1009, 1021 (1991) [noting that “[t]here is no such thing as negligent or even reckless sexual molestation. The very essence of child molestation is the gratification of sexual desire. The act is the harm. There cannot be one without the other. Thus, the intent to molest is, by itself, the same as the intent to harm”]. Because the alleged harm was intended by “an insured,” Safeco had no duty to defend Betty Schwartz even though she was personally only alleged to have acted negligently. Allstate Ins. Co. v. Gilbert, 852 F.2d 449, 454 (9th Cir. 1988); Fire Ins. Exch. v. Altieri, 235 Cal.App.3d 1352, 1361 (1991).
The Gilbert decision is directly on-point. In Gilbert, the plaintiff in the underlying action alleged damages resulting from sexual molestation committed by one of the defendants. Gilbert, supra, 852 F.2d at p. 450. The plaintiffs complaint also contained allegations of negligence against the molester's wife for failing to prevent the molestation. Ibid. Allstate Insurance Company, which had issued the couple two successive homeowner's insurance policies, filed a declaratory relief action to determine its obligations to defend the two against the underlying complaint.
Allstate contended that, because its policies contained exclusions for injuries intentionally caused by “an insured,” and because the damage was intentionally caused by the husband, who was insured under the policy, the wife was also not entitled to a defense. Gilbert, supra, 852 F.2d at p. 453. The wife argued that the term “an insured” should be interpreted in the singular and not the plural, and that coverage should only be precluded for the insured that intended the harm. Id. at p. 454. The court held that, by excluding coverage for injury intended by “an insured,” Allstate unambiguously excluded coverage for any insured against allegations of intentional damage caused by another insured. Accordingly, even though the wife was only alleged to have been negligent in failing to stop the molestation, the fact that her husband intentionally committed the harm precluded any duty to defend the wife. Ibid.
Under Gilbert and similarly decided cases, Safeco had no duty to defend Betty Schwartz against the Underlying Action unless the Severability Clause somehow reinstated coverage that was eliminated by the Intentional Acts Exclusion. Appellant conceded this point before the District Court and again concedes this point on appeal.19
D. The Severability Clause in Safeco's Policies Does not Change the Fact that Safeco did not Have a Duty to Defend Betty Schwartz in the Underlying Action.
1. The Only Published California Opinion On-Point Supports Safeco's Position, As Does a Recently Decided Unpublished California Opinion.
Minkler argues that the Severability Clause negates the rule clearly spelled out in Altieri and Gilbert, namely that exclusions for harm caused by the intentional actions of “an insured” preclude any duty on behalf of the insurer to defend any insured that allegedly acted negligently in failing to prevent the intentional harm. However, the only California court to rule on the effect of the Severability Clause in a context similar to the instant action held that such a clause does not vitiate an exclusion that precludes coverage based on the actions of “an insured.” California Cas. Ins. Co. v. Northland Ins. Co., 48 Cal.App.4th 1682, 1695-1697 (1996). As the Northland court explained, “the purpose of severability clauses is to afford each insured a full measure of coverage up to the policy limits, not to negate bargained-for and plainly worded exclusions.” Id.
In Northland, two watercraft collided, seriously injuring one woman. The injured woman brought an action against the married couple that owned the other watercraft. In a subsequent declaratory relief action, one of the liable couple's insurers, CCIC, claimed that it did not have a duty to defend either the husband or the wife because of an exclusion in its policy for injuries “arising out of... ownership ... of a watercraft... by any insured” Id. at p. 1691. Based on this exclusion, the court found that the husband was not entitled to a defense of the underlying action from CCIC because the husband was clearly being sued based on damage arising out of his ownership of the watercraft. Id. at p. 1694.
The plaintiff in the declaratory relief action argued that the wife was not being sued based on her ownership of the watercraft, and that she was therefore entitled to a defense in the underlying case. Northland, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 1695. The court ruled that, even if it were to agree that the wife was not being sued based on her ownership of the watercraft, the wife was still not entitled to a defense of the underlying action. According to the court, the husband, who clearly qualified as “an insured,” was being sued based on his ownership of the watercraft, and the cited exclusion therefore eliminated any duty to defend the wife under the theory articulated in Gilbert and Altieri. Id. at p. 1696.
CCIC's policy contained a severability clause virtually identical to Safeco's Severability Clause, which stated that the policy applied “separately to each insured.” The plaintiff in the declaratory relief action argued that this clause should be read to prevent an insurer from denying a defense to the wife based on the actions of the husband, or that at the very least the clause rendered the policy ambiguous, therefore requiring a finding in the insured's favor. Northland, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 1696-1697.
The court found the policy's intent to be clear, and held that, “a clause excluding liability for specific conduct should prevail over a more general severability provision.” Id. at p. 1697. It went on to state that “the purpose of severability clauses is to afford each insured a full measure of coverage up to the policy limits, not to negate bargained-for and plainly worded exclusions.” Ibid. Accordingly, the court held that, in spite of the severability clause, the husband's ownership interest in the watercraft combined with the policies exclusion for damages arising out of ownership of a watercraft by “any insured” precluded coverage for the wife. Id. at p. 1697-1698.
Under the Northland case, the presence of the Severability Clause in Safeco's policies is irrelevant for the purposes of evaluating the effect of the Intentional Acts Exclusion. Like the plaintiff in the Northland declaratory relief action, Minkler argues that the Severability Clause in Safeco's policies prohibits an insurer from denying coverage to an insured alleged of negligently failing to prevent harm intentionally caused by another insured. However, the Northland court rejected this argument, holding that “the purpose of severability clauses is to afford each insured a full measure of coverage up to the policy limits, not to negate bargained-for and plainly worded exclusions.” Northland, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 1697. Based on the Northland decision, this Court should hold that, notwithstanding the presence of the Severability Clause in Safeco's policies, Safeco had no duty to defend Betty Schwartz against the Underlying Complaint, and should affirm the District Court's dismissal of this action.
Safeco's argument is further supported by an unpublished California Appellate opinion with facts that are identical to the present case. Sitthiso v. Fire Ins. Exch., 2002 WL 31151628. (A copy of the Sitthiso decision is attached as Exhibit “A” to Safeco's Responding Brief.)
In Sitthiso, a woman sued her uncle for molesting her, and sued her aunt for, among other things, negligently failing to prevent that molestation. Id at p. 1. The insurer denied coverage to the uncle based on an exclusion almost identical to the Intentional Acts Exclusion in Safeco's policy, and denied coverage to the aunt based on the same exclusion because the complaint only alleged harm intentionally caused by “an insured.” The plaintiff obtained an arbitration award and then sued her uncle and aunt's insurer as a judgment creditor. The woman argued that a severability clause in the insurers' policy, a provision that is virtually identical to the Severability Clause in Safeco's policy, rendered the policy ambiguous, therefore creating coverage for the claim. Id. at pp. 7-8. The appellate court disagreed and held that the presence of the severability clause did not create coverage that was otherwise eliminated by the policy's relevant exclusion. Ibid. Like Northland, Sitthiso clearly shows that under California's law of policy construction, the Severability Clause in Safeco's policy does not render that policy ambiguous and does not change the fact that Safeco's policy does not afford coverage to any insured for harm intentionally caused by another insured.
2. The Majority of Cases from Other Jurisdictions Support Safeco's Position.
Safeco's position is also consistent with the majority of cases from other jurisdictions. At least 15 published cases from around the country have ruled on the matter presently before this Court and have arrived at the position advocated by Safeco.20 These cases far outnumber the cases supporting Minkler's position, therefore suggesting that the California Supreme Court would be more likely to issue a ruling in favor of Safeco than in favor of Minkler.
3. The History and Intent Behind the Inclusion of the Severability Clause in Insurance Policies Also Supports Safeco's Position.
Safeco's position is also supported by the intent and history behind the inclusion of severability clauses in liability policies. As Chief Justice Mathews of the Alaska Supreme Court explained in his concurring opinion in Alaska Department of Transportation and Public Facilities v. Houston Cas. Co., 797 P.2d• 1200 (Alaska 1990), a severability clause was added to liability insurance policies in 1955 to clarify the fact that a reference to “the insured” in exclusions was intended to apply only to the insured seeking coverage. Id at p. 1205. Prior to its inclusion, a rash of decisions in the 1940s and early 1950s misinterpreted the term “the insured.” These decisions typically involved cases where an insured was sued for bodily injury to an individual that was an employee of another insured. Because the majority of courts were issuing decisions contrary to the intent of the exclusion, which was to exclude coverage only for allegations of bodily injury to the employee of the particular insured that was being sued, the Severability Clause was inserted into the policy to clarify that the term “the insured” meant only the insured seeking coverage. Id. at pp. 1205-1206; see also Michael Carbone, Inc., supra, 937 F.Supp. at p. 419.
The inclusion of this clause, however, was not intended to have any bearing on exclusions for actions done by “an insured.” Unlike the term, “the insured,” which was deemed to be ambiguous in the absence of a severability clause, courts have held that the term “an insured” shows a clear and unambiguous intent to exclude coverage for any insured if the suit is based on the prohibited actions of any other insured. See, e.g., Gilbert, supra, 852 F.2d at p. 454. Thus, the Severability Clause was not intended to clarify or modify the meaning of exclusions based on the acts of “an insured” and, therefore, should not be held to alter the effect of the Intentional Acts Exclusion in Safeco's policies.
4. The Fact That the Severability Clause Is a Policy Condition and not a Coverage Provision Also Supports Safeco's Position.
The fact that the Severability Clause is a policy condition and not a coverage provision further supports Safeco's position. Policy conditions are generally intended to clarify the duties of claimants and insureds in the event of a claim, not to alter coverage provided by the policy.21 See Argent, supra, 901 A.2d at p. 427. The presence of the Severability Clause in the conditions section - surrounded by provisions that do not alter the coverage provided by the policy - would lead a reasonable insured to believe that the Severability Clause also does not alter the coverage provided by the policy. Accordingly, even if this Court were to find that the text of Safeco's policy is not clear on its face, in applying the test for insurance policy interpretation explained in Bank of the West, it should find that a reasonable insured would understand that the Severability Clause does not create coverage that is otherwise precluded by the Intentional Acts Exclusion.
5. Justice Baxter's Concurring Opinion in Robert S. Does Not Suggest that the California Supreme Court Would Rule in Minkler's Favor, and Certainly Is not “Convincing Evidence” that the California Supreme Court Would Support that Position.
Minkler argues that Judge Baxter's concurring opinion in Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Robert S., 26 Cal.4th 758, 776-777 (2001) is evidence that the California Supreme Court would rule in his favor. If anything, this concurrence suggests precisely the opposite result.
In Robert S., the son of the insured shot and killed his friend. The deceased's family sued the shooter's parents based on the theory that they were negligent in failing to prevent the shooting. Robert S., supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 761. The insurer denied coverage to the parents on the basis of an exclusion in its policy for illegal acts of “an insured”. The Court held that the exclusion was ambiguous in that it did not define the term “illegal act” and that the term could reasonably be read to mean intentional illegal acts as opposed to negligent illegal acts. On that basis, the Court ruled in favor of the insureds. Id at pp. 766-767. Justice Kennard wrote the opinion, which was joined by Chief Justice George, Justice Werdegar and Justice Chin. Each of those four justices is still on the Court.
Justice Baxter wrote a concurring and dissenting opinion, which was joined by Justice Brown, who is no longer on the Court. In his concurring and dissenting opinion, Justice Baxter stated that, even if the policy were not ambiguous, he would have held that the insureds were entitled to coverage based on the severability clause in the policy. Based on that clause, which is identical to the clause at issue in the present case, Justice Baxter argued that an insurer should not be allowed to properly deny coverage to insureds that were alleged to have acted negligently in failing to prevent the actions of another insured. Robert S., supra, 26 Cal.4th atp. 776-777.
While Justice Baxter's concurring opinion is contrary to Safeco's position, it does not suggest that the California Supreme Court would rule against Safeco in the present case. Because Justice Brown is no longer on the Court, the opinion drafted by Justice Baxter represents the thoughts on this issue of only one of the Court's current members. Moreover, four of the Court's current Justices refused to sign Justice's Baxter's opinion, thus suggesting that those Justices either disagree with Baxter's position or, at the very least, that they are at least as likely to rule in Safeco's favor as they would be to rule in Minkler's favor. Accordingly, Justice Baxter's concurring and dissenting opinion in Robert S. is not convincing evidence that the California Supreme Court would rule in Minkler's favor. Because Minkler has presented no such evidence, the District Court's opinion should be affirmed.
6. Minkler's Argument that Courts Issuing Opinions Consistent with Safeco's Position Have Uniformly Failed to Use California's Rules of Insurance Policy Interpretation Is Factually Inaccurate.
Minkler argues that the overwhelming authority supporting Safeco's position should be ignored because the cases coming to that conclusion have done so without following California's rules concerning insurance policy construction. More specifically, he states that all cases that have come to the conclusion advocated by Safeco, including the opinion rendered by the District Court in this matter, fail to read the insurance policy from the viewpoint of a reasonable insured. This argument is simply factually untrue.
First, and most obviously, both Northland and Sitthiso employed California's rules regarding insurance policy construction, as both are California cases issued after the Bank of the West decision in 1992. As is noted above, both the Northland and Sitthiso courts issued rulings consistent with Safeco's position.
Second, it is clear that the District Court analyzed the policy from the viewpoint of a reasonable insured. In its opinion, the District Court explicitly concluded that “a reasonable person would expect that the severability clause would not substantively modify the policy's coverage terms.”22 Therefore, Minkler's accusations against the District Court are erroneous.
Third, Minkler is also incorrect that the out-of-state cases that are in accord with Safeco's position did not utilize policy construction rules similar to those in California. Contrary to Minkler' s assertion, several of the cases finding in favor of Safeco's position explicitly note that part of their inquiry was to analyze the policy from the viewpoint of a lay person unschooled in law or insurance,23 while virtually all of those cases employ contract interpretation rules that nearly mirror those of California.
Ironically, it is Minkler's position that violates California's rules of insurance policy construction. As the District Court and several cases from arounc the country have noted, Minkler' s argument would render meaningless the term “an insured” from the Intentional Acts Exclusion, therefore violating the rule of policy construction requiring that each clause be given meaning. Titan Corp., supra, 22 Cal.App.4th at pp. 473-474. In fact, in an unpublished disposition, the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of California has noted that Minkler's position is untenable for precisely this reason. Evanston Ins. Co. v. OEA, Inc., 2005 WL 1828796, *9. (A copy of the OEA decision is attached as Exhibit “B” to Safeco's Responding Brief.) Because even Minkler's argument cuts in favor of Safeco, this Court should affirm the District Court's decision.
CONCLUSION
Safeco's construction of its policy is supported by virtually every relevant authority and policy rationale. The only published California case on-point, Northland, supports Safeco's position, as does the Sitthiso case, an unpublished California Appellate decision with facts virtually identical to the present matter. The majority of cases from around the country to rule on the application of the Severability Clause's affect on an exclusion for the acts of “an insured” support Safeco's position. The policy and history behind including the Severability Clause in insurance policies cut in favor of Safeco. The inclusion of the Severability Clause as a policy condition rather than a coverage provision also supports Safeco's position.
By contrast, Minkler's alleges that his position is supported by a concurring opinion signed by only one current California Supreme Court Justice and which four current Justices refused to sign, and an unfounded theory that California law was misapplied by several previous opinions on this issue, including the opinion of two California Appellate courts and the District's court's ruling in the present matter.
Safeco submits that this incredible contrast in support between its position and Minkler's position can lead only to the conclusion that the California Supreme Court would rule in Safeco's favor. The result suggested by the contrast in authority is made even more clear by the fact that, absent “convincing evidence” that the California Supreme Court would rule for Minkler's, this Court is required to follow the Northland decision and rule in Safeco's favor. Because no such evidence exists, Safeco respectfully requests that this Court affirm the District Court's decision.
