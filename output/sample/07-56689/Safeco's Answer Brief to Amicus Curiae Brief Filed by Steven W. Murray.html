*1 INTRODUCTION
Like Minkler, amici Steven Murray attempts to force the square peg that is the Severability Clause into a round hole. Murray argues that Civ Code section 1431 applies here, that it somehow creates a presumption that the rights of insured Betty Schwartz to a defense under Safeco's policy are several, i.e., separate from David's, and therefore, supposedly, her rights to coverage/defense are not affected by David's concededly-intentional acts of child sexual abuse.
But section 1431 has nothing to do with this case, and even if it did, it would create a presumption that the rights of Betty and David are joint, not several. Section 1431 says that, in the case of a right granted/created by a contract, that right is presumed to be joint unless the contract says otherwise. By its express terms, the statute applies only to contract terms that impose obligations or create rights. The Severability Clause is a condition, not a coverage grant or an exclusion, and does not grant any rights. Therefore, the statute simply does not apply here.
Moreover, assuming it might apply, section 1431 only functions to determine whether contract rights are joint or several. That is irrelevant to the issue in this case: whether, under Safeco's exclusion and the Severability Clause, coverage for a non-perpetrator insured can be barred because another insured acted intentionally.
Ironically, the very statute on which Murray relies itself refutes his claim that the insured's rights - supposedly created by the Severability Clause - are several. But if section 1431 were to apply here, it would create a presumption that the rights of the two insureds are joint. The statute thereby squarely rebuts Murray's claim that the Betty and David's rights under the insurance policy were several.
*2 Murray's final claim - that the Severability Clause creates separate policies for each insured - is likewise fatally flawed. The Clause simply requires that coverage be analyzed separately for each insured, a far cry from Murray's over-reading of the actual policy language, and no reasonable insured would read the language as creating entirely separate insurance policies, rendering the intent of one insured irrelevant for purposes of determining the potential for coverage with respect to the other insured. For all of these reasons, Murray's irrelevant brief should be disregarded for the red herring that it is.
LEGAL DISCUSSION
I.
SECTION 1431 IS IRRELEVANT TO THE INTERPRETATION OF THE SEVERABILITY CLAUSE
A. Section 1431 Only Applies To Contractual Terms That Bestow Rights Or Create Obligations, But The Severability Clause Does Neither.
In pertinent part, Civil Code section 14311 provides as follows:
“An obligation imposed upon several persons, or a right created in favor of several persons, is presumed joint, and not several.... This presumption, in the case of a right, can be overcome only by express words to the contrary.”
By its terms, this statute applies only to contractual provisions that have imposed obligations or created rights. California courts have enforced section 1431 in precisely that manner. (See, e.g., Hardie v. Chew Fish Yuen (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 301 [under section 1431, a lease provision creating a right to royalties in favor of four individuals was presumed joint]; Jameson v. Chanslor-Canfield Midway Oil Co. (1917) 176 Cal. 1, 4 *3 [section 1431 does apply to a forfeiture provision in a lease because that provision does create a right as contemplated by the statute].)
The Severability Clause, by contrast, does not create rights or impose obligations. Instead, it is a condition; it merely clarifies the rights created or limited elsewhere in the policy, in the coverage grants and exclusions. As discussed in detail in Safeco's Answer Brief on the Merits, the Severability Clause was added to liability insurance policies in the mid-1950's to clarify that policy references to “the insured” were intended to refer only to the particular insured seeking coverage. (See discussion at ABOM, pp. 18-21; see also Alaska Department of Transportation and Public Facilities v. Houston Cas. Co. (Alaska 1990) 797 P.2d 1200, 1205.) Here, of course, Safeco's policy does not use the restrictive term “the insured,” but instead bars coverage for all insureds when any has acted intentionally, and therefore has no application to the case at bench. But in any event, the Severability Clause does not expand or contract the rights or obligations of the parties under the coverage grants or exclusions. Since the clause neither creates rights nor imposes liabilities, section 1431 is not relevant to its interpretation.
B. Even If The Severability Clause - Or The Coverage Grant - Does Create “Rights,” Whether Those Rights Are Joint Or Several Is Irrelevant Because Even Several Rights Can Be Limited By The Acts Of Other Insureds.
The crux of Murray's argument is that, pursuant to section 1431, the Severability Clause requires that the rights created by the policy in favor of the insureds are several and not joint. However, even assuming that is true, the statute does not help Murray or Minkler. The issue in this case is whether the intentional acts exclusion may limit/bar the *4 coverage otherwise “created” by the coverage grant, not whether the rights bestowed by the policy are joint or several.
A joint right is one that can be satisfied by performance or tender of performance to any joint promisee; by contrast, a several right can be satisfied only by performing the obligation to each promisee individually. (Civ. Code, section 1475; Cober v. Connolly (1942) 20 Cal.2d 741, 744.) Thus, if Murray is correct, the Severability Clause creates rights in favor of Betty Schwartz that can only be satisfied by performance specifically to her.
But the analysis does not end there. Even if Betty's rights under the policy are “several,” those rights can still be limited, and coverage can be precluded as to her, if David acted intentionally within the meaning of the intentional acts exclusion. That her rights are several only means that Safeco cannot satisfy its obligations to Betty -assuming it owes any - by claiming it performed as to David. The intentional acts exclusion in the policy still properly limits her coverage. Under that exclusion, as consistently construed by the California courts, Safeco has no obligation to any insured when one insured's potential liability stems from the intentional act of another insured. Even several rights may be subject to limitations expressed in the contract.
Safeco has never argued that any obligations it has to one insured can be met through performance to another insured, nor that affording coverage to one insured eliminates the need to cover another. That is not the issue. Rather, the question is whether the intentional acts exclusion eliminates coverage for one insured based on the intentional sexual molestation committed by another. The classification of the relevant rights as joint or several is irrelevant.
An analysis of the cases cited by Murray illustrates the point. Neither of those decisions ever mentions the right of a contracting party *5 to include provisions limiting its obligations. (May v. Nevada Irrigation District (9th Cir. 1979) 600 F.2d 1280, 1282-1283 [holding that the language in the contract was sufficient to overcome the presumption created by section 1431, but rendering no opinion on contracting parties' right to restrict their obligations under the contract]; Petroleum Midway Co., Ltd. v. Moynier (1928) 205 Cal. 733, 739-740 [holding only that a lease created several rights to royalties, not whether those rights may be limited by the contract's terms].) Even if Murray is correct that the Severability Clause requires a finding that the rights bestowed on David and Betty Schwartz by the policy are several rather than joint, that does not prevent Safeco from limiting coverage for one insured based on the acts of another insured. The intentional acts exclusion would still eliminate coverage for all insureds for liability based on the intentional acts of “an insured,” such as the sexual molestation committed by David Schwartz.
C. Assuming It Applies At All, Section 1431 Creates A Presumption That The Right Is A Joint One, Squarely Contrary To Murray's Claim That Betty's Right Is Several.
Ironically, Murray's reliance on section 1431 actually defeats his ultimate position. Even assuming that the Severability Clause itself created rights in favor of Betty and David Schwartz, and further assuming that the classification of those rights as joint or several were relevant to any issue here, the result would be precisely contrary to that which Murray advocates.
By its terms, section 1431 creates a presumption that rights created by contract are joint rights unless the contract says otherwise. Yet Murray argues that the rights are several, i.e., that David's *6 intentional act cannot deprive Betty of coverage. The very statute on which Murray relies categorically refutes his position.
II.
THE SEVERABILITY CLAUSE DOES NOT CREATE SEPARATE POLICIES FOR EACH INSURED.
Murray further argues that the Severability Clause should be read as creating separate insurance policies for each insured. This interpretation is inherently unreasonable under both the “plain meaning” test and the “reasonable expectations” tests that this Court commonly applies to interpret insurance contracts. (See, e.g., Bank of the West v. Superior Court (Indus. Indem. Co.) (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1254; Rosen v. State Farm Gen. Ins. Co. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 1070, 1074-1075.)2
The Severability Clause states: “[t]his insurance applies separately to each insured. This condition will not increase our limit of liability for any one occurrence.” (2 ER 78, ¶2.) This does not create separate policies. It merely requires that coverage be analyzed separately for each of the relevant insureds. (See discussion in Safeco's ABOM, pp. 25-26.)
Analyzing coverage separately does not render the existence of the other insured, or the intent of that other insured, irrelevant. (See discussion in Safeco's ABOM, pp. 21-26.) Nor does separate analysis compel the conclusion that the intentional act exclusion - which, as Minkler concedes, bars coverage for all insureds when “an insured” has acted intentionally - should be rewritten as if it had keyed coverage to the acts of “the insured.” Any other reading of the Severability Clause would vastly over-read the actual words and violate the “plain meaning” and “reasonable expectations” tests.
*7 A reasonable insured would not expect the Severability Clause to override the clear import of the intentional acts exclusion. The history and intent behind the Severability Clause shows that it was not intended to be interpreted in that manner. (Alaska Dept. of Trans., supra, 797 P.2d at p. 1205.) The fact that the Severability Clause is located in the policy conditions, surrounded by the policy's other procedural requirements, supports that conclusion. Moreover, California law requires that the specific provisions of an insurance policy, such as the intentional acts exclusion, should govern on the specific topics they address irrespective of general provision such as the Severability Clause. (Ortega Rock Quarry v. Golden Eagle Ins. Corp. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 969, 981.) Accordingly, even if the Court were to examine this issue under the “reasonable expectations” test, that test would still require a finding in Safeco's favor.
*8 III.
CONCLUSION
Section 1431 's presumption in favor of joint rights has no bearing on the interpretation of the Severability Clause or Safeco's policy in general. Even if that statute were relevant, it would weigh in favor of Safeco's position and not Minkler's. Moreover, for the reasons discussed above and in Safeco's Answer Brief on the Merits, the Severability Clause does not create separate policies for each insured and should not be read to eviscerate the intentional acts exclusion.
