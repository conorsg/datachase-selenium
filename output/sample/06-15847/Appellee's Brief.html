INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT
This appeal represents Plaintiffs latest attempt to get another bite of the proverbial apple. Plaintiff and Appellant Kevin Murray (“Plaintiff”) is a former employee of Alaska Airlines who claims that the airline retaliated against him because he disclosed information regarding unsafe conditions and industrial safety violations to Federal Aviation Administration (“FAA”) officials. Alaska Airlines previously defended and defeated this same claim before the United States Department of Labor, pursuant to the comprehensive federal scheme to adjudicate whistle-blowing claims of employees providing air safety information, the so-called AIR21 statute, 49 U.S.C. §42121. The Secretary of Labor concluded that Plaintiffs claim was without merit and that there was no causal nexus between Plaintiffs termination and his whistle-blowing activity.
Plaintiff failed to request a review of the Secretary's decision by an Administrative Law Judge as prescribed by the statute, upon which the decision became final and not subject to judicial review. Regardless, Plaintiff brought this identical action in the hope that a “second opinion” would result in a more favorable outcome. The District Court properly granted summary judgment for Alaska Airlines on the grounds that Plaintiff is collaterally estopped from relitigating issues that were already decided by the Secretary and that are essential to his “new” claims.
By this appeal, Plaintiff argues that he was not afforded an adequate opportunity to litigate his claim because he abandoned the administrative proceeding prior to the point he would have been afforded such an opportunity. This Court should reject this argument and affirm summary judgment for Alaska Airlines for three related but independent reasons.
First, Plaintiff is collaterally estopped from relitigating issues already decided by the Secretary of Labor that are essential to establishing his common law claims. Plaintiff's failure to take advantage of sufficient administrative procedures to which he was entitled defeats his argument that he did not have an adequate opportunity to litigate his claims. See Argument I, infra.
Second, Plaintiff cannot maintain this action because he is statutorily barred from doing so. The AIR21 statute expressly prohibits Plaintiff from collaterally attacking the Secretary of Labor's decision by bringing this action. See Argument II, infra.
Finally, Plaintiff's claims are barred because he failed to exhaust his administrative and judicial remedies as prescribed by the AIR21 statute. See Argument III, infra.
JURISDICTIONAL STATEMENT
The Court has jurisdiction to consider this appeal in accordance with Plaintiff's jurisdictional statement.
STATEMENT OF ISSUES PRESENTED FOR REVIEW
The issues presented by this appeal are as follows:
1. Whether a plaintiff who initiates an administrative proceeding under the AIR21 statute, and then abandons that proceeding after obtaining an unfavorable decision from the administrative agency, is collaterally estopped from pursuing identical common law claims in a court of law where plaintiff failed to avail himself of additional administrative procedures following the administrative agency's decision.
2. Whether a plaintiff may collaterally attack a final decision of an administrative agency by bringing an action in a court of law based on the same primary right and issues litigated in the administrative proceeding.
3. Whether a plaintiff who fails to request a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge and who fails to appeal a final decision of the Secretary of Labor to the local United States Court of Appeals as set forth under the statute at issue is deemed to have failed to exhaust his administrative and judicial remedies as a matter of law.
STATEMENT OF FACTS AND OF THE CASE1
A. Plaintiff's Employment With Alaska Airlines.
Alaska Airlines hired Plaintiff in June 2002 as an Aircraft Maintenance Technician in its Oakland, California heavy maintenance base. ER 3 ¶9; ER 58. In June 2003, Plaintiff became a Quality Assurance Auditor at the Oakland facility. ER 123 ¶1. In this position, Plaintiff was primarily responsible for the development and implementation of Alaska Airlines' Quality Assurance auditing program. ER 50 ¶2.
In or about September 2004, Alaska Airlines closed its Oakland heavy maintenance base to improve its long-term viability in a competitive market. ER 50 ¶3; see also ER 123 ¶2. All employees, including Plaintiff, received notice of the Oakland base closure on September 9, 2004. ER 123 ¶3. With the closure of the Oakland maintenance base, Plaintiff's Quality Assurance Auditor position was eliminated. ER 123 ¶4. Plaintiff was not hired for another position within Alaska Airlines following the base closure; as a result, Plaintiff's employment was terminated. ER 123 ¶¶5-6.
B. Plaintiff Initiates - And Loses - AIR21 Administrative Proceedings Against Alaska Airlines.
1. The AIR21 Statute.
On December 8, 2004, Plaintiff filed a complaint with the Secretary of Labor against Alaska Airlines alleging that his termination and failure to be hired for another position violated Section 519 of the Wendell H. Ford Aviation Investment and Reform Act of the 21st Century (“AIR21”), codified in 49 U.S.C. §42121. ER 58-60; ER 123 ¶7. Inclusive within the AIR21 statute is the Whistleblower Protection Program (“WPP”), the comprehensive federal administrative program for adjudicating whistle-blowing claims of airline employees who raise air carrier safety concerns.
Under the WPP, an employee who believes that he was retaliated against for providing air carrier safety information to his employer and/or the federal government may file a complaint with the Secretary of Labor. 49 U.S.C. §42121 (b)(l). The Secretary conducts an investigation and then issues a written preliminary order of her findings, either that the complaint is without merit, or that there is “reasonable cause” to believe that a violation has occurred. Id. §42121 (b)(2)(A). If the Secretary determines that the complaint has merit, the Secretary will order the employer to (1) take affirmative action to abate the violation, (2) reinstate the employee to his former position and award compensation, including back pay, and (3) provide compensatory damages. Id. §42121(b)(3)(B).
No later than thirty days after the issuance of the Secretary's preliminary order, either side may file objections to the findings and/or preliminary order, and request a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge (“ALJ”) on the record. Id. §42121(b)(2)(A). Failure to timely request a hearing before an ALJ results in a final order that is not subject to judicial review. Id.; see also 29 C.F.R. §1979.106(b)(2) (2006).
If a hearing is requested, the ALJ must take documentary and witness evidence, maintain a record of the proceedings and issue a final order based on the evidence presented. See 29 C.F.R. §1979.107(a) (2007) (providing that AIR21 hearings are to be conducted in accordance with the rules of practice and procedure codified at 29 C.F.R. pt. 18(A) (2007)). Either party seeking to appeal the ALJ's decision must file a petition for review with the Administrative Review Board, and a party aggrieved by the Board's decision may file a petition for review to the local United States Court of Appeals no later than sixty days after the Board's decision is issued. 29 C.F.R. §1979.110(a) (2007); id. §1979.112(a) (2006); 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(4)(A). Failure to timely file a petition of review before the appropriate circuit court results in a final order that is not subject to judicial review. 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(4)(A-B); 29 C.F.R. §1979.112(a) (2007).
2. Plaintiff Alleges He Was Wrongfully Terminated In Retaliation For His Alleged Whistle-Blowing Activity.
Plaintiff's complaint to the Secretary of Labor (“AIR21 Complaint”) alleged that between July 2003 and September 2004, Plaintiff disclosed information regarding unsafe conditions and industrial safety violations at Alaska Airlines' Oakland facility to FAA inspectors. ER 59. Plaintiff alleged that he “was admonished and chastised” upon informing Alaska Airlines management of these disclosures. Id.
Plaintiff claimed that in retaliation for notifying the FAA of these violations and unsafe conditions, Alaska Airlines terminated Plaintiff and refused to rehire him as a Quality Assurance Auditor at another location. ER 58. Specifically, Plaintiff alleged that while “[m]any other Oakland management personnel remained in their departments and transitioned to similar positions in Seattle, without applying or interviewing for open positions.... I was refused an open QA auditor position in Seattle despite my stated and documented request to remain with Alaska Airlines.” Id.
3. The Department Of Labor Rejects Plaintiff's Claim.
On June 8, 2005, following an investigation, the Secretary of Labor found “no credible basis to believe” that Alaska Airlines retaliated against Plaintiff for whistle blowing in violation of the AIR21 statute and dismissed Plaintiff's AIR21 Complaint. ER 62. Relying on both documentary evidence and witness testimony, the Secretary found that Plaintiff failed to establish a causal connection between his termination and his disclosures of safety information to the FAA. ER 63-64. Specifically, the Secretary found that Plaintiff was not considered for the two open auditor positions in Seattle because he removed himself from consideration by removing his résumé from Alaska Airlines' employment website the same night he posted it. ER 63. Thus, the Secretary concluded that “the evidence shows that [Alaska Airlines] acted reasonably when [Plaintiff] was laid off from his job” and that Plaintiff “failed to establish a nexus between his protected activity and the perceived discriminatory action taken against him.” ER 64.
The Secretary's decision specifically informed Plaintiff of his right to file objections to the decision and the consequences of not doing so:
AIR21 permits an aggrieved party, WITHIN 30 DAYS of receipt of the Secretary's Findings, to file objections with the Department of Labor and to request a hearing on the record before an Administrative Law Judge. If you file objections, you will have the responsibility of litigating your position before an Administrative Law Judge of the Department of Labor. If no objections are filed WITHIN 30 DAYS, this decision shall become final and not subject to judicial review. (ER 64 (bold emphasis in original; italicized emphasis added); see also AOB 5)
Despite receiving notice of his right to file objections with the Department of Labor and to request a hearing before an ALJ, Plaintiff admittedly did not exercise this right. See AOB 4.
C. Plaintiff Abandons His Administrative Remedies And Files An Identical Action In Superior Court.
Rather than pursue his administrative remedies as set forth under the AIR21 statute, Plaintiff elected to file the present action in Alameda Superior Court on August 2, 2005. ER 1-15. Plaintiff's “new” Complaint (the “Civil Complaint”) alleged two causes of action: (1) Wrongful Termination and (2) Violation of Public Policy: Retaliation for Whistle Blowing. Id. Alaska Airlines removed the action to the United States District Court for the Northern District of California based on diversity jurisdiction. See AOB 6.
An examination of Plaintiff's Civil Complaint reveals that the action is virtually identical to his AIR21 Complaint, and in fact, incorporates verbatim some of the same language in the allegations forming the prior complaint. See, e.g., ER 6 ¶17; 9 ¶27 (alleging disclosures regarding unsafe conditions and industrial safety violations at the Oakland facility to FAA Aviation Inspectors); ER 7 ¶18; 9 ¶28 (alleging that Plaintiff “was admonished and chastised” upon informing Alaska Airlines management of the disclosures); compare ER 6 ¶17; 9 ¶27 with ER 59 (using verbatim language to describe industrial safety violations).
Plaintiff renewed his once-rejected allegation (in both claims) that he was terminated in retaliation for notifying FAA inspectors of these violations and unsafe conditions, and again alleged that he was refused an open Quality Assurance Auditor position in Seattle. ER 7 ¶¶19-20; 9-10 ¶¶29-30.
D. The District Court Grants Summary Judgment On The Civil Complaint.
On March 27, 2006, Judge Martin J. Jenkins of the U.S. District Court granted Alaska Airlines' Motion for Summary Judgment on both causes of action in Plaintiff's Civil Complaint. ER 125-35. Judge Jenkins held that Plaintiff was precluded from contesting the Secretary of Labor's factual findings under the doctrine of collateral estoppel. ER 131-35. Specifically, the court concluded that the Secretary's finding that there was no causal nexus between Plaintiff's termination and his whistle blowing activity estopped Plaintiff from relitigating the causation issue that was essential to establishing Plaintiffs state law claims. Id. In so holding, the court found that the WPP procedures were sufficiently adjudicatory for collateral estoppel purposes, and that Plaintiff could not complain about the opportunity to fully litigate his claim having abandoned the administrative procedures. ER 132-33. Plaintiff timely appealed. ER 137-39.
STANDARD OF REVIEW
In its de novo review of Judge Jenkins' order granting summary judgment, the Court must determine whether Plaintiff has identified specific facts showing that there is a “genuine issue for trial.” Matsushita Elec. Indus. Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U.S. 574, 586-87 (1986). Plaintiff must produce “significant probative evidence” in support of his legal theories; a mere “scintilla of evidence” will not suffice. Tritron Energy Corp v. Square D Co., 68 F.3d 1216, 1221-22 (9th Cir. 1995); Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., 477 U.S. 242, 252 (1986). Finally, any asserted dispute must be material and genuine; Plaintiff cannot rely on unreasonable inferences or “mere speculation, conjecture, or fantasy” to avoid summary judgment. Barnes v. Arden Mayfair, Inc., 759 F.2d 676, 681 (9th Cir. 1985).
ARGUMENT
I.
SUMMARY JUDGMENT WAS PROPER BECAUSE PLAINTIFF IS COLLATERALLY ESTOPPED FROM RELITIGATING ISSUES HE HAD THE OPPORTUNITY TO LITIGATE AND THAT WERE ACTUALLY DECIDED IN AN ADMINISTRATIVE PROCEEDING.
The District Court properly granted summary judgment on the basis that the Secretary of Labor's decision that there was no causal nexus between Plaintiff's termination and his whistle-blowing activity collaterally estops Plaintiff from relitigating those same issues in court. It is undisputed that causation is a required element of both claims in Plaintiff's Civil Complaint.2 Thus, so long as the requirements for applying collateral estoppel are met here, the Secretary of Labor's decision has a preclusive effect on and is fatal to Plaintiff's claims. Vandenberg v. Superior Court, 21 Cal. 4th 815, 827-29 (1999) (collateral estoppel precludes a party from relitigating issues previously decided in another proceeding).
Under California law, issues determined by an administrative agency may collaterally estop (i.e., have an “issue-preclusive” effect on) a later judicial action if (1) the administrative proceeding was sufficiently adjudicatory in nature and (2) the traditional criteria for the application of collateral estoppel are met. Jacobs v. CBS Broad. Inc., 291 F.3d 1173, 1177-78 (9th Cir. 2002).3
The former requirement looks to whether the “administrative agency is acting in a judicial capacity and resolves disputed issues of fact properly before it which the parties have had an adequate opportunity to litigate.” People v. Sims, 32 Cal. 3d 468, 479 (1982) (citation omitted). The latter requirement is met if (1) “the issue sought to be precluded from relitigation [is] identical to that decided in [the] former proceeding,” (2) the issue was “actually litigated in the former proceeding,” (3) the issue was “necessarily decided in the former proceeding,” (4) “the decision in the former proceeding [was] final and on the merits” and (5) “the party against whom preclusion is sought [is] the same as, or in privity with, the party to the former proceeding.” Lucido v. Superior Court, 51 Cal. 3d 335, 341 (1990).
Plaintiff does not dispute, and thus concedes, many of these requirements. He does not dispute that the Secretary of Labor resolved disputed issues of fact properly before her. Nor does he argue that the issue before this Court is identical to that considered by the Secretary, that the Secretary “necessarily decided” the issue, that such a decision was final and on the merits, and that the parties in both proceedings are identical. As such, Plaintiff has waived any challenge to any of these requirements. See Indep. Towers of Washington v. Washington, 350 F.3d 925, 929 (9th Cir. 2003) (court only reviews issues that are “specifically and distinctly” argued in opening brief) (citation omitted); Barnett v. U.S. Air, Inc., 228 F.3d 1105, 1110 n.1 (9th Cir. 2000) (“issues not raised in a party's opening brief are waived”).
Instead, Plaintiff's entire basis for appeal rests on the narrow claim that because the Secretary's decision was based on an initial investigation and Plaintiff admittedly abandoned the WPP administrative procedures, Plaintiff never had an adequate “opportunity to litigate” his claims. AOB 9-10. He thus claims the WPP procedures were not sufficiently adjudicatory in nature (AOB 9 (“[t]he relevant question thus becomes whether one who initiates, and then abandons such administrative procedure, shall be held to have participated in an adjudicatory proceeding”)), and that the traditional collateral estoppel criterion that the issue be “actually litigated” was not met. AOB 16- 17. As discussed below, Plaintiff is wrong.
A. The WPP Proceedings Provided The Parties An Adequate Opportunity To Litigate.
The crux of this appeal is Plaintiff's contention that he was denied an opportunity to litigate his whistle-blower claims. This argument is misguided, as it completely ignores the procedures available to Plaintiff under the WPP. In making this determination, “[i]t is the opportunity to litigate that is important... , not whether the litigant availed himself or herself of the opportunity.” Rymer v. Hagler, 211 Cal. App. 3d 1171, 1178-79 (1989) (emphasis added); see also Sims, 32 Cal. 3d at 481-82 (“[w]hat is significant here is that the County had ... the opportunity and incentive to present its case to the hearing officer.... The People cannot now take advantage of the fact that the County avoided its litigation responsibilities and chose not to present evidence at the prior proceeding”).
Here, Plaintiff failed to take advantage of the opportunity to have the issue of whether he was retaliated against heard by an ALJ. 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(2)(A). In such a proceeding, (1) the administrative hearing would be conducted in a judicial-like adversary proceeding, (2) the agency determination would involve the adjudicatory application of rules to a single set of facts, (3) the proceedings would be conducted before an impartial ALJ, (4) the parties would have the right to subpoena witnesses and present documentary evidence, and (5) the administrative agency would have maintained a verbatim record of the proceedings. See 29 C.F.R. §18.24 (2007) (subpoenas); id. § 18.47 (exhibits); id. § 18.52 (record of hearings); id. § 18.59 (certification of official record); see generally id. § 1979.107(a) (2007) (providing that WPP hearings under AIR21 statute will generally be conducted in accordance with rules of practice and procedure codified at 29 C.F.R. pt. 18(A)). Plaintiffs appeal to this Court that the Secretary's investigation did not afford him such “procedural safeguards” therefore rings hollow given his complete failure to timely petition for a hearing before an ALJ where such alleged safeguards would have been provided. AOB 10-11.4
Courts repeatedly reject claims that a party was not provided an adequate opportunity to litigate when the party failed to take advantage of sufficient administrative procedures to which they were entitled. In Fadaie v. Alaska Airlines, Inc., 293 F. Supp. 2d 1210, 1218 (W.D. Wash. 2003), a case strikingly similar to this one, the Western District of Washington considered whether plaintiff's retaliation claim under state law was barred by res judicata where a significant portion of the claim rested on allegations already presented in an AIR21 complaint to the Department of Labor. Plaintiff argued that res judicata was inappropriate because the Secretary of Labor's decision was based on an investigation rather than an adjudicative proceeding and thus he was denied a full and fair opportunity to present his claims. 293 F. Supp. 2d at 1219. The court rejected this argument, finding that the WPP procedures afforded plaintiff “ample opportunity to fully present his claims, including avenues of appeal that provided direct and apparently unique access to the federal appellate courts” (id. at 1219 n.3), and that plaintiff was estopped from making this argument as he opted not to request a hearing before an ALJ after receiving an unfavorable decision from the Secretary:
For whatever reason, Mr. Fadaie opted not to request a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge. After receiving the Regional Administrator's decision letter, he decided not to follow through on the procedures set forth in the letter, thereby waiving his right to an adversarial hearing. Contrary to plaintiffs argument, the WPP provides complainants with an opportunity to fully and fairly litigate their claims: plaintiffs cannot now argue that the procedures utilized by the agency were insufficient when it was Mr. Fadaie's choice to forego the admittedly sufficient procedures to which he was entitled. (Id. at 1219-20)5
See also Taylor v. Heckler, 765 F.2d 872, 875-76 (9th Cir. 1985) (plaintiff's failure to seek hearing before ALJ after Secretary of Health and Human Services denied her claim for benefits resulted in res judicata on subsequent review where plaintiff failed to seek timely reconsideration of initial determination); Eilrich v. Remas, 839 F.2d 630, 632 (9th Cir. 1988) (“[i]f an adequate opportunity for review is available, a losing party cannot obstruct the preclusive use of the state administrative decision simply by foregoing [the] right to appeal”) (citation omitted); Misischia v. Pirie, 60 F.3d 626, 630 (9th Cir. 1995) (same); Mola Dev. Corp. v. City of Seal Beach, 57 Cal. App. 4th 405, 413 (1997) (where plaintiff abandoned right to an administrative hearing, plaintiff “cannot rely on a silent record to challenge the fairness of the administrative forum, or to assert that some of the ... issues could not have been litigated. [Plaintiff] is ‘drawn into a vacuum of its own making ...”’ (citation omitted).
Plaintiff's citations to Jacobs, 291 F.3d at 1179, and Vella v. Hudgins, 20 Cal. 3d 251, 255-56 (1977) (see AOB 10, 13-14, 16), are inapposite here. Neither case stands for the proposition that a court is to look exclusively at the determination at issue and not at the entire scope of administrative procedures available to the plaintiff. Indeed, neither case even involved administrative proceedings, and in neither case did the plaintiffs have access to such additional procedures. Jacobs and Vella are thus both distinguishable from this case, where Plaintiff failed to avail himself of the opportunity to fully litigate his case before an ALJ.
Finally, Plaintiff's argument that his abandonment of the administrative forum somehow changes the analysis because the WPP procedures are not mandatory misses the point. AOB 8-9, 16. While it is true that Plaintiff could have initially brought his claim in a court of law,
this choice of remedies ended once plaintiff initiated the administrative complaint process and obtained a final decision from the Secretary of Labor. At that point, the statute obligated him to complete the administrative process, such that the only avenues of relief available to him were a hearing before the ALJ and a direct appeal to the Ninth Circuit. (Fadaie, 293 F. Supp. 2d at 1220)
The AIR21 statute very clearly sets forth the consequences for Plaintiff's failure to timely request a hearing before an ALJ after the Secretary issued her preliminary order, i.e., that the decision resulted in a final order that was not subject to judicial review. 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(2)(A); 29 C.F.R. § 1979.106(b)(2) (2007). Thus, what Plaintiff was not entitled to do was to enter into the WPP proceeding and treat the outcome as merely an advisory opinion if it was unfavorable to him. Were this Court to hold otherwise, it would create an incentive for plaintiffs to needlessly encumber the resources of administrative agencies, and, ultimately, the courts. This is not what Congress intended. As stated by one court in this context:
It is entirely acceptable in the area of medical practice to seek a second opinion (though that should be contrasted with the unseemly image conveyed by a patient who goes from doctor to doctor until the patient at last obtains a diagnosis that agrees with his or her self-evaluation), but that procedure finds no echo in the field of law. (Patricia P. v. Bd. of Educ. of Oak Park & River Forest High Sch. Dist. No. 200, 8 F. Supp. 2d 801, 807 (N.D. Ill. 1998), aff'd, 203 F.3d 462 (7th Cir. 2000))
In short, Plaintiff is hoist by his own petard by his failure to take advantage of the WPP administrative proceedings available to him. Any failure to give preclusive effect to the Secretary of Labor's finding here “‘would encourage litigants to bypass prescribed appeal procedures of an agency, [which] should weigh strongly in the decision”’ in applying collateral estoppel. Alten v. Ellis & Tucker, Chartered, 854 F. Supp. 283, 291-92 (D. Del. 1994) (citation omitted). The Court should affirm the District Court's grant of summary judgment.
B. The Causation Issue Was Actually Litigated Before The Department Of Labor.
Plaintiffs claim that the issue of causation was never “actually litigated” at the administrative level must also be rejected. AOB 16-17. “An issue is actually litigated when it is properly raised, by the pleadings or otherwise, and is submitted for determination, and is determined.” Sims, 32 Cal. 3d at 484 (citing Restatement (Second) of Judgments §27 cmt. d (1982)) (alternations and internal quotation marks omitted). Plaintiff concedes that he submitted a complaint to the Secretary of Labor, alleging that Alaska Airlines retaliated against him due to his whistle-blowing activities, which the Secretary investigated and eventually found to be meritless based on the lack of a causal nexus between Plaintiff's whistle-blowing and the adverse employment actions he experienced. AOB 4-5. As the District Court properly found, “the Secretary of Labor did address Plaintiff's claim on the merits, issuing a written opinion based upon statements and witness evidence from both parties. The Secretary articulated its reasoning for its factual findings including references to particular pieces [ofi evidence in the record[.]” ER 133 (emphasis in original).
Plaintiff's contention that the issue was never “actually litigated” because Plaintiff abandoned the administrative proceeding and thus never had the opportunity to litigate does not alter this conclusion. AOB 16-17. What is relevant is that Plaintiff “was entitled to a full hearing and [would have] had ample opportunity to raise issues and present evidence at that hearing.” Castillo v. City of Los Angeles, 92 Cal. App. 4th 477, 482 (2001); see also Sims, 32 Cal. 3d at 484.
Both Sims and Castillo demonstrate the importance that California courts place on litigants having had the opportunity - rather than having taken advantage of the opportunity - to fully and actually litigate their claims in the prior administrative proceeding. In Sims, the California Supreme Court determined that the issue of respondent's guilt or innocence of welfare fraud had been fully litigated even though the County failed to participate in the hearing on the matter. 32 Cal. 3d at 484. In Castillo, the California Court of Appeal determined that plaintiffs argument that the issue of whether he was discharged due to discrimination was not litigated in the former administrative proceedings lacked merit because plaintiff did not show “that he was prevented from introducing admissible evidence relevant to that issue” in the prior proceeding. 92 Cal. App. 4th at 482.
Because Plaintiff raised and submitted the causation issue for determination, was provided the full opportunity to litigate the issue and received a determination on that issue, causation was “actually litigated” in the prior administrative proceeding and summary judgment should be affirmed.
II.
THE JUDGMENT SHOULD BE AFFIRMED BECAUSE PLAINTIFF CANNOT COLLATERALLY ATTACK THE SECRETARY OF LABOR'S DECISION.
Summary judgment should also be affirmed because the AIR21 statute bars Plaintiff from collaterally attacking the Secretary of Labor's decision.6 “A collateral attack is made, not in a proceeding brought for the specific pur-pose of attacking the judgment, but in some other proceeding having a different purpose - it is an attempt to avoid the effect of a judgment or order made in some other proceeding.” Gonzales v. State, 68 Cal. App. 3d 621, 632 (1977); see also 56 Am. Jur. 2D Motions, Rules, and Orders §59 (2000) (same).
Plaintiff's Civil Complaint is nothing less than an attempt to avoid the effect of the AIR21 judicial review provisions and to collaterally attack the Secretary of Labor's decision. The AIR21 statute provides, “If a hearing [before an ALJ] is not requested in such 30-day period [after the date of notification of the Secretary's findings], the preliminary order shall be deemed a final order that is not subject to judicial review.” 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(2)(A) (emphasis added).7 The statute further clarifies that a Secretary's final order is not subject to collateral attack:
Limitation on collateral attack. - An order of the Secretary of Labor with respect to which review could have been obtained under subparagraph (A) [which provides for review by the local circuit court of a Secretary's final order issued after a hearing before an ALJ] shall not be subject to judicial review in any criminal or other civil proceeding. (49 U.S.C. §42121 (b)(4)(B))
See also 29 C.F.R. §1979.112(a) (2007); H.R. REP. No. 106-167(1), at 121 (1999), available at 1999 WL 355951 (stating that Section 42121(b)(4)(B) “prohibits a person from challenging the final order in another judicial proceeding if it could have been appealed under subparagraph (A) above”).
Plaintiff concedes that he did not timely request a hearing before an ALJ. ER 123-124 ¶9. As discussed above, the AIR21 judicial review provisions make clear that once Plaintiff initiated WPP procedures to the point of receiving a preliminary order from the Secretary of Labor, and failed to timely request a hearing before an ALJ, he was precluded from seeking judicial relief from this order. 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(2)(A); id. §42121(b)(4)(B); see generally Argument 1(A), supra. In sum, Plaintiff “cannot, as he has done here, collaterally attack an adverse ruling from the Secretary by filing a new civil action in district court.” Fadaie, 293 F. Supp. 2d at 1220 (citation omitted); see also Olson v. Morris, 188 F.3d 1083, 1086 (9th Cir. 1999) (where statute provided that failure to appeal administrative decision rendered decision final, “the failure to seek judicial review of an administrative order precludes collateral attack of the order in a separate complaint”).8
The District Court found that Plaintiff's action here does not constitute a collateral attack because “a decision in the instant action would not attack the separate federal right adjudicated in the WPP proceedings.” ER 130. This was error. Plaintiff seeks to enforce the same primary right in both his AIR21 Complaint and the Civil Complaint - the right to be free from an adverse employment action based on retaliatory discrimination. See Swartzendruber v. City of San Diego, 3 Cal. App. 4th 896, 904-06 (1992) (where plaintiff failed to seek timely review of administrative action as required by statute, subsequent claims based on same primary right - right to continued employment - were barred), disapproved on other grounds by Johnson v. City of Loma Linda, 24 Cal. 4th 61 (2000).9 If Plaintiff were successful in this action, it would eviscerate the Secretary of Labor's finding that there was no connection between the adverse employment action he suffered and his whistle-blowing.
A pair of decisions by this Court are instructive. In Gilbert v. BenAsher, 900 F.2d 1407 (9th Cir. 1990), plaintiff failed to seek judicial review of an administrative decision revoking his medical license. Under the pertinent statute, this failure barred judicial review of the order. Id. at 1408-09 & n.4. Plaintiff filed suit in federal district court asserting numerous claims, most of which this Court found were precluded on resjudicata and collateral estoppel grounds. Id. at 1410-11. The Court separately analyzed plaintiff's claim for abuse of process, but held that the claim was an impermissible collateral attack on the administrative decision “under the guise of a complaint sounding in tort. [Plaintiff] can prevail on the tort claims only by proving that the revocation was improper. This pivotal issue has already been litigated and decided against him.”' Id. at 1411 (emphasis in original; citation omitted).
Similarly, in Tur v. FAA, 104 F.3d 290 (9th Cir. 1997), plaintiff was found guilty of violating two FAA regulations in a prior administrative proceeding by an ALJ of the National Transportation Safety Board (“NTSB”). Id. at 291. The underlying statute provided that courts of appeals have “exclusive jurisdiction to affirm, amend, modify, or set aside” an NTSB order. 49 U.S.C. §46110(c). Plaintiff subsequently filed suit in district court against the FAA attorneys based on the knowing use of false testimony against him in the NTSB proceeding. 104 F.3d at 291. This Court held that the merits of plaintiff's claim were “inescapably intertwined with the merits of the previous” NTSB order and as such, constituted an impermissible collateral attack on the previous administrative proceeding. Id. at 292. As this Court explained, if allowed to proceed, plaintiff's suit “would result in new adjudication over the evidence and testimony adduced in the [prior administrative] hearing, the credibility determinations made by the ALJ, and, ultimately, the findings made by the ALJ. The judicial review provision of the FAA does not permit the district court to entertain such a suit.” Id.10
Here, Plaintiff can only prevail on his retaliation and violation of public policy claims by showing that there was a causal nexus between the adverse employment action and Plaintiff's whistle-blowing activity. This pivotal issue was already litigated and decided against him in the previous administrative proceeding. Gilbert, 900 F.2d at 1411. The merits of Plaintiff's Civil Complaint are thus “inescapably intertwined” with the merits of the Secretary of Labor's findings and order such that if allowed to proceed, it would bring into question the Secretary's finding that there was no connection between Plaintiffs adverse employment action and his whistle-blowing. Tur, 104 F.3d at 292. Because Plaintiff failed to seek review of the Secretary of Labor's decision as provided in the AIR21 statute, he is precluded from further litigating the issue decided by the Secretary - whether he was retaliated against for blowing the whistle on Alaska Airlines - in any court. As such, Plaintiff's Civil Complaint is an impermissible collateral attack and must be dismissed.
III.
THE JUDGMENT SHOULD BE AFFIRMED BECAUSE PLAINTIFF FAILED TO EXHAUST HIS ADMINISTRATIVE AND JUDICIAL REMEDIES.
Plaintiff's failure to exhaust his administrative and judicial remedies also compels summary judgment for Alaska Airlines.11 “Under the doctrine of exhaustion of administrative remedies, ‘the rule is that where an administrative remedy is provided by statute, relief must be sought from the administrative body and this remedy exhausted before the courts will act.”’ Knickerbocker v. City of Stockton, 199 Cal. App. 3d 235, 240 (1988) (citation omitted). Under this rule, a court has no jurisdiction as a matter of law to hear an independent action until administrative remedies have been exhausted. Id. at 241-42.
The requirement of exhaustion of judicial remedies, on the other hand, necessitates that a party pursue the exclusive judicial remedy for reviewing an adverse administrative decision to prevent the administrative decision from achieving finality. Johnson v. City of Loma Linda, 24 Cal. 4th 61, 70 (2000). This rule is preclusive in effect, such that an aggrieved party who has the opportunity to seek direct judicial review of an administrative decision cannot avoid the preclusive effect by simply forgoing the right to have the decision reviewed. Briggs v. City of Rolling Hills Estates, 40 Cal. App. 4th 637, 647-48 (1995); Knickerbocker, 199 Cal. App. 3d at 241-43.
Here, Plaintiff failed to exhaust his administrative remedies by failing to request a hearing before an ALJ as required by the AIR21 statute upon receipt of the Secretary of Labor's preliminary order. 49 U.S.C. §42121(b)(2)(A). This failure resulted in a final order that is not subject to judicial review. Id. Likewise, under the doctrine of judicial exhaustion, because Plaintiff did not seek direct judicial review by pursuing his AIR21 Complaint to this Court, the decision of the Secretary of Labor is final and precludes Plaintiff from circumventing the effect of the AIR21 statute by bringing his Civil Complaint. Id. §42121(b)(4)(B).
CONCLUSION
For the foregoing reasons, the District Court's Order granting summary judgment should be affirmed.
